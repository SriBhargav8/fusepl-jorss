# Term Sheet vs SHA vs SSA: The 3 Documents That Define Your Startup

Every Indian startup funding round produces three documents. Most founders read one carefully, skim one, and trust their lawyer on the third.

That's how founders end up surprised at an exit, surprised at a board meeting, and surprised when an investor blocks a decision they thought was theirs to make.

Here's the honest truth: your lawyer protects you from legal risk. But only you can protect yourself from business risk buried inside legal language. The clauses that matter most — liquidation preferences, anti-dilution, drag-along, board composition — are business decisions dressed in legal clothes.

This guide explains what each document does, how they interact, and the specific provisions that have materially damaged Indian founders.

---

## The Three Documents: An Overview

| Document | What It Is | When It's Signed | Legally Binding? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Term Sheet** | Summary of deal terms | At the start of the process | Usually not (except exclusivity + confidentiality) |
| **SHA** (Shareholders' Agreement) | Governs ongoing relationship between shareholders | At closing | Yes — perpetual |
| **SSA** (Share Subscription Agreement) | Governs the actual share issuance transaction | At closing | Yes — single event |

They are signed in this order: Term Sheet → Due Diligence → SHA + SSA (simultaneously at closing).

The Term Sheet sets the terms. The SHA and SSA implement them. But the SHA and SSA also add provisions that weren't in the Term Sheet — which is where many founders get caught.

## Document 1: The Term Sheet

### What It Is

A term sheet is a non-binding summary of the proposed investment terms. It covers the headline economics and key governance points. It is intentionally shorter and less precise than the final documents.

**Typical length:** 5–15 pages
**Typical timeline:** Signed 2–8 weeks before closing

### What It Contains

**Economic terms:**
- Pre-money valuation
- Investment amount
- Post-money valuation
- Price per share
- Type of security (equity, CCD, CCPS)
- Liquidation preference
- Anti-dilution provision type

**Governance terms:**
- Board composition
- Reserved matters (actions requiring investor consent)
- Drag-along / tag-along rights
- Right of first refusal (ROFR)
- Pro-rata rights

**Process terms:**
- Exclusivity period (usually 30–60 days — this IS binding)
- Confidentiality (usually binding)
- Conditions to closing

### The Non-Binding Trap

The term sheet says "non-binding" but this creates a false sense of security. In practice:

1. **Social commitment:** Once you've signed a term sheet, walking away creates reputational damage unless there's a genuine reason.
2. **Exclusivity is binding:** You cannot negotiate with other investors during the exclusivity window (typically 30–60 days). Missing a better deal while locked up is a real cost.
3. **The SHA/SSA will follow the term sheet closely** — so anything you missed in the term sheet will likely appear in binding form later.

**Rule:** Negotiate the term sheet as if it were binding. Because in practice, it is.

### The 5 Term Sheet Clauses That Matter Most

**1. Liquidation Preference**

The term sheet will say something like: "1x non-participating preferred" or "1x participating preferred" or "2x non-participating."

This determines who gets paid first at exit and how much. We'll cover this in detail in the liquidation preference section below — but in the term sheet, it appears as a brief sentence. Don't let its brevity fool you.

**2. Anti-Dilution**

The term sheet will specify: "broad-based weighted average anti-dilution" or "full ratchet" or "narrow-based weighted average."

This clause protects investors in down rounds. Full ratchet is extremely founder-unfriendly. Always push for broad-based weighted average.

**3. Board Composition**

Typical early-stage structures in India:
- **2-1-1:** 2 founders, 1 investor, 1 independent (founder-controlled)
- **2-2-1:** 2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent (balanced — can deadlock)
- **2-1:** Pre-seed, 2 founders, 1 observer right for investor

Watch what happens at your **next round** — the term sheet should specify. If your Series A investor also gets a board seat, and the structure becomes 2-2-1, you've lost control.

**4. Reserved Matters**

Also called "protective provisions" — these are company decisions that require **investor consent** even if investors don't control the board.

Standard reserved matters (reasonable):
- Issuing new shares / new equity
- Amending the articles
- Major acquisitions or disposals (above a threshold)
- Taking on significant debt
- Changes to business scope

Aggressive reserved matters (watch out):
- Hiring or firing the CEO (this is your job!)
- Annual budget approval
- Any single purchase above ₹10 lakhs (too low)
- Entry into any new market
- Any partnership agreement above a trivial threshold

**If reserved matters require investor consent for operating decisions, you don't run your company anymore.**

**5. ESOP Pool**

Where the term sheet says "ESOP pool of 10% to be created pre-money or post-money" — this matters enormously.

- **Pre-money ESOP pool:** The ESOP shares come from the founders' equity. Founders bear the dilution.
- **Post-money ESOP pool:** The ESOP shares dilute everyone proportionally.

Investors always want pre-money ESOP pool. The difference on a ₹5Cr raise at ₹20Cr pre-money with a 10% ESOP pool:
- Pre-money ESOP: Founders effectively start at 90% of their stated stake before dilution
- Post-money ESOP: Investors and founders share the ESOP dilution proportionally

**Always push for post-money ESOP pool.** If you can't get it, at minimum ensure the ESOP pool size is reasonable (not inflated to what you'd need for 5 years of hiring).

## Document 2: The SHA (Shareholders' Agreement)

### What It Is

The SHA is the master governance document. It governs the ongoing relationship between all shareholders — founders, investors, ESOP pool trustees, and any future shareholders.

**Typical length:** 40–80 pages
**Governs:** Everything that happens after the investment closes

### The SHA Clause Map

#### Section 1: Share Capital and Ownership

Records the post-closing cap table. Confirms who owns what percentage and what type of shares (equity, CCPS, CCD).

**Key point:** Make sure the SHA cap table matches your actual cap table exactly. Discrepancies here cause problems at every subsequent round.

#### Section 2: Transfer Restrictions

**Right of First Refusal (ROFR):**
Before a shareholder can sell shares to a third party, existing shareholders have the right to buy those shares at the offered price. Standard and reasonable.

**Right of First Offer (ROFO):**
Before marketing shares for sale, the selling shareholder must first offer them to existing shareholders. More restrictive than ROFR.

**Lock-in periods:**
Founders are typically locked in for 2–3 years from the date of investment. You cannot sell your shares during this period. If you leave the company during lock-in, your unvested shares are either bought back or forfeited.

**What to watch:** Some SHAs have lock-in periods that prevent founders from selling any shares (including vested shares) for 3–5 years. This is unreasonable for a seed-stage investment. Push back.

#### Section 3: Drag-Along Rights

The drag-along clause allows a majority of shareholders (often including the lead investor) to force all other shareholders to sell their shares in an acquisition.

**Why it exists:** If a buyer wants 100% of the company but a minor shareholder blocks the deal, the drag-along forces the sale.

**The founder danger:** If an investor holds more than the drag-along threshold (often 50–51% of preference shares), they can force a sale even if founders don't want to exit. This is rare but it happens — especially when a portfolio company isn't growing and the fund needs to return capital.

**What to negotiate:**
- Drag-along threshold should require consent of founders (or a majority of founders + investors combined)
- The drag-along price should be at least the original investment amount or a minimum return
- Founders should have co-drag rights (if investor can drag, founders can force investor to drag too)

#### Section 4: Tag-Along Rights

The tag-along (or co-sale right) says: if a major shareholder sells their shares, minority shareholders have the right to sell their proportional shares in the same transaction at the same price.

**Why it exists:** Protects minority shareholders from being left behind in a secondary transaction.

**Who benefits:** Usually protects investors when founders do secondary sales. But also protects founders if investors do secondaries to other investors.

**What to watch:** Some tag-along clauses kick in even for small secondary transactions (e.g., a founder selling 0.5% to a strategic advisor). Set a minimum threshold below which tag-along doesn't apply.

#### Section 5: Anti-Dilution

The anti-dilution clause protects investors if you raise a "down round" — i.e., a round at a lower valuation than when they invested.

**Full Ratchet (very investor-friendly):**
If you raise at any price lower than the investor's price, their shares are repriced to the new price. On a severe down round, this can give investors a massive ownership increase at the expense of founders.

**Weighted Average (standard):**
The anti-dilution adjustment is weighted by the size of the new issue. The impact on founders is proportional and more moderate.

*Broad-based weighted average* includes all issued shares (including options, warrants, convertibles) in the calculation — resulting in the smallest adjustment to investors and the most founder-friendly outcome.

*Narrow-based weighted average* includes fewer shares in the denominator — resulting in a larger adjustment, more investor-friendly.

**Full Ratchet Example:**
- Series A investor bought shares at ₹100 per share for a 10% stake
- Company raises Series B at ₹50 per share (down round)
- Full ratchet: investor's 10% is now repriced to ₹50/share. To maintain the same ₹ investment at the new price, they now own 20%. Founders just lost 10% without any new money.

**Rule:** Never accept full ratchet. Broad-based weighted average is the reasonable standard.

#### Section 6: Information Rights

Investors have the right to receive financial information about the company. Standard provisions:

- Monthly MIS (management information system report)
- Quarterly financial statements
- Annual audited accounts
- Right to inspect books and records (usually with notice)

**What to watch:** Some SHAs include the right for investors to hire an independent auditor at the company's expense. This is unusual and should be resisted unless the investor has genuine governance concerns.

#### Section 7: Reserved Matters (Protective Provisions)

As discussed in the term sheet section — these are decisions that require investor consent. The SHA will list them in detail.

**The key negotiation:** Make sure reserved matters are limited to matters that genuinely affect investor economics and rights. Day-to-day operating decisions (hires below a certain seniority, vendor contracts below a threshold, normal course of business) should not require investor consent.

**Typical reasonable reserved matters list (10–12 items):**
1. Issuance of new shares or options
2. Amendments to charter/articles
3. Acquisition of another company (above threshold)
4. Disposal of substantial assets
5. Incurring debt above a threshold
6. Related party transactions above a threshold
7. Winding up or dissolution
8. Change of business scope
9. Changes to ESOP plan
10. Capital expenditure above a threshold
11. Dividends or distributions
12. Settlement of litigation above a threshold

**Red flag items that should NOT be reserved matters:**
- Hiring or firing employees below senior management
- Annual budget (unless investor approval is limited to significant deviations)
- Marketing decisions
- Normal course vendor agreements

#### Section 8: Exit Provisions

The SHA should include provisions that give all parties a path to liquidity:

**Deemed Liquidation Events:**
Usually defined as: acquisition, merger, asset sale, IPO. The liquidation preference applies to all of these, not just company wind-down.

**IPO Rights:**
If the company achieves a certain size or timeframe, investors can demand the company pursue an IPO. This is called a "drag to IPO" and is rare at seed stage but appears in some growth-stage SHAs.

**Redemption Rights:**
In some cases, investors have the right to demand that the company buy back their shares after a certain period (typically 5–7 years). This is designed as a backstop if no exit has occurred — and it creates a serious problem if the company doesn't have the cash to comply.

**What to do:** If the SHA includes redemption rights, ensure:
- The timeline is long enough (7+ years)
- The redemption is subject to solvency (company must be able to pay)
- The price is at the original investment amount, not compounded

## Document 3: The SSA (Share Subscription Agreement)

### What It Is

The SSA governs the specific transaction of the investor subscribing for new shares. It's a one-time document that records the mechanics of the investment.

**Typical length:** 20–40 pages
**Governs:** The share issuance transaction itself

### What the SSA Contains

#### Representations and Warranties

The SSA includes representations and warranties by **both parties**:

**Company representations (the riskier side):**
You're warrantying that:
- The company is properly incorporated and in good standing
- All IP is owned by the company (not the founders personally)
- There are no pending or threatened legal actions
- Financial statements are accurate and complete
- There are no undisclosed liabilities
- All regulatory approvals are in place
- There are no third-party rights over company assets

**Why this matters:** If any representation turns out to be false, the investor has a claim against the company (and sometimes the founders personally) for damages. This is called a **warranty claim**.

**Common warranty breaches that cause problems:**
- IP assigned to the company after investment that should have been done before
- Employee agreements not in place
- Prior IP created by founders on employer's time (rare but happens with ex-FAANG founders)
- Undisclosed side agreements with early employees or advisors
- Tax returns not filed, statutory compliances missed

**Rule:** Before signing the SSA, do a proper legal health check. Fix everything you can. Disclose everything you can't fix. Undisclosed issues that surface later are much worse than disclosed issues.

#### Conditions Precedent

The investment won't close until these conditions are met:

- Board and shareholder resolutions approving the investment
- FEMA compliance (for foreign investors): RBI reporting, ARF filing
- No material adverse change in the company's business
- All representations being true at closing
- Updated cap table reflecting the new investment

**FEMA compliance for foreign investment:**
If your investor is a non-resident, the SSA must explicitly address:
1. The investment must be reported to RBI within 30 days via the **Advance Remittance Form (ARF)**
2. Shares must be allotted within 60 days of receiving funds
3. The valuation must comply with FEMA NDI Rules (separate from Rule 11UA — requires a SEBI-registered Merchant Banker or Chartered Accountant with specific qualifications)
4. Within 30 days of allotment, file **Form FC-GPR** with RBI through authorized dealer bank

Missing FEMA timelines creates compounding compliance issues. This is one area where you genuinely need a CA who handles foreign investment regularly.

#### Indemnity Provisions

The SSA will include indemnities — protections against financial loss caused by specific events.

Standard indemnities:
- Company indemnifies investors against losses from false warranties
- Founders may be asked to give personal indemnities for fundamental warranties (incorporation, ownership, no litigation)

**Personal founder indemnity** is a major red flag. This means if the company can't pay, the investor can come after you personally. Resist this for everything except genuinely fundamental matters (you are who you say you are, you own what you say you own).

## How the Three Documents Interact: The Cascade Effect

Here's where most founders get confused. The documents don't just add to each other — they can modify each other in complex ways.

**The typical cascade:**

1. **Term sheet** sets: "1x non-participating liquidation preference, board 2-1-1, ROFR, drag-along at 75%"

2. **SHA** implements: The actual liquidation preference calculation, board meeting procedures, reserved matters list, full ROFR mechanics with 15-day response window, drag-along threshold and pricing mechanism

3. **SSA** adds: Representations, warranties, conditions, FEMA compliance mechanics, specific indemnities

**Where the gap between term sheet and SHA/SSA creates surprises:**

- Term sheet: "Reserved matters requiring investor consent"
  SHA: Lists 18 reserved matters including "hiring any employee with CTC above ₹25 lakhs" — much broader than the founder expected

- Term sheet: "Anti-dilution: weighted average"
  SHA: "Narrow-based weighted average" — more investor-friendly than the broad-based standard

- Term sheet: "Drag-along at majority"
  SHA: "Majority of preference shares" — investor may already hold a majority of preference shares, so they can drag alone

**Rule:** Read the SHA and SSA carefully against the term sheet. For every material term sheet provision, find the corresponding SHA/SSA clause and verify it means what you think it means.

## The 10 SHA Clauses Founders Miss (And Regret)

| Clause | What Founders Miss | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Deemed liquidation** | Includes acquisition as a liquidation event — preference applies at M&A exit | Ensure definition doesn't include normal course asset sales |
| **Reserved matter threshold** | ₹10L capex threshold means every laptop purchase needs investor sign-off | Push threshold to ₹50L or higher |
| **Anti-dilution formula** | "Weighted average" can be narrow-based (more aggressive) | Always specify "broad-based weighted average" |
| **Lock-in on all shares** | Prevents even small secondary sales for 3+ years | Carve out partial liquidity rights after certain milestones |
| **ESOP reserved matter** | Any ESOP grant requires investor consent | Carve out grants within approved ESOP plan and pool |
| **Drag-along threshold** | "Majority of preference shares" — investor may already qualify | Require founder co-consent for drag-along |
| **Information rights timing** | "Monthly MIS within 5 days of month end" — unrealistic for early team | Negotiate 15 days |
| **Redemption rights** | After 5 years, investor can demand buyback — you may not have cash | Push to 7+ years, add solvency condition |
| **Personal warranty indemnity** | Founders personally liable for company warranty breaches | Limit to cap (usually 1x investment amount) |
| **Right of first offer vs ROFR** | ROFO is more restrictive than ROFR | Always push for ROFR, not ROFO |

## The Negotiation Reality: What's Actually Movable

Not every SHA clause is negotiable. Here's a realistic assessment:

**Almost always negotiable (investors expect pushback):**
- Anti-dilution: narrow → broad-based weighted average
- ESOP pool: pre-money → post-money
- Reserved matters: scope and thresholds
- Redemption: timeline and conditions
- Drag-along: threshold and pricing floor

**Negotiable if you have leverage:**
- Board composition (if you have multiple term sheets)
- Founder lock-in period and vesting cliff
- Personal indemnity scope
- Information rights frequency

**Rarely negotiable at seed stage:**
- Liquidation preference (1x non-participating is standard; investors won't go lower)
- ROFR (standard market practice)
- Tag-along (standard market practice)
- Reserved matters (the list may be shortened, but the concept stays)

## Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

Some terms signal a problematic investor relationship that will play out badly regardless of valuation:

1. **Participating preferred with 2x+ liquidation preference** — investors double-dip at exit
2. **Full ratchet anti-dilution** — a single down round hands the company to investors
3. **Reserved matters including CEO hire/fire** — you can be fired from your own company
4. **Investor board majority at seed stage** — you lose control before you've proven anything
5. **Personal indemnity with no cap** — unlimited personal liability for company issues
6. **Drag-along threshold below 50%** — investor can force exit without your agreement
7. **Redemption rights within 3 years** — premature liquidity demand before you've had time to build

If you see any of these, either negotiate them out or seriously consider whether this is the right investor.

## A Note on Indian Law vs US Law

Many founders have read US startup legal guides (Y Combinator, a16z, etc.) and assume the same norms apply in India. They don't — there are important differences:

| Issue | US | India |
|---|---|---|
| **SAFE notes** | Standard | Not legally recognised — use CCDs or CCPSs |
| **Preferred stock** | Common | Indian law uses CCPSs (Compulsorily Convertible Preference Shares) |
| **1-day close** | Possible with SAFEs | Minimum 3–4 weeks due to statutory requirements (board meetings, ROC filings) |
| **Oral board votes** | Common | Formal board meeting + minutes required by Companies Act |
| **Cap table via spreadsheet** | Common | Statutory register of members is the legal record |
| **ESOP plans** | Board-approved | Must be approved by special resolution at AGM for PLCs; board-approved for private companies if authorised |

Your US investor's standard term sheet will likely need significant modification for India. Make sure your lawyer is experienced in both cross-border transactions and Indian corporate law — not just one.

## What to Do Before Signing

**Before signing the term sheet:**
1. Read every clause — especially reserved matters and liquidation preference
2. Mark up anything you want changed
3. Get a startup-specialist lawyer to review (not your family CA)
4. Compare to recent comparable deals (ask your angel network or other founders)

**Before signing the SHA/SSA:**
1. Do a side-by-side comparison of SHA vs term sheet — find every deviation
2. Ensure your Rule 11UA / FEMA valuation compliance is in order
3. Do a legal health check (IP assignments, employment contracts, statutory compliances)
4. Get board and shareholder resolutions done properly
5. Confirm the FEMA reporting timeline is in your post-closing checklist

**The legal cost:** A good startup transaction lawyer charges ₹1–3 lakh for a seed round review. That is the best ₹1–3 lakh you will spend. The alternative is discovering a bad clause 3 years later when it fires.
