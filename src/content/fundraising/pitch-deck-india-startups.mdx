## The Deck That Gets Read vs The Deck That Gets Deleted

Indian VCs receive between 200 and 1,000 pitch decks per month depending on the fund size. Partners at mid-size funds personally review 50–100 per month. The average time spent on the first pass of a deck is 3–4 minutes.

In those 3–4 minutes, an investor is not reading every word. They are pattern-matching against frameworks they've developed across hundreds of investments. They're looking for specific signals on specific slides. When those signals are absent or wrong, the deck goes into the "follow up later" pile — which, for most funds, means it never gets followed up.

This guide gives you the exact framework that Indian VCs use, slide by slide. Not the generic startup advice you'll find on American blogs. The India-specific version — what matters to Sequoia India, Elevation Capital, Accel India, Blume Ventures, Kalaari, and the dozens of sector-specific funds that have emerged in the last five years.

## How Indian VC Evaluation Differs from US VC Evaluation

Before we go slide by slide, understand the fundamental difference in how Indian investors evaluate pitches.

**US investors (especially at seed) bet heavily on team.** "We invest in missionaries, not mercenaries." The Airbnb origin story, the founder's obsession with the problem, the "why this person" narrative — these dominate early-stage US investing.

**Indian investors at seed bet on traction.** There is a simple reason for this: the Indian startup ecosystem has produced more failures from teams that looked strong on paper than from teams that had weak paper credentials. The market is also harder to predict. What works in Bangalore may not work in Tier 2 cities. What works for English-speaking customers doesn't necessarily work at scale in regional languages. Given all this uncertainty, traction — actual customers paying actual money — is the most trusted signal.

This changes everything about which slides carry the most weight.

| Slide | US Seed Weight | India Seed Weight | India Series A Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Team | Very High | Medium | High |
| Problem | High | Medium | Low (assumed) |
| Market Size | High | Medium (skeptical) | High (must be proven) |
| Solution | High | Medium | Medium |
| Traction | Medium | Very High | Critical |
| Business Model | Medium | High | High |
| Unit Economics | Low | High | Critical |
| Competitive Landscape | Medium | Low | Medium |
| Financials/Projections | Low | Medium | High |
| The Ask | Medium | High | High |

The second thing to understand: **Indian investors have deep market skepticism.** Every pitch deck says "India has 1.4 billion people." Indian VCs are tired of hearing it. They have seen a hundred companies target "even 1% of India's population" only to find that 1% of India's population is much harder and more expensive to acquire than the slide implied.

What Indian investors actually want to see: bottom-up market sizing from real data (how many businesses in India buy similar SaaS tools, at what average price, growing at what rate — not "India's IT services market is $200 billion").

## The 12 Slides That Indian VCs Actually Read

Here is the structure. Note: some investors prefer 10 slides, some 15. The content matters more than the count, but if you're over 18 slides for a seed deck, you're losing people.

### Slide 1: The Hook / Cover

This is the slide the investor sees first. It should do one thing: make them want to open the next slide.

**What works:** A single, specific, surprising statement about the problem you're solving.

**What doesn't work:** Your company logo and tagline. "Revolutionizing [industry] through [technology]" is meaningless.

**Examples of good hooks for Indian startups:**

- "₹40,000 crore of B2B invoices go unpaid every year in India. We collect 87% of them in under 30 days." (Collections SaaS)
- "3.2 crore Indian truck drivers earn ₹8,000/month but can't access any financial products. We've given 40,000 of them their first bank account." (Fintech)
- "India manufactures 4 crore mattresses per year. 97% are sold through unorganized retailers with zero data. We're changing that." (B2B supply chain)

The hook does not need to be flashy. It needs to be specific and credible.

**Template:**
```
[Specific number] + [specific problem] + [specific audience]
OR
[Counterintuitive fact about the market] that leads to your solution
```

---

### Slide 2: The Problem

This slide exists to create urgency and establish that you understand the problem better than anyone else in the room.

**The mistake most Indian founders make:** Describing the problem from a distance. "SMEs in India face working capital challenges" is not a problem — it's a category. The slide should show that you have lived the problem, studied it deeply, or have primary research that makes it real.

**What Indian VCs look for:**
- Specificity: which customer segment, in which geography, experiencing what exact pain
- Quantification: how much does this problem cost (time, money, lost revenue)
- Frequency: how often does this problem occur
- Why it hasn't been solved: what structural reason explains the gap

**The "Why Now" element:** Indian VCs increasingly ask "why is this the right time for this solution?" The answer is usually one of: a new regulation (UPI enabling payments at scale), a new technology (LLMs making X possible), a market shift (post-COVID behavior change), or a new data availability (IndiaStack, OCEN, AA Framework).

If you don't have a "why now" answer, you need to find one or question whether the timing is right.

**Template for the Problem slide:**

```
Header: [Customer] loses [₹X / Y hours / Z% revenue] every [time period] because of [specific problem]

Body:
- [Specific pain point 1] — current workaround: [what they do today]
- [Specific pain point 2] — current workaround: [what they do today]
- [Cost of inaction]: ₹X per year / per transaction / per customer

Visual: Photo or quote from a real customer experiencing the problem
(This is powerful — a WhatsApp screenshot from a customer complaint is more compelling than a stock photo)
```

### Slide 3: The Solution (Product Demo or Screenshots)

Do not describe your product in abstract terms. Show it.

**What Indian investors want:** Proof that you have built something, not just planned something. Even a prototype or early version is better than a concept visualization.

**The mistake:** A slide that says "Our AI-powered platform leverages machine learning to provide real-time insights..." This says nothing. Show the actual interface. Show the flow. Show what the user sees and does.

**For B2B products:** Walk through one specific customer workflow. "Here's how a CA firm uses our platform to file 100 GST returns in 2 hours instead of 20 hours."

**For B2C products:** Show the core loop. Acquisition → activation → key action → value delivery. Make it real.

**One rule:** If your product requires 4 slides to explain, it is either too complex or too early-stage. One strong product slide (with a link to a demo video or live demo offer) is better than four weak explanation slides.

### Slide 4: Market Size — The Slide That Kills Most Indian Decks

This is where the most Indian pitch decks die. Let's be very specific about why.

**The wrong approach (which most founders use):**

> "India's [industry] market is $X billion. Growing at Y% CAGR. Even capturing 1% represents ₹Z crore in revenue."

Indian VCs have seen this hundreds of times. The problems with this approach:

1. The $X billion figure is usually from an analyst report that is either wrong, outdated, or defines the market differently than your actual business
2. "Capturing 1%" implies no differentiation or moat — why would you get 1%?
3. It doesn't tell the investor how many customers there are, who they are, what they pay, and how you reach them

**The right approach: bottom-up market sizing**

Build your TAM from the customer up.

```
Example: B2B SaaS for CA firms in India

Step 1: Define the addressable customer
- Number of active CA firms in India: ~3.5 lakh (ICAI data, 2024)
- Firms with >5 clients (who need software): ~1.5 lakh (our estimate, validated by ICAI survey)

Step 2: Estimate willingness to pay
- Current tools used: Excel + manual processes
- Comparable products: Tally (₹18,000/year), Winman (₹15,000/year)
- Our target price: ₹24,000/year (after piloting at ₹20,000–30,000 range)

Step 3: Calculate SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
- SAM = 1.5 lakh firms × ₹24,000/year = ₹360 Cr/year

Step 4: Calculate SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market for 3 years)
- Target: 5% market share in 3 years = 7,500 firms
- Revenue at 3 years: 7,500 × ₹24,000 = ₹18 Cr ARR

Step 5: Show the expansion path
- Add payroll module: additional ₹12,000/year per firm
- Add GST compliance module: additional ₹8,000/year
- 3-year expanded ARPU: ₹44,000/year
- Expanded 3-year revenue: 7,500 × ₹44,000 = ₹33 Cr ARR
```

This approach is far more credible. An investor reading it can challenge specific assumptions rather than dismissing the whole thing as "India = big market."

**How large does the market need to be?**

Indian VCs at the seed stage want to see a path to ₹100 crore ARR in 7–10 years. At Series A, they want to see a path to ₹500 crore+ ARR. Work backwards from those targets.

**India-specific market sizing sources to use:**
- MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs) data for number of registered companies
- MSME Ministry data for SME counts by sector
- IRDAI data for insurance metrics
- RBI data for lending/banking metrics
- DGFT data for export/import markets
- TRAI data for telecom
- IndiaStack / NPCI data for payment volumes
- Industry associations (NASSCOM, IAMAI, CII) for sector-specific data

Always cite your source. "3.5 lakh CA firms (ICAI, 2024)" is 10x more credible than "3.5 lakh CA firms."

### Slide 5: Business Model

This slide should answer three questions in 60 seconds: How do you charge? Who pays? How does revenue grow with usage?

**Common Indian startup business models and how to present them:**

| Model | Key Metrics to Show | Red Flag to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| SaaS (B2B) | MRR, ACV, churn rate, expansion revenue | Contracts without committed revenue |
| Transaction fee (Fintech) | Take rate, GMV, net revenue, attach rate | Gross GMV without net revenue clarity |
| Marketplace | GMV, take rate, liquidity metrics (supply/demand balance) | Showing supply without demand or vice versa |
| D2C / E-commerce | Gross margin, CAC, LTV, repeat purchase rate | Blended margins that include one-time promo sales |
| B2B2C | Both B and C metrics: enterprise contract value + consumer engagement | Only showing B metrics without C proof |
| Subscription (consumer) | DAU/MAU, paying conversion rate, churn, LTV | Monthly actives that include free tier only |

**The India-specific nuance:** Indian consumers are extremely price-sensitive. If your business model depends on Indian consumers paying a recurring subscription, you need to show evidence that your specific customer segment actually pays, not just evidence that the market is large.

### Slide 6: Traction — The Most Important Slide in India

This is the slide Indian investors stare at longest. If this slide is weak, the meeting often ends here (politely, but it ends).

**What traction means at different stages:**

| Stage | Minimum Traction to Be Fundable | Strong Traction |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | Problem validation (50+ customer interviews), waitlist or LOIs | 10–20 paying pilot customers |
| Seed | 10–50 paying customers, ₹10–50L ARR | ₹50L+ ARR, strong MoM growth (>15%) |
| Pre-Series A | ₹50L–2 Cr ARR, product-market fit signal | ₹2 Cr+ ARR, NRR >110%, clear unit economics |
| Series A | ₹3–10 Cr ARR, proven GTM playbook | ₹10 Cr+ ARR, defensible growth mechanics |

**What to show on the traction slide:**

1. **Revenue chart (monthly, 12–18 months):** The shape matters. Consistent MoM growth is better than a few big months and flat months.

2. **Key metric trend:** Depends on your model. For SaaS: ARR and MRR. For marketplace: GMV and orders. For consumer: DAU/MAU and retention. Show one metric that proves the core business is working.

3. **Logo slide (B2B only):** For B2B companies, a slide showing 10–20 customer logos with recognizable names is extremely powerful. One Tata or Infosys logo changes the conversation.

4. **Retention/cohort data:** This is the most underused and most valued metric in India. A cohort chart showing that customers who joined 12 months ago are still using the product (and ideally paying more) is more convincing than any revenue projection.

**The traction slide mistakes:**

- Showing total registered users without separating active users
- Showing GMV without showing net revenue (especially egregious in lending or marketplace)
- Showing "pilot" numbers as if they're paying customers
- Not showing growth rate — absolute numbers without trend are meaningless
- Showing traction from a geography or channel that isn't scalable ("We got all this traction from one corporate partnership")

**The honest approach:** If your traction is early or mixed, be upfront about it. "We have 30 paying customers at ₹15,000/month average. Three cohorts. Month 6 retention is 85%. We're at ₹5L MRR and growing 20% month over month over the last 3 months." This is early, but honest and specific. It's better than obscuring weak traction with vague language.

### Slide 7: Unit Economics

For Indian seed-stage companies, this is often combined with the business model slide. At Series A, it needs its own slide with detailed numbers.

**The unit economics slides that Indian VCs want to see:**

**For SaaS:**
```
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC):
- Sales cycle: X weeks
- Sales headcount: Y reps
- Monthly sales cost: ₹Z lakh
- New customers per month: N
- CAC = ₹Z lakh / N = ₹___

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV):
- ACV (Annual Contract Value): ₹___ per year
- Gross margin: ___%
- Average customer life: ___ years (based on observed churn)
- LTV = ACV × gross margin % × average life = ₹___

LTV:CAC ratio: ___ (target >3x)
CAC payback period: ___ months (target <18 months for India SaaS)
```

**For Consumer / D2C:**
```
Per-Order Unit Economics:
- Average order value: ₹___
- COGS (landed cost): ₹___
- Gross margin: ₹___ (___%)
- Delivery/fulfillment: ₹___
- Packaging: ₹___
- Payment gateway: ₹___
- Contribution margin: ₹___ (___%)
- Monthly orders per customer: ___
- Monthly contribution per customer: ₹___
- CAC (blended): ₹___
- Contribution margin payback: ___ months
```

**The number Indian investors fixate on:** CAC payback period. If it takes more than 24 months to recover the cost of acquiring a customer, the business is burning cash faster than it can grow. Many D2C and consumer fintech companies ran into this problem in the 2022–2024 period.

**What strong unit economics look like for India:**

| Model | Strong | Acceptable | Weak |
|---|---|---|---|
| B2B SaaS | LTV:CAC >5x, payback <12m | LTV:CAC 3–5x, payback 12–18m | LTV:CAC <3x, payback >24m |
| D2C | CM2 >30%, payback <6m | CM2 15–30%, payback 6–12m | CM2 <15%, payback >12m |
| Fintech | Net interest margin >5%, CAC payback <12m | NIM 3–5%, payback 12–18m | NIM <3%, payback >18m |
| Marketplace | Take rate >8%, CM contribution positive | Take rate 4–8%, path to positive CM | Take rate <4%, negative CM |

### Slide 8: Competition

This is the slide where Indian founders most commonly embarrass themselves. Two failure modes:

**Failure mode 1: "We have no competition."** No investor believes this. It tells the investor either you haven't done your research, or the market doesn't exist. Both are bad signals.

**Failure mode 2: The 2x2 "Magic Quadrant" Matrix.** You put yourself in the top-right corner and all competitors in the other three quadrants. Every founder does this. It's not credible.

**The right approach:**

Be honest about who the real competitors are. Then clearly explain why you win. The most credible framing:

1. **Name the actual alternatives:** Not just direct competitors. Include "do nothing," "Excel," "hire a person," and legacy incumbents. The question is "what does the customer use today?"

2. **Show a feature comparison table honestly:** Include some features where competitors are better. Investors know you can't be best at everything. If you claim to be, they stop trusting your competitive analysis.

3. **Articulate your actual advantage:** Is it price? Speed? Integration with a specific ecosystem (GST, UPI, ONDC)? Network effects? Geographic focus? The advantage must be real and specific.

**Example competitive slide structure:**

```
Who customers use today:
- Manual process / Excel: 60% of market
- Legacy software (Tally, Busy): 25% of market
- Competitors A and B: 10% of market
- Us: 5% (and growing 40% MoM)

Why we win:
1. Native GST/TDS integration (competitors built pre-GST, retrofitted)
2. WhatsApp-first interface (critical for Tier 2+ India)
3. Price: ₹8,000/year vs ₹18,000/year for comparable tools
4. Implementation time: 2 hours vs 2 weeks for competitors

Where competitors are stronger:
- Tally has richer accounting features (but overkill for our segment)
- Competitor A has more enterprise clients (not our target market)
```

This level of honesty is rare and powerful. Investors who see it trust the founder's judgment on everything else.

### Slide 9: Team

The team slide is weighted differently in India than in the US. Here's how Indian VCs think about it.

**At seed stage, Indian VCs look for:**

1. **Domain expertise / unfair access.** Have you worked in this industry? Do you have relationships that others don't? A founder who spent 8 years at a logistics company building a logistics tech startup is far more credible than an IIT grad who "identified the opportunity."

2. **Execution history.** Have you built something before, even if it wasn't a startup? A founder who built and scaled a team inside a large company, or who launched a product to 100,000 users as a side project, signals execution ability.

3. **Complementarity.** In a 2-3 person founding team, the basic split is: someone who can build (technical), someone who can sell (commercial/GTM), and often someone with deep domain knowledge. All three in one person is rare; 2 out of 3 with a plan to hire the third is acceptable.

4. **Skin in the game.** Are the founders full-time? Have they put in personal capital? A founder who is "still consulting on the side to cover rent" is a yellow flag.

**What doesn't impress Indian seed investors (but founders think it does):**
- IIT/IIM credentials alone (necessary but not sufficient; almost every startup has an IITian)
- Previous work at McKinsey, Goldman Sachs, or a big tech company (great background, but startup is different)
- Impressive-sounding advisory board with no active involvement
- Large team (for seed, 8–10 people is usually too many before product-market fit)

**At Series A, Indian VCs look for:**
- Evidence that the founding team can hire (the team you've built around you says more than your own background)
- A VP Sales or Head of Sales who can own GTM independently
- Clear functional ownership (who owns product, who owns engineering, who owns growth)

**Template for the team slide:**

```
[Founder 1 Name] — CEO/Co-founder
[Photo]
• [Specific past achievement]: "Scaled [Company X] from ₹2Cr to ₹40Cr ARR in 3 years"
• [Domain connection]: "7 years in BFSI sales, former team lead at HDFC"
• [Education — one line, not the focus]

[Founder 2 Name] — CTO/Co-founder
[Photo]
• [Specific past build]: "Built and shipped 3 consumer apps with 100K+ downloads"
• [Technical credential that matters]: "Led engineering at [X] for [specific system]"

[Key hire #1 — if relevant]: "Head of Sales, ex-[strong name], closed ₹12Cr ARR in previous role"
```

Do not list advisors unless they are genuinely involved. Listing Nandan Nilekani as an advisor when he attended one meeting is embarrassing if any VC in the room knows Nandan.

### Slide 10: Financials and Projections

Indian VCs at seed stage do not expect to see audited financials — but they do expect to see a P&L, a burn rate, and a projection model that is internally consistent.

**What they actually look at:**

1. **Current burn rate.** How much capital are you spending per month? This tells them how long the raise buys you and whether the team is operationally disciplined.

2. **Current revenue breakdown.** Where is revenue coming from? Is it concentrated (top 3 customers = 80% of revenue is a yellow flag at seed)? Is it recurring or one-time?

3. **Projections — the hockey stick problem.** Every projection shows exponential growth. Indian VCs have been burned by this enough times that they discount it heavily. The credibility of your projections comes from the assumptions, not the numbers.

**How to present projections credibly:**

```
Year 1 (Current → Month 12):
- Current MRR: ₹15L
- Assumptions: 3 sales reps hired by Q2, 15 new customers/month by Q3
- Projected MRR at Month 12: ₹50L
- Implied growth rate: ~20% MoM for first 6 months, then 10% MoM

Year 2 (Month 13–24):
- Assumptions: Expand to 2nd city (Pune), add 5 sales reps
- New customers per month: 30 by Month 18
- Projected ARR at Month 24: ₹10 Cr
- Key dependencies: (a) Sales rep ramp takes 3 months, (b) Expansion city same economics as Mumbai

Year 3:
- Only show directional. "With current unit economics and GTM, ₹30-50 Cr ARR is the range."
  Don't give specific month-by-month Year 3 numbers — it implies false precision.
```

**The use of funds slide (often combined with financials):** Show where the money goes at a category level. Indian investors like to see that sales/GTM gets more budget than corporate overheads.

### Slide 11: The Ask

This is the slide most founders handle worst. Here is exactly how to structure it.

**The ask slide must answer:**
1. How much are you raising?
2. What instrument (equity, CCPS, CCD/convertible note)?
3. What is the pre/post-money valuation (or cap for convertible)?
4. What will the money be used for (use of funds)?
5. What milestones will this money get you to?

**The Indian-specific context:** Indian VC deals have historically been structured as CCPS (Compulsorily Convertible Preference Shares) or CCDs (Compulsorily Convertible Debentures). SAFEs as used in the US don't work under Indian law — though iSAFE (India-specific variant) is gaining some adoption. If you're talking to Indian funds for an India-incorporated entity, be prepared to discuss CCPS structure.

**Template for the ask slide:**

```
The Ask

Raising: ₹8 Crore (Series Seed)
Instrument: CCPS
Pre-money valuation: ₹32 Crore
Post-money valuation: ₹40 Crore
Current round closed: ₹2 Cr from [Angel/Family Office]
Remaining: ₹6 Cr

Use of Funds (18-month runway):
| Category | Amount | % |
|---|---|---|
| Sales & Marketing (3 reps, campaigns) | ₹3.2 Cr | 40% |
| Product & Engineering (2 senior hires) | ₹2.0 Cr | 25% |
| Operations & Customer Success | ₹1.2 Cr | 15% |
| G&A (legal, compliance, finance) | ₹0.8 Cr | 10% |
| Buffer | ₹0.8 Cr | 10% |

This takes us to:
- ₹3 Cr ARR (up from ₹60L today)
- 50+ enterprise customers (up from 12 today)
- Series A ready: NRR >110%, CAC payback <15 months
```

**The valuation conversation:** Do not hide the valuation on the ask slide. Indian VCs hate being surprised by valuation asks after they've invested time in the conversation. Showing the valuation clearly signals confidence and saves everyone time.

**What investors mean when they say "the valuation is too high":** This almost always means "I don't believe the traction justifies this valuation." The real fix is better traction, not a lower valuation — though sometimes you genuinely are overvaluing and need to adjust.

### Slide 12: The Closing Slide / Why Us / Why Now

The final slide serves two purposes: summarize why this is a compelling investment, and give the investor something to leave with.

**The most effective structure:**

```
Why [Company Name] Wins

The timing: [UPI/ONDC/GST/regulation X] made this possible. It wasn't 3 years ago.

The unfair advantage: [Your specific moat — distribution, IP, relationships, data network effect]

The proof: [One-sentence traction summary: "₹2Cr ARR, 40 enterprise customers, 95% retention"]

We're looking for a partner who: [One specific thing — not money, but value-add]
- Deep BFSI network for enterprise sales
- OR: Experience scaling SaaS to ₹50Cr ARR in India
- OR: Expertise in navigating RBI licensing

Contact: [Founder email, LinkedIn, deck link]
```

The "looking for a partner who" line is powerful — it shows you've thought about who the right investor is, and it makes the investor think "that's me."

## The Mistakes Indian Founders Make in the Full Pitch Meeting

Getting a meeting means the deck worked. But the meeting is where most deals are lost. Here are the India-specific pitfalls:

### Mistake 1: Presenting to a Junior Team Member as if They're the Decision Maker

Indian VC firms often send an Associate or Analyst to the first meeting. Many founders make the error of either (a) not presenting seriously to them or (b) treating them as the decision maker and not asking for the Partner meeting.

The right approach: present with full seriousness, ask at the end "what would the path to a partner meeting look like from here?"

### Mistake 2: Inability to Defend Numbers Under Pressure

Indian VCs ask hard questions about numbers. "Your CAC payback is 14 months — what's your gross margin at month 14 vs month 1?" "Your NRR is 110% — what's the upsell mechanism, and is that repeatable?" If you built the financial model yourself, you should know every cell. If someone else built it, you're exposed.

Build the model yourself. Know every assumption.

### Mistake 3: Overselling the Market, Underselling the Customer

Indian founders often spend 15 minutes explaining why India is a large market and 2 minutes talking about specific customers. Invert this. Investors know India is large. They don't know your specific customers. Tell them about 3–5 specific customers: who they are, what problem they had, how they found you, what they pay, and what they say about you. Nothing is more convincing.

### Mistake 4: Positioning as B2B2C Without Understanding Which Side Has the Problem

B2B2C is a common structure in India (sell to businesses who serve consumers). The trap: the business side is easy to get but the consumer value is unclear, or the consumer value is clear but businesses won't pay to deliver it. Know which side of the transaction has the real willingness to pay and the real problem.

### Mistake 5: Not Having India-Specific Answers for India-Specific Questions

Questions Indian VCs routinely ask that US-focused founders are unprepared for:
- "How does this work in Hindi and regional languages?"
- "What's your Tier 2/3 city strategy?"
- "How do you handle payments — UPI, credit, Bharat QR?"
- "What are the FEMA implications if you raise from foreign investors?"
- "Is the company registered in India or a Delaware flip? What's the plan?"
- "How does GST apply to your revenue model?"

If any of these are relevant and you don't have an answer, it signals you haven't thought deeply about the India-specific execution.

## The Pre-Seed / Angel Deck vs The Seed Deck vs The Series A Deck

One deck does not fit all. The content shifts significantly by stage.

| Element | Pre-seed / Angel | Seed | Series A |
|---|---|---|---|
| Length | 8–10 slides | 12–15 slides | 15–20 slides |
| Financial focus | Minimal — burn rate, runway | Unit economics, MRR trend | Full P&L, cohort analysis, retention |
| Team weight | High (little else to show) | Medium-High | High (who you've hired, not just founders) |
| Market size | Directional is fine | Bottom-up sizing expected | Rigorous bottom-up + TAM expansion path |
| Traction required | Customer interviews, LOIs | 10+ paying customers | ₹3Cr+ ARR, proven GTM |
| Investor materials | Deck only | Deck + one-pager | Deck + full financial model + data room |
| Legal structure | May not be incorporated yet | Incorporated, DPIIT if eligible | Full legal diligence materials |

## Sending the Deck: Process and Timing

**The cold outreach problem:** Most decks sent cold never get read. Indian VCs receive too many cold emails to read them all. Your deck needs a warm introduction. The ranking of intro quality:

1. Portfolio company founder introduction (highest quality — VC trusts them)
2. Co-investor or fellow VC introduction
3. LinkedIn connection who knows the VC personally
4. Direct LinkedIn message to Partner (works better than email if personalized)
5. Cold email to general contact address (lowest — often filtered)

**When to send the deck:** Tuesday–Thursday mornings. Never on Mondays (post-weekend backlog) or Fridays (mentally weekend). Never the day of a major industry event where the investor is speaking.

**What to send in the first email:**

```
Subject: [Company Name] — [One-line description] — Introduction from [name]

[Name],

[One sentence from introducer context if warm intro]

[Company name] helps [specific customer] do [specific thing] — we're at [traction metric].

[One specific reason you're reaching out to this fund: "Your investment in [portfolio company] shows
you understand the [sector] ecosystem."]

Deck attached. Happy to share more.

[Name]
[Title]
[Mobile — Indian investors call, they don't email back]
```

The deck attachment is fine for the first send. Do not put the deck behind a link that requires login. Do not use Docsend for the first outreach (it signals you're tracking them, which can feel invasive). Use a direct PDF or an unlocked Docsend link.

## Quick-Reference: The 30-Second Deck Test

Before you finalize your deck, run this test: have someone who doesn't know your company look at the deck for 30 seconds, then answer:

1. What does the company do?
2. Who is the customer?
3. What's the evidence it's working?
4. What are they asking for?

If the answers to all 4 are correct and specific, your deck passes the test. Most decks fail on question 1 (too jargon-heavy) and question 3 (traction is buried or vague).
