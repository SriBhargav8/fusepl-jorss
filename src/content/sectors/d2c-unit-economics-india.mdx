# D2C Unit Economics in India: The Numbers That Determine if Your Brand Survives

India's D2C ecosystem crossed ₹55,000 crore in GMV in 2025. Over 800 brands have raised institutional capital since 2019. Dozens have crossed ₹100 crore in annual revenue. A handful — Mamaearth, Boat, Lenskart, mCaffeine — have crossed ₹1,000 crore.

And yet, the ecosystem has a dirty secret: most D2C brands in India are not unit-economics positive. They are GMV stories dressed up as growth stories, with contribution margins that turn negative the moment you strip out first-order discounts, account for actual return rates, and use blended CAC instead of reported performance marketing CAC.

This is not an opinion. The data from CRISIL's 2024 D2C Credit Assessment Report showed that 68% of D2C brands that raised Seed or Series A between 2020 and 2022 were still contribution-margin negative at the order level as of mid-2024. A RedSeer analysis from late 2023 found that the average Indian D2C brand in fashion and beauty had a payback period of 18–24 months on customer acquisition — in a market where repeat purchase rates at 180 days rarely exceed 30%.

This article does not sugarcoat any of that. It gives you the real math, the real benchmarks, and the framework to know whether your brand has a genuine business or a well-funded treadmill.

## The 7 Unit Economics Metrics Every Indian D2C Brand Must Track

Before we get to benchmarks, let's establish the right metrics. Most D2C founders track 3–4 of these. Profitable ones track all 7.

### Metric 1: First-Order Contribution Margin (CM1)

This is the margin on a single order after deducting everything variable: COGS, packaging, payment gateway fees, last-mile logistics, and returns processing.

**Formula:**

```
CM1 = Revenue - COGS - Packaging - Payment Gateway - Logistics - Returns Cost
```

**Example calculation for a ₹800 order (fashion, prepaid):**

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Selling Price (net of discount) | ₹800 |
| COGS (fabric, manufacturing) | ₹240 (30%) |
| Packaging | ₹40 |
| Payment Gateway (2%) | ₹16 |
| Last-Mile Delivery | ₹80 |
| Return Rate Adjustment (12% prepaid × ₹120 reverse logistics) | ₹14 |
| **CM1** | **₹410 (51%)** |

Now run the same order as COD:

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Selling Price | ₹800 |
| COGS | ₹240 |
| Packaging | ₹40 |
| Payment Gateway (COD handling fee ~₹30 + 1.5%) | ₹42 |
| Last-Mile Delivery | ₹90 |
| Return Rate Adjustment (28% COD × ₹150 reverse logistics) | ₹42 |
| **CM1** | **₹346 (43%)** |

That's an 8 percentage point swing based purely on payment mode. In a category where 50–60% of orders are COD (fashion, beauty), the blended CM1 looks dramatically different from the prepaid-only version most founders report.

### Metric 2: Contribution Margin After Marketing (CM2)

CM2 adds customer acquisition cost to the picture. This is where most Indian D2C brands break.

```
CM2 = CM1 - CAC (performance marketing attributed)
```

If your CM1 is ₹410 and your Meta/Google attributed CAC is ₹350, your CM2 is ₹60. That looks workable.

But if your blended CAC (accounting for sampling, influencer spend, brand spend, and agency fees all divided by total new customers) is ₹900, your CM2 is -₹490. You are paying ₹490 to acquire every new customer.

This is the gap most D2C brands refuse to confront honestly.

### Metric 3: Repeat Purchase Rate at 90 and 180 Days

The 90-day repeat rate tells you whether your product delivers on its promise quickly enough to generate a second purchase. The 180-day rate tells you whether you have a loyal customer or a one-and-done buyer.

In India's D2C ecosystem, these numbers vary dramatically by category:

| Category | 90-Day Repeat Rate | 180-Day Repeat Rate |
|----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| Health Supplements | 35–50% | 50–65% |
| Skincare / Personal Care | 25–40% | 40–55% |
| Hair Care | 20–35% | 35–50% |
| D2C Food & Beverages | 30–45% | 45–60% |
| Fashion (non-basics) | 15–25% | 25–40% |
| Home & Living | 10–18% | 18–30% |
| Pet Care | 40–55% | 60–75% |

*Sources: Blume Ventures D2C Benchmark Report 2024, RedSeer D2C Tracker, company disclosures*

Fashion has structurally low repeat rates because customers buy outfits for occasions, not replenishments. Health supplements have high repeat rates because they are consumables. Your business model needs to match your category's structural repeat behavior — not fight it.

### Metric 4: Lifetime Value (LTV)

LTV is where most D2C founders get their math wrong. The common mistake is using average order value × average orders per customer. The correct formula accounts for the time value of money and customer churn.

**Simple LTV formula:**

```
LTV = (AOV × Gross Margin %) / Monthly Churn Rate
```

**More precise cohort-based LTV:**

```
LTV = Σ (CM1 per order × Purchase Frequency in Period n × Retention Rate^n)
     for n = 1 to 36 months
```

**Worked example for a skincare brand:**

| Input | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Average Order Value | ₹1,200 |
| CM1 per order | ₹540 (45%) |
| Monthly Purchase Frequency (repeat customers) | 0.5 orders/month |
| Monthly Retention (1 - monthly churn) | 85% |

```
12-month LTV = ₹540 × 0.5 × (1 - 0.85^12) / (1 - 0.85)
             = ₹270 × (1 - 0.142) / 0.15
             = ₹270 × 5.72
             = ₹1,544
```

That's a 12-month LTV of ₹1,544. If your CAC is ₹800, your LTV/CAC is 1.9x — technically positive, but thin. Industry standard for a healthy D2C brand is 3x+.

### Metric 5: LTV/CAC Ratio

This is the most-cited, most-misunderstood metric in D2C.

| LTV/CAC | Signal |
|---------|--------|
| < 1x | Actively destroying capital with every customer acquired |
| 1x–2x | Marginally positive but no room for error |
| 2x–3x | Acceptable at early stage, needs improvement |
| 3x–5x | Healthy — reinvest aggressively |
| > 5x | Strong moat in acquisition or retention — scale |

**The Indian D2C reality:** Most brands present their best-cohort LTV/CAC to investors. The blended number across all cohorts is usually 30–40% lower.

### Metric 6: Payback Period

Payback period = how many months until your CM2 cumulative cash flow turns positive on a customer. This is more practically useful than LTV/CAC because it tells you how much working capital you need to fund growth.

```
Payback Period = CAC / (Monthly CM1 per customer × Retention Rate)
```

**Example:**

| Input | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Blended CAC | ₹900 |
| Monthly CM1 per retained customer | ₹270 (based on 0.5 order/month × ₹540 CM1) |
| 6-month retention | 72% |

```
Monthly Contribution from retained customer = ₹270 × 0.72 = ₹194
Payback Period = ₹900 / ₹194 = 4.6 months
```

A 4.6-month payback on a blended CAC of ₹900 is strong. The problem is most Indian D2C brands have blended CACs of ₹1,500–₹2,500 and monthly contributions of ₹100–₹150, yielding payback periods of 12–20 months. If you're raising a round every 18 months and your payback period is 20 months, you are permanently dependent on external capital.

### Metric 7: Gross Margin

Gross margin is the foundation everything else rests on. Before CAC, before logistics, before returns — what is the margin between your selling price and your cost to produce the product?

| Category | Healthy Gross Margin | Warning Zone |
|----------|---------------------|--------------|
| Skincare / Beauty | 55–70% | < 50% |
| Health Supplements | 50–65% | < 45% |
| D2C Food | 40–55% | < 35% |
| Fashion (private label) | 50–65% | < 45% |
| Home & Living | 45–60% | < 40% |
| Pet Care | 45–60% | < 40% |

If your gross margin is below 40%, you cannot build a profitable D2C brand at scale on performance marketing alone. The economics do not work. You need either premium pricing, alternative distribution, or a fundamentally different product structure.

## India-Specific D2C Benchmarks by Category

### Beauty and Personal Care

**Market context:** India's BPC D2C market was ₹14,000 crore in 2025 (Redseer). Dominated by skincare (40%), hair care (30%), and cosmetics (30%).

| Metric | Early Stage (< ₹5Cr ARR) | Growth Stage (₹5–50Cr ARR) | Scale Stage (₹50Cr+ ARR) |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Gross Margin | 55–65% | 58–68% | 60–70% |
| Blended CAC | ₹600–₹1,200 | ₹800–₹1,500 | ₹700–₹1,200 |
| 90-day Repeat Rate | 20–30% | 28–40% | 35–50% |
| LTV (12-month) | ₹1,200–₹1,800 | ₹1,800–₹2,800 | ₹2,500–₹4,000 |
| LTV/CAC | 1.2–2.5x | 1.8–3x | 2.5–4x |
| COD % | 40–55% | 30–45% | 20–35% |
| Return Rate (COD) | 15–25% | 12–20% | 10–18% |

**Key insight:** BPC brands that crack the subscription/auto-replenishment model (like mCaffeine's subscription program or Minimalist's subscription box) report 180-day retention 2x higher than non-subscription cohorts. This is the unlock.

### Food and Beverages

**Market context:** India's D2C food market is ₹8,000 crore and growing at 28% CAGR. The categories with the best D2C economics are health snacks, specialty coffee, and regional foods.

| Metric | Healthy Snacks | Specialty Coffee | Regional/Ethnic Food |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Gross Margin | 45–55% | 50–65% | 40–55% |
| Blended CAC | ₹500–₹900 | ₹400–₹800 | ₹400–₹700 |
| 90-day Repeat Rate | 30–45% | 40–55% | 25–35% |
| AOV | ₹600–₹1,000 | ₹500–₹900 | ₹400–₹800 |
| Payback Period | 4–8 months | 3–6 months | 5–10 months |

**Key challenge:** Food D2C is one of the few categories where Amazon and quick-commerce platforms (Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart) actually help D2C economics — brands can use them for repeat buyers while using D2C website for first-order economics capture with better margins. The cannibalization fear is usually overblown.

### Fashion

**Market context:** Fashion D2C is the most challenging category for unit economics in India. The combination of high returns, trend-driven purchasing, and intense Meta/Instagram advertising competition creates brutal CAC dynamics.

| Metric | Ethnic Wear | Western Casual | Premium/Luxury |
|--------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Gross Margin | 50–65% | 45–60% | 55–70% |
| Blended CAC | ₹1,000–₹2,000 | ₹1,200–₹2,500 | ₹1,500–₹3,000 |
| 90-day Repeat Rate | 15–25% | 12–20% | 20–30% |
| COD Return Rate | 25–40% | 30–45% | 15–25% |
| Prepaid Return Rate | 8–15% | 10–18% | 5–12% |
| 12-month LTV | ₹1,500–₹2,500 | ₹1,200–₹2,000 | ₹3,000–₹6,000 |

**The fashion D2C trap:** The brands that look best on Instagram are often the worst on unit economics. High-fashion, aspirational branding drives expensive CAC and low repeat rates. Functional basics (innerwear, socks, activewear) with premium positioning are the only fashion D2C sub-category with consistently good unit economics in India — brands like Bummer and Wrogn's basics line demonstrate this.

### Health Supplements

**Market context:** India's health supplements D2C market crossed ₹7,500 crore in 2025. Post-COVID health awareness drove a structural shift. This category has the best structural unit economics in Indian D2C.

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Gross Margin | 55–70% |
| Blended CAC | ₹700–₹1,400 |
| 90-day Repeat Rate | 40–55% |
| 180-day Repeat Rate | 55–70% |
| AOV | ₹900–₹1,800 |
| Subscription Penetration (top brands) | 25–40% of revenue |
| 12-month LTV | ₹3,000–₹6,000 |
| LTV/CAC | 3–6x |

**Why supplements win on unit economics:** The product is consumable (built-in repeat), has high gross margins (manufacturing cost is low relative to perceived value), and addresses a genuine recurring need. The subscription angle converts well. Brands like OZiva, Wellbeing Nutrition, and The Whole Truth have demonstrated that LTV/CAC above 4x is achievable at scale in this category.

### Home and Living

**Market context:** Home D2C is an opportunistic category — high AOV, low purchase frequency. The economics work if you get creative with cross-sell and upsell architecture.

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|

| Blended CAC | ₹1,200–₹2,500 |
| 90-day Repeat Rate | 10–18% |

| Return Rate | 8–15% (lower than fashion because products are seen before ordering) |
| 12-month LTV | ₹2,500–₹5,000 |

**The home D2C challenge:** Low repeat frequency means high payback periods. Successful home D2C brands solve this by expanding SKU range aggressively (turning a bedsheet brand into a full home furnishing brand), launching subscription products (candles, cleaning products), or building B2B channels alongside D2C.

## The CAC Trap: Why Indian D2C Blended CAC Is 2–3x Reported CAC

This is the single most important section of this article. The gap between "reported" CAC and "true blended" CAC is where Indian D2C economics go wrong.

### What Gets Left Out of Reported Performance CAC

Most founders (and many investors) look at **performance marketing attributed CAC**: the cost of the Meta/Google campaigns that directly led to purchases, divided by the number of purchases. This number is almost always misleading for 5 reasons.

**1. Attribution Inflation**

Meta's last-click attribution claims credit for purchases from customers who would have bought anyway (existing brand searchers, repeat purchasers, etc.). A properly de-duplicated CAC is 20–40% higher than what Meta's Ads Manager reports.

**2. Sampling and Influencer Spend**

Most beauty and food brands spend 8–15% of revenue on product sampling, PR samples, and micro-influencer gifting. This rarely appears in performance CAC calculations but directly drives new customer acquisition.

**3. Return-Induced CAC Inflation**

When a customer returns a product and gets a refund, you've spent CAC to acquire a customer who generated zero net revenue. If your return rate is 25%, your effective CAC is 25% higher than it appears:

```
Effective CAC = Reported CAC / (1 - Return Rate)
= ₹800 / (1 - 0.25)
= ₹1,067
```

**4. First-Order Discount Subsidy**

Indian D2C brands routinely offer 15–30% discounts on first orders (welcome code, first-purchase offer, etc.). This subsidy is a cost of customer acquisition. If your AOV is ₹800 and you offer a 20% first-order discount, you're subsidizing ₹160 per new customer — but this doesn't show up in CAC.

**5. Agency and Tool Fees**

Performance marketing agencies charge 12–18% of ad spend as management fee. Analytics tools (Klaviyo, Clevertap, Webengage), attribution software (Northbeam, Triple Whale), and creative production costs add another 5–10% of ad spend.

### Calculating True Blended CAC

**Formula:**

```
Blended CAC = (Performance Marketing Spend + Influencer/Sampling + Brand Spend + Agency Fees
               + First-Order Discounts + Tools) / Net New Customers Acquired
```

**Where Net New Customers = Total New Customers × (1 - Return Rate)**

**Worked example: Beauty brand, ₹10 crore monthly GMV:**

| Acquisition Cost Component | Monthly Spend |
|---------------------------|---------------|
| Meta/Google Ads | ₹80L |
| Influencer Marketing | ₹15L |
| Sampling/PR | ₹8L |
| Agency Fees (15% of ad spend) | ₹12L |
| Tools (Klaviyo, Triple Whale, etc.) | ₹3L |
| First-Order Discounts (avg ₹160 × 8,000 new customers) | ₹12.8L |
| **Total Acquisition Spend** | **₹130.8L** |

Reported performance CAC (₹80L / 10,000 attributed new customers) = **₹800**

Actual new customers after returns (10,000 × 0.85) = **8,500**

True blended CAC (₹130.8L / 8,500) = **₹1,538**

The gap: 92% higher than reported. This brand thinks its CAC is ₹800. It's actually ₹1,538. Nearly double.

## Why COD Returns Destroy D2C Unit Economics

Cash-on-delivery is the default payment mode for most Indian e-commerce customers outside the top 6–8 metros. For any D2C brand targeting tier-2 and tier-3 India, COD is unavoidable.

The problem: COD orders return at 2–4x the rate of prepaid orders.

### The Return Rate Data

| Category | Prepaid Return Rate | COD Return Rate |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Fashion (tops, dresses) | 8–15% | 28–42% |
| Footwear | 10–18% | 25–38% |
| Beauty (liquid products) | 3–7% | 12–18% |
| Beauty (makeup/cosmetics) | 5–10% | 15–25% |
| Health Supplements | 2–5% | 5–10% |
| Home Furnishing | 5–10% | 12–20% |
| Electronics/Gadgets | 8–12% | 18–25% |

*Sources: Shiprocket 2024 E-commerce Returns Report, Delhivery Annual Insights, company discussions*

### The Full Cost of a COD Return

Most brands count only the reverse logistics cost of a return. The full cost is much higher:

| Cost Component | Amount (fashion, ₹800 order) |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| Reverse logistics | ₹120–₹150 |
| Re-QC and repackaging | ₹30–₹50 |
| Restocking / time to resell | ₹40–₹80 |
| Payment gateway (COD handling fee, not recovered) | ₹30 |
| Opportunity cost of inventory held | ₹20–₹40 |
| **Total Return Cost per Order** | **₹240–₹350** |

On a ₹800 COD fashion order with 35% return rate, your expected return cost is:
```
₹290 (average return cost) × 35% = ₹101.50 per COD order
```

Now compare: your logistics cost to deliver a COD order is ₹90. Your expected return cost is ₹101. You're spending more on dealing with returns than you are on delivering the product.

### The Prepaid Push Imperative

Every 10 percentage point increase in prepaid orders improves blended contribution margins by 2–4 percentage points. Strategies that work:

- **Prepaid discount**: Offer ₹50–₹100 discount for UPI/card payments (most effective)
- **COD charges**: Add ₹30–₹60 to COD orders (effective but reduces conversion)
- **Prepaid-only flash sales**: Drive prepaid habit formation
- **Size guarantee programs**: Reduces fashion return rate by giving customers confidence (Nykaa Fashion's fit guarantee reduced prepaid returns by 30%)

The math makes this a priority above almost every other optimization.

## The Worked Example: Profitable vs Looks-Profitable D2C Brand

Let's build out two complete P&L scenarios for a D2C brand at ₹5 crore monthly GMV.

### Brand A: Looks Profitable on Paper

**Setup:** Beauty brand, ₹600 AOV, 45% gross margin, ₹800 reported CAC

| Metric | Brand A |
|--------|---------|
| Monthly GMV | ₹5,00,00,000 |
| AOV | ₹600 |
| Orders | 83,333 |
| New Customers (30% of orders) | 25,000 |
| Revenue after returns (8% blended) | ₹4,60,00,000 |
| COGS (55% = 45% gross margin) | ₹2,53,00,000 |
| **Gross Profit** | **₹2,07,00,000** |
| Logistics | ₹58,33,000 (₹70/order) |
| Payment Gateway (2%) | ₹9,20,000 |
| Returns Processing | ₹4,00,000 |
| Performance Marketing (reported ₹800 × 25,000) | ₹2,00,00,000 |
| **Contribution Margin** | **-₹64,53,000** |

Wait — but Brand A's founder says CAC is ₹800 and gross margin is 45%, that should be positive! The problem: ₹800 × 25,000 customers = ₹2 crore in performance spend on ₹5 crore GMV = 40% of revenue on marketing alone. That's before agency fees, influencer spend, or first-order discounts.

### Brand B: Actually Profitable

**Setup:** Health supplements brand, ₹1,200 AOV, 62% gross margin, ₹1,200 blended CAC — but only 8,000 new customers/month (more selective acquisition)

| Metric | Brand B |
|--------|---------|
| Monthly GMV | ₹5,00,00,000 |
| AOV | ₹1,200 |
| Orders | 41,667 |
| New Customers (20% of orders) | 8,333 |
| Revenue after returns (4% blended) | ₹4,80,00,000 |
| COGS (38% = 62% gross margin) | ₹1,90,00,000 |
| **Gross Profit** | **₹2,90,00,000** |
| Logistics | ₹37,50,000 (₹90/order on lower volume) |
| Payment Gateway (2%) | ₹9,60,000 |
| Returns Processing | ₹80,000 |
| Blended Marketing (₹1,200 × 8,333) | ₹1,00,00,000 |
| **Contribution Margin** | **₹1,42,10,000 (29.6%)** |

Brand B is generating ₹1.42 crore in contribution profit on ₹5 crore GMV — a 29.6% CM2 margin. Brand A is losing ₹64 lakh on the same revenue.

The difference is not that Brand B spends more on marketing. It spends less. The differences are: better gross margins from a higher-margin category, lower return rates, and — critically — growing through repeat purchases rather than constant new customer acquisition.

## Building Toward Profitability: The Path for Indian D2C Brands

### Phase 1: Fix the Foundation (< ₹5 crore ARR)

**Priority 1: Know your real numbers.** Calculate true blended CAC. Calculate CM1 per order by payment mode. Understand your return rate by channel, category, and region.

**Priority 2: Hit gross margin thresholds.** If you're below 50% gross margin, fix your product economics before spending on growth. Either increase price (test incrementally), reduce COGS (negotiate with manufacturers, optimize formulation), or both.

**Priority 3: Reduce COD exposure.** Target > 60% prepaid within 18 months of launch. This single lever improves margins more than most operational improvements.

### Phase 2: Build Retention Infrastructure (₹5–25 crore ARR)

The brands that survive the D2C shakeout are the ones that build repeat purchase infrastructure before they need it.

**Retention levers that work in India:**

1. **WhatsApp CRM**: India-specific. WhatsApp message open rates are 70%+ vs 25% for email. Brands using WhatsApp for retention (not just transactional notifications) report 25–35% improvement in 90-day repeat rate.

2. **Loyalty Program**: Not a points-only program (Indian consumers have points fatigue from credit cards). Value-based loyalty — early access to new products, free samples with orders, membership discounts — outperforms points-only by 40%.

3. **Subscription/Auto-Replenishment**: Works best for consumables (supplements, coffee, skincare basics). Brands with >20% subscription revenue have LTV 2.5–3x higher than non-subscription cohorts.

4. **Post-Purchase Experience**: The 48-hour post-delivery window is the highest-leverage retention window. A personal thank-you WhatsApp message, a QR code with usage instructions, a request for feedback — these drive return purchase intent.

### Phase 3: Move Beyond Performance Marketing (₹25–100 crore ARR)

At ₹25 crore ARR, you're spending ₹3–6 crore/month on performance marketing. At this scale, performance marketing CAC is structurally rising because you're saturating your addressable audience.

The brands that succeed past ₹50 crore have usually done one or more of the following:

**Brand marketing investment**: Content, community, press. The brands that can say "we don't run ads anymore — our customers do the marketing for us" are 18–24 months of consistent brand building away from where you are at ₹5 crore ARR.

**Omnichannel expansion**: Nykaa, Amazon, and quick-commerce (Blinkit/Zepto for eligible categories) are not the enemy. They are repeat-purchase channels where your existing customers buy because it's convenient. Using these channels for repeat customers while using your website for first-time acquisition is not cannibalization — it's channel optimization.

**B2B/Institutional**: Many D2C brands have found that B2B channels (hospitals for supplements, corporate gifting for food, hotels for home/bath) have lower CAC, higher order values, and better margins than D2C. Marico, Emami, and HUL have all used institutional channels to subsidize their direct channels.

## What Indian D2C Investors Look for Now vs 2021

The D2C investment landscape changed fundamentally between 2021 and 2024. Here's what changed:

### 2021: What Got You Funded

- High MoM GMV growth (30%+ MoM was table stakes)
- Social media following and engagement
- Brand story and founder narrative
- Category tailwinds
- "We're the X for India" positioning

### 2024–2026: What Gets You Funded

**Contribution margin evidence**: Investors now want to see CM2 (after marketing) at or approaching positive territory at the unit level. A company growing 15% MoM with 25% CM2 is more fundable than one growing 40% MoM with -10% CM2.

**Cohort retention data**: Specifically the 90-day and 180-day repeat purchase rates by acquisition cohort. Declining cohort quality (newer cohorts repeating less than older ones) is a red flag.

**Blended vs. attributed CAC**: VCs now specifically ask for blended CAC. If you present only performance-marketing CAC, expect a follow-up question about what the blended number looks like.

**Payback period < 12 months**: The 2021 era of 18–24 month payback periods funded by growth capital is over. Investors want to see payback periods under 12 months before Series A, under 9 months for Series B.

**Path to profitability on existing revenue base**: Not "we'll be profitable at 3x our current revenue" but "here's the operational leverage that gets us to EBITDA positive on ₹X crore monthly revenue, and we're ₹Y crore away."

**Omnichannel credibility**: Investors are skeptical of brands that are 100% D2C website at growth stage. The narrative they want to hear: "We built on website to learn, now we're building offline/omnichannel for scale."

### The Metrics That Will Get You a Term Sheet in 2026

If you can walk into a room and show:
- LTV/CAC > 3x on blended basis
- 180-day repeat rate > 40%
- CM2 > 20% at blended CAC
- Payback period < 10 months
- < 25% COD across all channels
- YoY growth > 60%

You will get a term sheet from most India-focused consumer investors within 60 days. These metrics simultaneously.

## Conclusion

Indian D2C is not a broken model. It is a model that punishes those who don't understand their numbers — and rewards those who do.

The brands that will survive the next wave of D2C consolidation in India are not the ones with the biggest Instagram following or the fastest GMV growth. They are the ones that know their blended CAC, have a plan to reduce COD exposure, have retention infrastructure that generates repeat purchases without re-acquisition cost, and have gross margins high enough to absorb the inevitable cost increases as they scale.

The math is unforgiving. But it is also transparent. If you track the 7 metrics in this article honestly, you will know exactly where your business stands — and exactly what you need to fix.
