# Fintech Licensing in India — RBI, SEBI, IRDAI: Which License Does Your Startup Need?

Shashank Kumar co-founded Razorpay without a payment aggregator license. He operated under a payment aggregator partner model. By the time RBI's PA licensing regime was announced in 2020, Razorpay was large enough to apply on its own — and received in-principle approval in 2022. Today, a payment aggregator license is table stakes for any serious payments business.

This is how Indian fintech regulation works: the rules evolve, enforcement tightens, and founders who built under partner models eventually need to own their license stack or sell to someone who does.

Understanding which license you need — and whether you need one at all right now — is not just a compliance question. It is a business model question, a fundraising question, and increasingly a moat question.

---

## India's Fintech Regulatory Architecture: Five Bodies, Not One

The single most dangerous misconception in Indian fintech is treating "fintech regulation" as monolithic. There is no single fintech regulator in India. Five different bodies govern different aspects of financial services, and most fintech startups touch at least two of them.

| Regulator | Full Name | What It Governs | Key Fintech License |
|---|---|---|---|
| RBI | Reserve Bank of India | Payments, lending, banking, NBFCs | PA license, NBFC, PPI, Account Aggregator |
| SEBI | Securities and Exchange Board of India | Investment advice, stock broking, mutual funds | RIA, Stock Broker, Investment Adviser |
| IRDAI | Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority | Insurance products and distribution | IRDAI Broker, PoSP, IMF |
| PFRDA | Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority | NPS, pension products | POP (Point of Presence) |
| DPIIT + MCA | Dept for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade | Startup recognition, company incorporation | DPIIT Startup recognition |

Most early-stage fintech founders are primarily dealing with RBI. SEBI comes into play for wealth management and stockbroking. IRDAI governs anything touching insurance.

The inter-regulatory gaps are where Indian fintech lives and breathes. UPI-based credit products, for instance, involve both RBI (payments infrastructure) and RBI's NBFC framework (credit deployment). Insurance distribution via a payments app involves both IRDAI (insurance distributor license) and RBI (payments).

## The Fintech License Map: Match Your Model to Your Regulator

Before spending a rupee on legal fees, answer this question: what is the core financial flow in your business?

### Lending Fintechs → NBFC License (RBI)

If your startup deploys credit to end customers — whether personal loans, BNPL, merchant loans, or microfinance — you need an NBFC license from RBI. There is no workaround here.

The co-origination model (where an NBFC partner provides the capital and you provide the customer) is not a workaround. It means you are operating as a loan service provider (LSP) or direct selling agent (DSA) for a licensed NBFC. You can do this without your own license. But you cannot deploy capital from your own balance sheet, and you cannot take credit risk on your own books, without an NBFC license.

**NBFC Types Relevant to Fintech Startups:**

| NBFC Type | What It Does | Min Net Owned Funds |
|---|---|---|
| NBFC (Loan Company) | General lending — personal, business, consumer | ₹2 Crore |
| NBFC-MFI | Microfinance to low-income households | ₹5 Crore |
| NBFC-P2P | Peer-to-peer lending platform | ₹2 Crore |
| NBFC-AA (Account Aggregator) | Consent-based financial data sharing | ₹2 Crore |
| NBFC-ND (Non-Deposit Taking) | Most common for fintech | ₹2 Crore |
| CIC (Core Investment Company) | Holding company for financial group | ₹100 Crore |

For most fintech lending startups, the NBFC-ND (Non-Deposit Taking, Systematically Important above ₹500Cr asset size) is the relevant license. Start with NBFC-ND, operate below the ₹500Cr SI threshold initially.

### Payments Fintechs → Payment Aggregator License (RBI)

If your startup facilitates payment collection on behalf of merchants — you receive customer money and pass it to merchants — you are a Payment Aggregator (PA) under RBI's 2020 PA/PG guidelines.

**Who specifically needs a PA license:**
- Any platform collecting payments from end customers on behalf of third-party merchants
- Buy Now Pay Later platforms where you sit between the customer and merchant
- Marketplace payment settlement systems

**Who does NOT need a PA license:**
- Payment Gateways (PG) that only provide technology — no money movement through their books
- Companies collecting payment only for their own goods/services (pure e-commerce selling own products)
- Platforms using licensed PA infrastructure via APIs (Razorpay, PayU, Cashfree, etc.)

The distinction between PA and PG is critical. A PG just provides the technical plumbing. A PA actually touches the money. If the funds flow through your escrow/nodal account, even for a moment, you are a PA.

**PA License Requirements:**

| Requirement | Specification |
|---|---|
| Legal Structure | Indian company under Companies Act 2013 |
| Minimum Net Worth | ₹15 Crore at application; ₹25 Crore by March 31, 2023 (existing players) |
| Net Worth for New Applications | ₹25 Crore at time of application |
| Board Composition | Independent directors required; no criminal record |
| Escrow/Nodal Account | Mandatory with scheduled commercial bank |
| Data Localisation | Cardholder data must be stored in India |
| Information Security | PA-DSS compliance, annual VAPT audit |
| Timeline to Approval | 18–36 months from application to final authorization |

The ₹25 Crore net worth requirement killed most mid-tier payment startups. Companies like CCAvenue, Atom, and several smaller aggregators either raised emergency capital or exited to larger groups between 2020 and 2023.

### Neo-Banking / Banking-as-a-Service → No Direct License (But Multiple Partner Licenses Required)

There is no "neo-bank license" in India. RBI does not allow non-bank entities to offer deposit accounts or issue debit cards on their own. Every neo-bank in India — Jupiter, Fi, Niyo, Slice, OneCard — operates on top of a licensed bank.

The structure is:
1. Neo-bank partners with a scheduled commercial bank (Federal Bank, Equitas, SBM, etc.)
2. The bank issues the account/card under its own license
3. The neo-bank provides the UX, product design, and customer acquisition
4. Revenue sharing happens between neo-bank and bank partner

For the Prepaid Payment Instrument (PPI) — digital wallets — RBI issues PPI licenses. There are two types:

| PPI Type | What It Allows | Min Net Worth |
|---|---|---|
| Small PPI | Loading up to ₹10,000; can only be used at specific merchants | ₹5 Crore |
| Full-KYC PPI | Loading up to ₹2,00,000; interoperable; funds transfer allowed | ₹15 Crore |

Most "wallet" products use the partner bank's PPI license rather than obtaining their own. Paytm Payments Bank has its own license (payments bank license — a separate RBI category with ₹100 Crore minimum capital).

### Wealth Management / Investment Advisory → SEBI RIA License

If your startup gives personalized investment advice to individuals — recommending specific securities, mutual funds, portfolio allocations — you need a SEBI Registered Investment Adviser (RIA) license.

The distinction SEBI draws: generic financial education (allowed without license) vs. personalized investment advice based on a client's financial profile (requires RIA license).

**SEBI RIA Requirements:**

| Requirement | For Individual | For Corporate Entity |
|---|---|---|
| Net Worth | ₹5 Lakh | ₹50 Lakh |
| Qualification | Post-graduate in finance/economics or relevant professional qualification | Principal officer must qualify |
| Experience | 5 years in financial services | Same for principal officer |
| Certification | NISM Series-X-A and X-B | Same |
| Fee Structure | Only fee-from-client model | Same |

The most important regulatory change for wealth-tech startups: SEBI's 2021 amendment banned mutual fund distributors (MFDs) from calling themselves advisors. If you earn commission from the mutual fund/AMC, you are an MFD, not an RIA. An RIA can only charge clients — not earn commissions. This bifurcated the entire Indian wealth-tech space.

Companies like Scripbox, Kuvera, Groww, and Zerodha Coin operate as MFDs, not RIAs, for mutual fund distribution. SEBI's RIA framework is used by fee-only financial planning platforms.

For stockbroking — executing trades — you need a SEBI Stock Broker license, which requires a SEBI registration and membership of NSE/BSE. Minimum net worth: ₹100 Lakh for cash segment, ₹250 Lakh for derivatives. This is why every zero-commission broker in India (Zerodha, Groww, Angel One, Upstox) is a heavily capitalized, SEBI-regulated entity.

### Insurance Distribution → IRDAI PoSP / IRDA Broker License

If your startup facilitates the sale of insurance policies — travel insurance at checkout, health insurance embedded in a lending app, two-wheeler insurance in a vehicle services app — you need an IRDAI authorization.

There are three routes:

| Route | Full Form | What You Can Sell | Licensing Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|
| PoSP | Point of Sale Person | Simple, standardized products (motor, term, health) | Individual/platform-level registration; relatively easy |
| IMF | Insurance Marketing Firm | Multiple products across multiple insurers | Company-level registration; moderate |
| Insurance Broker | — | Full range of products; can advise on claims | Most comprehensive; significant capital and compliance |

For most embedded insurance fintechs, the PoSP route is the entry point. The corporate agent license (where you represent one insurance company) is common for large distribution networks. The full broker license is for dedicated insurance distribution platforms like PolicyBazaar (which operates as a licensed insurance broker).

**IRDAI Broker License Capital Requirements:**

| Broker Type | Minimum Capital |
|---|---|
| Direct Broker | ₹75 Lakh |
| Reinsurance Broker | ₹4 Crore |
| Composite Broker | ₹4 Crore |

### Crypto / Digital Assets → Currently Unregulated, But PMLA Fully Applies

As of 2026, India does not have a crypto-specific regulatory framework. The Cryptocurrency and Regulation of Official Digital Currency Bill has not been passed. Virtual digital assets (VDAs) are defined in the Income Tax Act for taxation purposes but not in any financial regulation.

However, "unregulated" does not mean "exempt from compliance." Since March 2023, Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) — crypto exchanges, wallet providers — are covered under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA). Mandatory requirements:

- **KYC/AML compliance**: Full KYC for all users, AML policies
- **STR filing**: Suspicious transaction reporting to FIU-IND
- **CTR filing**: Cash transaction reporting above ₹10 Lakh
- **Transaction monitoring**: Real-time monitoring systems
- **FIU-IND registration**: All VASPs must register with Financial Intelligence Unit

Tax obligations are separate: 30% flat tax on crypto gains, 1% TDS on transactions above ₹10,000 per transaction (₹50,000 per year for certain cases).

The practical reality: running a crypto exchange in India without robust KYC/AML infrastructure invites FIU enforcement action. WazirX's complications in 2024 demonstrated the severity of PMLA enforcement in this space.

## Deep Dive: The Payment Aggregator License

The PA license is the most sought-after fintech license among Series A–C stage startups in India. Here is the complete picture of what the process actually looks like.

### Who Actually Needs to Apply

Not every payments startup needs a PA license. The decision tree:

```
Do your customers pay YOU (your company's account) before the money
reaches the merchant?
  YES → You are potentially a PA and need a license (or a licensed PA partner)
  NO → You may be a PG (technology only) and don't need a license

Do you hold customer funds even briefly in a nodal/escrow account?
  YES → You need a PA license
  NO → Review your structure with a qualified RBI-licensed lawyer

Are you collecting payments only for your own products/services?
  YES → Not a PA; you're a merchant
  NO → Continue analysis
```

### The Application Process Timeline

The RBI PA licensing process is notoriously slow. Founders consistently underestimate the timeline by 12–18 months.

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-application preparation | 6–12 months | Build net worth to ₹25Cr, appoint compliance team, implement required systems |
| Application submission | 1 month | Submit to RBI DPSS (Department of Payment and Settlement Systems) |
| RBI preliminary review | 3–6 months | RBI raises clarifications, site visits, IT system review |
| In-Principle Approval (IPA) | — | RBI grants IPA; valid for 6 months |
| System audit and compliance certification | 3–6 months post-IPA | CERT-In empanelled auditor conducts system audit |
| Final Authorization | — | Full PA authorization granted |
| **Total typical timeline** | **18–36 months** | — |

### What RBI Scrutinizes in PA Applications

RBI's review focuses on six areas:

**1. Merchant Onboarding Process**
RBI wants to see that you have a merchant due diligence framework — you are not just onboarding anyone who applies. Specifically: verification of business legitimacy, negative list screening, ongoing transaction monitoring.

**2. Escrow Account Management**
Your nodal/escrow account structure must meet RBI's settlement timelines. For T+1 settlement (transactions settled next business day), the account management protocols must be documented and audited.

**3. Customer Grievance Mechanism**
A dedicated grievance redressal officer (not a shared resource), a turnaround time commitment for complaints (within 30 days for first resolution), and escalation to the Banking Ombudsman must all be operational.

**4. Data Security**
Full PA-DSS compliance for the payment environment. Annual Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing (VAPT) by a CERT-In empanelled agency. No storage of prohibited card data elements (CVV, full track data, PIN).

**5. Fit and Proper Directors**
All directors undergo background checks. Criminal records, prior regulatory enforcement actions, or directorship in failed/fraud companies are disqualifying. RBI has rejected applications where promoters had prior insolvency proceedings.

**6. Business Plan and Viability**
RBI reviews whether your PA business is sustainable — sufficient capital, a credible merchant pipeline, and financial projections that don't rely on unsustainable pricing. Razor-thin economics that only work at massive scale are scrutinized.

## Deep Dive: The NBFC License

The NBFC license is the workhorse of Indian fintech. Every lending startup that wants to deploy capital on its own balance sheet needs one.

### The Fit and Proper Director Requirement

This is where most NBFC applications fail or get delayed. All directors and promoters must meet RBI's "fit and proper" criteria:

- No criminal prosecution for economic offenses
- No conviction by a court for moral turpitude
- No insolvency proceedings
- No directorship in a company whose license was cancelled by RBI
- Financial soundness (no personal defaults on loans)
- Educational qualifications (for the MD/CEO, relevant professional background preferred)

RBI takes this extremely seriously. A single promoter with a history of loan defaults — even a personal car loan default 10 years ago — can delay or derail an application.

### The NBFC-AA (Account Aggregator): India's Most Underbuilt Fintech Opportunity

The Account Aggregator framework is a consent-based financial data sharing architecture built on India Stack. An AA acts as a consent manager — it does not store financial data but routes it (with user consent) from Financial Information Providers (FIPs: banks, insurers, depositories) to Financial Information Users (FIUs: lenders, wealth managers, insurance companies).

As of early 2026, there are 11 NBFC-AAs licensed by RBI: Finvu, OneMoney, CAMS FinServ, Cookiejar (PhonePe), NADL (NSDL), Perfios Account Aggregation, Juspay, and a few others.

The moat here is network effects: the AA with the most FIP connections and FIU relationships becomes the default data pipe for financial consent in India. The licensing barrier (₹2 Crore net worth is the minimum, but actual build cost is ₹5–15 Crore) creates a natural oligopoly.

**NBFC-P2P (Peer-to-Peer Lending): A Cautionary Tale**

RBI has been progressively tightening P2P regulations. In August 2023, RBI issued new guidelines restricting P2P platforms from guaranteeing returns, offering credit enhancements, or facilitating cross-platform transactions. Platforms like LiquiLoans, Faircent, and i2ifunding were forced to restructure their products. The P2P lending space in India remains viable but is much narrower than it appeared in 2018–2021.

## Operating Without a License: The Partner Model

Most early-stage fintech startups should not apply for their own license. The partner model is more capital-efficient and faster to market.

### Banking-as-a-Service (BaaS) Partners

Indian BaaS providers offer licensed banking infrastructure via APIs:

| BaaS Provider | Underlying Bank | What They Offer |
|---|---|---|
| Setu | — | NBFC-AA, UPI, lending APIs |
| RazorpayX | — | Current accounts, payouts |
| Open (now Zwitch) | — | SMB banking infrastructure |
| Zeta | — | Card issuance, core banking |
| M2P Fintech | — | Card issuance, prepaid programs |
| Yodlee / Perfios | — | Account aggregation, data APIs |

For a lending startup pre-Series A: partner with an NBFC to co-originate loans. You source the customer, the NBFC deploys capital from its balance sheet, you share in the economics as a Loan Service Provider. This is how FlexiLoans, KreditBee, and dozens of others started before obtaining their own NBFC licenses.

### The Co-Origination Model

RBI introduced the co-lending/co-origination model via its 2018 master direction to allow banks and NBFCs to jointly originate loans. The structure:

```
Co-origination structure (post-2018 RBI model):

Bank → provides 80% of loan capital
NBFC-Fintech → provides 20% of loan capital
NBFC-Fintech → handles customer sourcing, underwriting, servicing
Shared: Interest income split in agreed proportion
Risk: Shared pro-rata based on capital contribution
```

For fintech startups without their own NBFC license, the equivalent is the LSP (Loan Service Provider) model — you handle the customer interface and underwriting, a licensed NBFC deploys 100% of capital, and you earn a service fee or commission.

Post-RBI's 2022 Digital Lending Guidelines, the LSP model has stricter requirements: disbursements must go directly from NBFC to borrower (not via fintech intermediary), collections must go directly from borrower to NBFC's bank account, and the NBFC remains fully responsible for credit risk.

### BNPL After the RBI Crackdown (2022–2023)

Pre-2022, BNPL products in India operated via a creative structure: a fintech would load credit onto a prepaid card issued by a PPI license holder, which customers could use at merchant checkout. This created a credit-via-PPI product that bypassed the NBFC framework.

RBI's June 2022 circular killed this: non-bank PPIs cannot be loaded using credit lines (including BNPL). Credit can only be loaded onto PPIs by scheduled commercial banks. This forced every BNPL startup (ZestMoney, LazyPay, Simpl, Slice, etc.) to either partner with a bank, obtain an NBFC license, or pivot to a different structure.

The aftermath: ZestMoney shut down in 2023. Slice merged with North East Small Finance Bank in 2024. The survivors are those that obtained NBFC licenses or integrated deeply with banking partners.

## Regulatory Sandboxes: Testing Without a Full License

Both RBI and SEBI operate regulatory sandboxes for fintech startups — a structured environment where innovations can be tested with real customers under relaxed regulatory requirements for a defined period.

### RBI Regulatory Sandbox

RBI has run multiple cohorts since 2019, each focused on specific themes:

| Cohort | Theme | Year | Notable Participants |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cohort 1 | Retail Payments | 2019–2020 | Multiple payment innovation startups |
| Cohort 2 | Cross-Border Payments | 2020–2021 | — |
| Cohort 3 | MSME Lending | 2021–2022 | — |
| Cohort 4 | Prevention of Financial Fraud | 2022–2023 | — |
| Cohort 5 | Sustainable Finance | 2023–2024 | — |

**Sandbox Benefits:**
- Test with real customers (up to 10,000 users for testing period)
- Regulatory relaxation on specific requirements during test window
- Direct engagement with RBI teams — invaluable for understanding regulatory intent
- Does NOT guarantee a license at the end (but greatly accelerates the application)

**Sandbox Limitations:**
- Cohort themes are pre-set; you must fit the announced theme
- Application process is competitive; fewer than 15 startups per cohort
- Test period typically 6 months; not enough to prove a lending business
- Does not exempt you from PMLA, consumer protection laws, or Companies Act

### SEBI Regulatory Sandbox

SEBI's sandbox (launched 2020) is focused on securities market innovations: new trading mechanisms, investor onboarding technology, market surveillance tools. Less relevant for pure lending or payments fintechs, but important for fintech products touching capital markets.

## The Real Cost of Fintech Compliance

Founders consistently underestimate the total cost of running a licensed fintech entity. The license fee itself is the smallest expense.

### Annual Compliance Cost by License Type

| License | Annual Compliance Cost | Components |
|---|---|---|
| NBFC (small, < ₹100Cr AUM) | ₹25–50 Lakh/year | CA fees, RBI returns, legal, compliance officer |
| NBFC (₹100–500Cr AUM) | ₹75L–1.5Cr/year | Above + additional reporting, larger compliance team |
| Payment Aggregator | ₹75L–2Cr/year | VAPT audit (₹15–25L), PCI DSS (₹20–40L), CA, legal, compliance team |
| SEBI RIA | ₹10–25L/year | SEBI fees, NISM certifications, compliance officer |
| SEBI Stock Broker | ₹50L–1.5Cr/year | NSE/BSE fees, compliance team, technology audits |
| IRDAI Insurance Broker | ₹25–75L/year | Actuary, compliance, IRDAI filings |

### The Compliance Team You Need

For an NBFC with ₹50–200 Crore AUM:
- **Chief Compliance Officer**: ₹15–25 LPA (typically CA/CS with NBFC experience)
- **CA firm retainer (RBI returns + audit)**: ₹5–15 Lakh/year
- **CS firm retainer (ROC, company secretary functions)**: ₹3–6 Lakh/year
- **Legal counsel retainer (ongoing advisory)**: ₹5–15 Lakh/year
- **IT security / CISO function**: ₹10–20 LPA or outsourced

Total: ₹50–80 Lakh per year minimum for a mid-scale NBFC's compliance function. This is before the cost of building the technology required for regulatory reporting (RBI XBRL submissions, etc.).

### Technology for Compliance

Fintech compliance is increasingly a technology problem, not just a process problem. Key systems required:

```
NBFC Technology Stack for Compliance:

  Loan Origination System (LOS) → captures complete underwriting trail
  Loan Management System (LMS) → payment schedules, EMI tracking, NPA classification
  KYC/AML Engine → Video KYC, PAN verification, CIBIL pull, blacklist screening
  Reporting Engine → RBI XBRL returns (monthly, quarterly, annual)
  Customer Grievance Portal → SEBI/RBI mandated

Estimated technology build cost: ₹50L–1.5Cr
Annual licensing/SaaS cost for off-the-shelf solutions: ₹20–50L/year
```

## Why the License Is a Startup Moat

Beyond compliance, a fintech license creates defensible competitive advantage in three specific ways.

### 1. Barrier to Entry Increases Competitive Moat

A NBFC license that takes 12–18 months and ₹2 Crore minimum capital to obtain, requires fit-and-proper directors, and has ongoing compliance costs — that is a genuine barrier to entry. New competitors cannot simply clone your product and launch immediately. They face the same regulatory hurdle.

In a market where most startup moats are temporary (better UX, first-mover brand, marketing budget), regulatory moats are among the most durable.

### 2. Valuation Premium

At Series B and beyond, licensed fintech entities command a meaningful valuation premium over unlicensed BaaS-dependent models:

- **NBFC**: Valued on P/BV (Price/Book Value) in addition to revenue multiples — the license and capital sitting in the NBFC have inherent book value
- **Payment Aggregator**: PA-licensed companies have sold at significant premiums. PayU acquired PaySharp; several PA-licensed startups command 15–25x revenue multiples
- **SEBI-registered entities**: RIA/broker licenses enable product breadth (advisory + execution) that purely tech platforms cannot offer

### 3. Partnership Leverage

Licensed entities can negotiate better economics with banking partners, insurance companies, and enterprise customers. An NBFC with its own capital can negotiate co-lending terms that an LSP model cannot access. A PA-licensed entity has better settlement terms than a sub-merchant of another PA.

## The License Decision Framework: A Practical Guide for Founders

Before making any licensing decision, answer these questions in sequence:

```
Step 1: What is the primary financial flow in your business?
  → Customer pays merchant via your platform → PA license
  → You deploy credit to end customers → NBFC license
  → You give investment advice → SEBI RIA license
  → You distribute insurance → IRDAI PoSP/IMF/Broker
  → You build data infrastructure → NBFC-AA license
  → You operate crypto exchange → PMLA compliance only (for now)

Step 2: Can you start with a partner model?
  → Yes for most categories
  → Timeline advantage: partner model = 2–4 months to launch vs 18–36 months for license
  → Capital advantage: save the ₹15–25Cr net worth requirement for 2–3 years

Step 3: At what scale does owning the license become essential?
  → PA: when your TPV exceeds ₹1,000Cr/year (sub-PA economics become unattractive)
  → NBFC: when you want to deploy > ₹100Cr from own balance sheet
  → RIA: when personalized advisory is core to your value proposition

Step 4: What is your licensing runway?
  → Start the application process 18–24 months before you need the license
  → Net worth requirements must be met at application, not approval
  → Budget ₹2–4Cr for the application process (legal, compliance build, CA)
```

## Regulatory Trends to Watch in 2026

**RBI's Fintech Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO-FT)**
RBI has been pushing the fintech sector to establish a self-regulatory body. The Fintech Association for Consumer Empowerment (FACE) and Digital Lenders Association of India (DLAI) are the likely pillars. SRO membership may become a compliance requirement for LSPs.

**SEBI's Registered Investment Adviser Reforms**
SEBI is expected to lower barriers to RIA registration for pure digital advisory models while simultaneously increasing enforcement against unregistered advisors on social media ("finfluencers"). This creates both opportunity and risk.

**Account Aggregator Expansion**
The NBFC-AA ecosystem is expanding from financial data to non-financial data (GST data via GSTN, health records via ABDM). This expands the AA moat for licensed entities significantly.

**Digital Lending Guidelines Enforcement**
RBI's 2022 Digital Lending Guidelines are being enforced with increasing stringency. LSPs operating without proper agreements with regulated entities, or fintechs acting as de facto lenders without NBFC licenses, face enforcement action.
