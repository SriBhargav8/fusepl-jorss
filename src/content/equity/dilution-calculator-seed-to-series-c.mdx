# Dilution Calculator: Model Seed to Series C in 5 Minutes

Most founders think about dilution deal by deal. "I'm giving up 20% in this round — that's okay."

What they don't model is the **cumulative effect** of 4–5 rounds of dilution, ESOP pool creation, and employee option grants. By the time they're at Series B or C, many founders are shocked to discover they own 15–20% of a company they founded.

Is 15% bad? Not necessarily — it depends on the valuation. A 15% stake in a ₹1,000 crore company is ₹150 crore. A 40% stake in a ₹50 crore company is ₹20 crore.

But founders who don't model the dilution path make bad decisions — they give up too much too early, they create ESOP pools that are pre-money when they should be post-money, and they agree to terms that compound badly.

This guide walks through the full dilution math from day one to Series C, with the actual numbers Indian startups typically see.

---

## The Starting Point: Founder Equity at Incorporation

When you incorporate, the cap table is simple. Let's use a 2-founder company:

| Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| Founder A | 5,00,000 | 50% |
| Founder B | 5,00,000 | 50% |
| **Total** | **10,00,000** | **100%** |

Face value: ₹10 per share (standard for Indian private companies)
Total paid-up capital: ₹1,00,00,000 (₹1 crore at face value — this is not the valuation)

---

## Round 1: Angel / Pre-Seed Round

**Terms:**
- Amount: ₹50 lakhs
- Pre-money valuation: ₹2 crore
- Post-money valuation: ₹2.5 crore
- New shares issued: calculated below

**Dilution calculation:**

```
Price per share = Pre-money valuation / Pre-money shares
= ₹2,00,00,000 / 10,00,000 = ₹20 per share

New shares issued = Investment / Price per share
= ₹50,00,000 / ₹20 = 2,50,000 shares

Angel investor ownership = 2,50,000 / 12,50,000 = 20%
```

**Post-Angel Cap Table:**

| Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| Founder A | 5,00,000 | 40% |
| Founder B | 5,00,000 | 40% |
| Angel Investor | 2,50,000 | 20% |
| **Total** | **12,50,000** | **100%** |

**Founders' dilution:** 50% → 40% each (20% relative dilution)

---

## Round 2: Seed Round (with ESOP Pool Creation)

**Terms:**
- Amount: ₹1.5 crore
- Pre-money valuation: ₹6 crore
- ESOP pool: 10% (created pre-money — which is standard but unfavorable for founders)
- Post-money valuation: ₹7.5 crore

**Step 1: Create the ESOP pool pre-money**

Most term sheets say "10% ESOP pool on a fully-diluted pre-money basis." This means:

```
Fully diluted shares needed post-ESOP = Current shares / (1 - ESOP%)
= 12,50,000 / (1 - 0.10) = 13,88,889

New ESOP shares to create = 13,88,889 - 12,50,000 = 1,38,889 shares
```

**Post-ESOP-creation cap table (pre-money):**

| Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| Founder A | 5,00,000 | 36% |
| Founder B | 5,00,000 | 36% |
| Angel | 2,50,000 | 18% |
| ESOP Pool (unissued) | 1,38,889 | 10% |
| **Total** | **13,88,889** | **100%** |

**Step 2: Issue seed round shares**

```
Price per share = ₹6,00,00,000 / 13,88,889 = ₹43.20 per share

New shares = ₹1,50,00,000 / ₹43.20 = 3,47,222 shares

Seed investor % = 3,47,222 / (13,88,889 + 3,47,222) = 3,47,222 / 17,36,111 = 20%
```

**Post-Seed Cap Table:**

| Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| Founder A | 5,00,000 | 28.8% |
| Founder B | 5,00,000 | 28.8% |
| Angel | 2,50,000 | 14.4% |
| ESOP Pool | 1,38,889 | 8.0% |
| Seed Investor | 3,47,222 | 20.0% |
| **Total** | **17,36,111** | **100%** |

**Founders' cumulative dilution:** 50% → 28.8%

## Round 3: Series A

**Terms:**
- Amount: ₹8 crore
- Pre-money valuation: ₹32 crore
- ESOP top-up: 5% (pre-money, to bring total ESOP to ~12%)
- Post-money: ₹40 crore
- Series A investor target: 20%

**Step 1: ESOP top-up**

Assume 3% of ESOP has been issued to employees (1,38,889 × 30% = 41,667 shares issued). Remaining unissued ESOP = 97,222 shares. Investor wants total ESOP of 12% — so 5% more needs to be created.

```
New ESOP needed ≈ 17,36,111 × 5% / (1-5%) ≈ 91,375 shares
```

**Step 2: Issue Series A shares**

```
Pre-money fully diluted shares = 17,36,111 + 91,375 = 18,27,486

Price per share = ₹32,00,00,000 / 18,27,486 = ₹175.1

New shares = ₹8,00,00,000 / ₹175.1 = 4,56,882 shares

Series A % = 4,56,882 / (18,27,486 + 4,56,882) = 4,56,882 / 22,84,368 = 20%
```

**Post-Series A Cap Table:**

| Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| Founder A | 5,00,000 | 21.9% |
| Founder B | 5,00,000 | 21.9% |
| Angel | 2,50,000 | 10.9% |
| ESOP (issued) | 41,667 | 1.8% |
| ESOP (unissued pool) | 1,47,930 | 6.5% |
| Seed Investor | 3,47,222 | 15.2% |
| Series A Investor | 4,56,882 | 20.0% |
| **Total** | **22,43,701** | **~98%** |

**Founders' cumulative dilution:** 50% → 21.9% each

## Round 4: Series B

**Terms:**
- Amount: ₹30 crore

- Post-money: ₹150 crore
- Series B investor target: 20%

Founder A: ~17.5%
Founder B: ~17.5%

## The Full Dilution Journey: Summary Table

| Round | Founder A | Founder B | New Investor | Total Raised |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Incorporation | 50% | 50% | — | — |
| Angel (₹50L) | 40% | 40% | 20% | ₹50L |
| Seed (₹1.5Cr + ESOP) | 28.8% | 28.8% | 20% | ₹2Cr |
| Series A (₹8Cr) | 21.9% | 21.9% | 20% | ₹10Cr |
| Series B (₹30Cr) | 17.5% | 17.5% | 20% | ₹40Cr |
| Series C (₹100Cr) | ~13% | ~13% | 20% | ₹140Cr |

**By Series C, each founder typically owns 10–15% of the company.** This is normal and expected for a venture-backed startup that has raised ₹100–150 crore.

## The ESOP Effect: The Hidden Diluter

ESOP is the most undermodeled form of dilution. Founders think of it as "employee equity" but it comes from the same pool as their shares.

**The standard ESOP journey:**

| Round | ESOP Pool Created | Cumulative ESOP |
|---|---|---|
| Seed | 10% (pre-money) | 10% |
| Series A top-up | +3–5% | 12–15% |
| Series B top-up | +2–3% | 14–18% |

By Series B, a typical company has a 12–18% ESOP pool. If 60–70% of it is issued to employees, the remaining 4–7% is "shadow dilution" — it will dilute everyone when issued.

**Pre-money vs. post-money ESOP: the ₹ difference**

On a ₹40 crore Series A (₹32Cr pre-money, ₹8Cr raised):
- Pre-money ESOP 5% pool: Founders bear the entire dilution
- Post-money ESOP 5% pool: Dilution shared with new investor proportionally

**Impact:**
- Pre-money: Founders' effective pre-money value = ₹32Cr × 95% = ₹30.4Cr (the 5% ESOP comes out of founder value)
- Post-money: Everyone is diluted by the ESOP pro-rata

Always push for **post-money ESOP pool creation**. It's not always achievable, but the difference compounds across rounds.

## How to Model Your Own Dilution

**Step 1: Start with your current cap table** (shares per shareholder, not just %)

**Step 2: Model each future round as:**
- Pre-money valuation
- Investment amount
- ESOP pool requirement (pre or post-money)
- Calculate new shares issued
- Update % for everyone

**Step 3: Model the exit at different valuations**

| Exit Valuation | Your Stake | Your Proceeds |
|---|---|---|
| ₹100Cr | 13% | ₹13Cr |
| ₹250Cr | 13% | ₹32.5Cr |
| ₹500Cr | 13% | ₹65Cr |
| ₹1,000Cr | 13% | ₹130Cr |

**Remember to subtract liquidation preferences** from the exit proceeds before calculating your share — the gross numbers above are pre-preference.

## The 3 Dilution Mistakes That Cost Founders the Most

### Mistake 1: Pre-Money ESOP Pools (Not Pushing Back)

Every percentage point of pre-money ESOP is taken from founders. On a 10% pre-money ESOP at a ₹20Cr pre-money valuation, founders give up ₹2Cr in value. Push for post-money or at minimum negotiate the ESOP pool size down to what you actually need for the next 18 months.

### Mistake 2: Taking Too Many Small Rounds

Each round has transaction costs (legal fees, dilution, time). Taking 3 angel rounds of ₹25L, ₹50L, ₹75L creates 3x legal cost and 3x dilution events. Better to raise one ₹1.5Cr seed from a lead with co-investors.

### Mistake 3: Not Tracking Dilution in Future Rounds

Founders who modelled dilution carefully at seed are often blind to it at Series A. They don't notice when a new ESOP pool top-up plus the investor's ownership target combine to dilute them 10 percentage points in one round.

**Rule:** Before every round, run the full dilution model through to Series C and check: are my projected founder economics still acceptable at the most likely exit scenarios?
