# Founder Vesting: Why Investors Want You to Vest Your Own Company

The moment feels surreal. You've spent 18 months building this company. You gave up your salary, your weekends, your holidays. You incorporated it, named it, hired the first employees.

And now an investor is telling you that you need to "vest" your own equity — that you'll earn your shares back over 4 years.

**Why would you vest shares you already own?**

The short answer: you don't. Founder vesting is not about taking shares away from you. It's about creating a buyback mechanism that protects everyone — the company, co-founders, investors, and employees — if a founder leaves.

Understanding this distinction changes everything about how you negotiate and implement founder vesting.

---

## What Founder Vesting Actually Is

**What it is NOT:**
You don't give up your shares. You don't lose equity you've already earned. Your existing shares remain yours.

**What it IS:**
Founder vesting creates an agreement that if you leave the company before a specified period, the company has the right to buy back a portion of your shares — usually at face value (₹10 per share) or at the original issue price.

The shares that are "unvested" are the shares subject to this buyback right. As time passes and you continue working in the company, more of your shares "vest" — meaning the buyback right lapses on those shares.

After full vesting, all your shares are fully yours with no buyback right attached.

---

## The Standard Structure: 4-Year Vesting with 1-Year Cliff

The standard founder vesting structure you'll see in term sheets:

**Duration:** 4 years
**Cliff:** 1 year
**Vesting after cliff:** Monthly (1/36th per month for remaining 36 months)

**What this means:**

| Time | Vested | Unvested (subject to buyback) |
|---|---|---|
| Month 6 | 0% | 100% |
| Month 11 | 0% | 100% |
| **Month 12 (cliff)** | **25%** | **75%** |
| Month 18 | 37.5% | 62.5% |
| Month 24 | 50% | 50% |
| Month 36 | 75% | 25% |
| **Month 48** | **100%** | **0%** |

**The cliff:** If you leave before 12 months, you vest 0% — the company can buy back 100% of your shares.
**After the cliff:** You vest 25% on month 12, then 1/36th more each month until month 48.

**Why a cliff?** It protects the company from a founder who joins, gets the valuation, and leaves after 3 months. The cliff ensures a minimum commitment period.

---

## Why Investors Require This (And Why It's Rational)

Put yourself in the investor's position.

You invest ₹5 crore at ₹25 crore post-money. 20% of the company. The founding team owns 60% — 30% Founder A, 30% Founder B.

Six months after closing, Founder A leaves. Takes a job at a competitor. Keeps all 30%.

You now have a company where:
- 30% is owned by a person who no longer works there
- The remaining founder owns 30% (now 50% of active equity)
- You own 20% (now 33% of active equity)
- The absent founder has no incentive to help and could become a competitor
- Any future investor will have serious concerns about the dead cap table

This scenario has played out in dozens of Indian startups. Investors have been burned. Vesting is the structural fix.

**For co-founders:** It's also protection against each other. If your co-founder leaves in year 1 and keeps 50% of the company, you're severely hampered in hiring, raising, and operating. Vesting protects you from that scenario.

## Good Leaver vs. Bad Leaver: The Most Important Distinction

Not all departures are the same. Most SHAs classify departing founders into two categories:

### Good Leaver

**Who qualifies:**
- Founder leaves due to death
- Founder leaves due to permanent disability
- Founder is terminated by the company without cause
- Founder leaves due to mutual agreement (negotiated exit)
- Sometimes: resignation after a specified period (often 2–3 years)

**Consequences:**
- Buyback applies only to unvested shares
- Buyback price is fair market value (as determined by independent valuation)
- Sometimes: vesting accelerates partially (e.g., 12 months acceleration)

### Bad Leaver

**Who qualifies:**
- Founder resigns voluntarily before vesting cliff or within first 2 years
- Founder is terminated for cause (fraud, material breach, gross misconduct)
- Founder competes with the company during the vesting period

**Consequences:**
- All or most shares are subject to buyback (not just unvested)
- Buyback price is original issue price (₹10 face value) or nominal amount
- Much more punitive

**The critical negotiation:** Make sure the definition of "bad leaver" is narrow and specific. "Terminated for cause" should be defined precisely — not just "in the board's reasonable opinion." Vague bad leaver definitions can be weaponised.

## Acceleration: Double-Trigger and Single-Trigger

Acceleration clauses allow vesting to speed up under certain conditions.

### Single-Trigger Acceleration

Vesting accelerates on a single event — typically an acquisition.

**Example:** 12 months of additional vesting acceleration on acquisition.

If you're 24 months into a 48-month schedule (50% vested), and the company is acquired, single-trigger acceleration gives you an additional 12 months of vesting — so you exit as 75% vested.

**Investor concern with single-trigger:** If a founder accelerates at acquisition but the acquirer wanted them to stay, the founder has less incentive to remain with the acquirer post-deal. This can kill acquisition deals.

### Double-Trigger Acceleration

Vesting accelerates only when TWO events happen together:
1. Acquisition (trigger 1)
2. Founder is terminated without cause or resigns for good reason within 12 months of acquisition (trigger 2)

**Why double-trigger is the standard:** The acquirer can't unilaterally fire founders to trigger vesting and take their shares — founders are protected. But the founder also doesn't get instant acceleration just because an acquisition happens.

**What to negotiate:** Double-trigger acceleration with 100% acceleration on trigger (full vesting upon acquisition + termination). This is the most standard, most founder-friendly, and most investor-acceptable structure.

## Credit for Time Already Worked

One of the most important founder vesting negotiations is getting credit for time already worked before the investment.

**The problem:**
You incorporate in January 2024. You work for 18 months before closing your Series A in July 2025. The investor wants 4-year vesting starting from the investment date.

Under this structure, after 4 years from investment (July 2029), you'll have vested fully — but you'll have worked for the company for 5.5 years, and you had 0% of your time credited.

**The fair structure:**
Credit you for time worked before investment. If you worked 18 months before closing, you should be 18 months into your 48-month vesting schedule at close.

**At close:** 18/48 = 37.5% already vested
**Full vesting:** 30 months from close (not 48)

Most investors will accept this. Some will push back and want to start the clock fresh. If they insist on no credit, negotiate: at minimum, get credit for the first 12 months (the cliff).

## Vesting in a Multi-Founder Setup

When you have 2–4 co-founders, vesting becomes more complex because:

1. Co-founders may have joined at different times
2. Co-founders may have different roles and time commitments
3. Co-founders should ideally all be on the same vesting schedule

**The most common problem:** Early-joining co-founder is 18 months into their journey. Late-joining co-founder just joined 3 months ago. Investor wants everyone on 4-year vesting from investment date. Early co-founder negotiates credit; late co-founder starts from scratch. This creates different vesting endpoints — which creates complexity at exit and can cause conflict.

**The clean structure:** Everyone on the same vesting schedule from the same effective date (either incorporation date or investment date). Credit time worked before investment uniformly.

## ESOP vs. Founder Vesting: The Key Difference

Founder vesting and ESOP (Employee Stock Option Plan) are both time-based equity mechanisms, but they're legally and structurally different:

| | Founder Vesting | ESOP |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Buyback right on existing shares | Options to purchase new shares |
| **Trigger** | Company exercises buyback right | Employee exercises option to buy |
| **Tax event** | On buyback (capital gains for founder) | On exercise (perquisite tax for employee) |
| **At exercise price** | Original issue price (usually ₹10) | Exercise price (set at grant — usually FMV or discount) |
| **Dilution** | No new shares (company buys back) | New shares issued |

The practical implication: when the company buys back unvested founder shares, the founder receives the buyback price (face value) and the shares return to the company. No new shares are issued.

---

## What Happens to Unvested Shares When Company Is Sold

This is where the good leaver / bad leaver analysis really matters.

**If founder is actively employed at acquisition:**
All shares (vested and unvested) participate in the exit proceeds, subject to the acceleration terms in the SHA.

**If founder left as good leaver before acquisition:**
Vested shares: participate in exit at full value
Unvested shares: have been bought back by company at nominal price — founder doesn't benefit from these

**If founder left as bad leaver:**
Most or all shares bought back at nominal price — founder receives minimal value regardless of exit outcome

**The financial reality:** On a ₹200 crore acquisition with a 30% founder stake:

| Scenario | Vested% | Unvested% | Founder Proceeds |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employed at exit | 100% | 0% | ₹60Cr |
| Left as good leaver (Month 24) | 50% | 50% | ₹30Cr |
| Left as bad leaver (Month 12) | 0% | 100% | ₹0 |

The difference between ₹60 crore and ₹0 is whether you're still employed at exit and whether you're classified as a good or bad leaver.

## Negotiating Founder Vesting: What to Push For

| Term | Standard Investor Ask | Founder Target | Acceptable Compromise |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vesting period | 4 years | 3 years | 4 years with accelerators |
| Cliff | 1 year | 6 months | 1 year |
| Credit for past service | None | Full credit | 12 months credit |
| Acceleration | None or single-trigger | Double-trigger 100% | Double-trigger 50–100% |
| Bad leaver trigger | Broad (board's discretion) | Narrow (specific misconduct only) | Defined categories with process |
| Buyback price (bad leaver) | Face value | FMV | Original issue price |

---

## The Co-Founder Departure Clause

SHAs should also address what happens when one co-founder wants to buy out another — separate from the investor-driven vesting mechanism.

**Common provision:** Right of first refusal among co-founders — if one wants to sell/transfer shares (including back to company), the other has the right to buy at the same price.

**What's often missing:** A clear valuation mechanism for co-founder buyouts. Without this, a co-founder departure becomes a litigation risk. Include a provision that any buyback price below a specified threshold is determined by an agreed-upon valuation method (DCF, third-party valuation, formula).

---

## Summary: The 5 Things to Negotiate in Founder Vesting

1. **Credit for time worked before investment** — get at least 12 months, ideally full credit
2. **Double-trigger acceleration** — 100% on acquisition + involuntary termination
3. **Good leaver at FMV, bad leaver narrowly defined** — ensure you can't be arbitrarily classified as bad leaver
4. **Cliff at 1 year max** — do not agree to 18-month or 2-year cliff
5. **Co-founder buyout valuation mechanism** — define it before there's a dispute

Founder vesting feels punitive when you first encounter it. It isn't — it's mutual protection. The goal is to make sure that if someone leaves early, everyone (remaining founders, investors, employees) isn't burdened with their dead equity.

Get the terms right, and vesting becomes just a standard part of your governance. Get them wrong, and they become the reason a future exit doesn't go the way you hoped.
