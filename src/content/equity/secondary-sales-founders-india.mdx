# Secondary Sales for Founders — How Indian Founders Take Money Off the Table

In 2021, during the peak of India's startup boom, a Bengaluru-based SaaS founder received a $12 million term sheet for his Series B. His startup was valued at $80 million. His personal equity stake, before dilution, was worth approximately $32 million on paper.

His savings account had ₹22 lakhs.

This is the reality of Indian founder wealth: enormous on paper, inaccessible in practice. Most Indian founders — even those running well-valued, well-funded startups — live on their salaries and have little liquid wealth. Every rupee of their "net worth" depends on a future exit event (acquisition or IPO) that may be 7–10 years away, or may never come.

Secondary sales are the mechanism that changes this equation. They let founders sell a portion of their existing shares to investors — converting paper wealth into real wealth — without issuing new shares, diluting the cap table, or sending any signal about the health of the business.

Understanding how secondaries work, when they are appropriate, and how to execute them in the Indian regulatory context is increasingly important knowledge for any founder building a company that will be in private markets for years.

## Primary vs. Secondary: The Fundamental Distinction

Before anything else, understand the structural difference between primary and secondary transactions.

**Primary transaction**: The company issues new shares. Investor money goes into the company's bank account. The company is diluted. Founders' percentage ownership decreases. Used for: fundraising for operations, growth, hiring.

**Secondary transaction**: An existing shareholder sells their existing shares to a buyer (new or existing investor). Investor money goes to the selling shareholder — the founder or early employee. The company's bank account is not affected. The cap table's total shares outstanding do not change. Dilution: zero.

```
Example: Secondary Sale Mechanics

Before secondary:
  Founder: 40% (4,000,000 shares)
  VC Fund A: 30% (3,000,000 shares)
  ESOP Pool: 15% (1,500,000 shares)
  Angel investors: 15% (1,500,000 shares)
  Total: 10,000,000 shares
  Valuation: ₹100 Crore
  Founder paper value: ₹40 Crore

Founder sells 5% of the company (500,000 shares) at ₹100/share
(implying ₹100 Crore company valuation)

After secondary:
  Founder: 35% (3,500,000 shares)
  VC Fund A: 35% (3,000,000 existing + 500,000 purchased)
  ESOP Pool: 15% (unchanged)
  Angel investors: 15% (unchanged)
  Total: 10,000,000 shares (unchanged — no new shares issued)

  Founder received: 500,000 × ₹100 = ₹5 Crore in cash
  Founder remaining paper value: ₹35 Crore
  Company valuation: ₹100 Crore (unchanged)
```

The secondary sale is purely a transfer of existing ownership. The company is unaffected; the cap table reflects a different shareholder composition, but total shares outstanding and the company's cash do not change.

## Why Secondary Sales Matter for Indian Founders Specifically

The case for founder secondaries is stronger in India than in the US or Europe for several specific reasons.

### The Illiquidity Premium of Indian Startup Equity

Indian startups take longer to exit than US startups. The median time from Series A to exit (acquisition or IPO) for Indian startups is 9–12 years, compared to 7–9 years for US startups. Indian public markets (BSE/NSE) have stricter listing criteria, and SEBI's SME listing requirements — while improving — are still less accessible than NASDAQ for growth-stage companies.

This means an Indian founder who incorporates at 28 may not have liquidity from their startup until they are 40. A secondary sale at Series B or C — when the company is demonstrably building toward an exit — lets a founder access meaningful cash while still in their 30s, when the financial impact is higher than it will be at 45.

### Founder Concentration Risk

The typical Indian tech founder at Series B has 70–90% of their net worth in a single illiquid asset: their startup equity. In finance terms, this is catastrophic concentration. No financial advisor would recommend holding more than 20–30% of wealth in a single illiquid asset.

Secondary sales are, in part, a risk management tool. By converting some equity to cash, founders:
- Reduce catastrophic downside exposure (if the company fails)
- Establish a financial cushion that removes personal financial pressure from startup decisions
- Improve decision-making by removing the "I can't afford to be wrong" dynamic from high-stakes choices

### Salary Dynamics: Indian Founders Are Often Underpaid Relative to Their Opportunity Cost

Many Indian startup founders — particularly those coming from IIT/IIM backgrounds — are earning ₹20–40 LPA in founder salary when they could earn ₹50–100 LPA in a senior role at a MNC or large tech company. Over a 7–8 year building period, this represents a significant opportunity cost.

A secondary sale of ₹5–10 Crore at Series B compensates for this opportunity cost and removes the "financial pressure" dimension from founding. Founders who are financially stressed make worse decisions than founders who are financially secure.

## When Are Secondary Sales Permitted?

This is where most founders get confused. The answer depends on your Shareholders' Agreement.

### The Role of the SHA in Secondary Sales

Almost all Indian startup Shareholder Agreements (SHAs) contain clauses that govern when and how shares can be transferred. The key provisions:

**Right of First Refusal (ROFR)**: Before selling shares to a third party, the selling shareholder must offer the shares to existing shareholders at the same price. If existing shareholders decline to purchase within the notice period (typically 15–30 days), the seller can proceed with the third-party sale.

**Tag Along Rights**: If a founder sells shares, other shareholders (typically investors) have the right to sell a proportionate number of their shares to the same buyer at the same price.

**Drag Along Rights**: If investors want to sell their shares as part of a full exit, they can require founders to sell their shares too. This is not directly relevant to partial founder secondaries but matters for exit planning.

**Board/Investor Approval**: Many SHAs require board approval for any share transfer above a certain size, or any transfer to a third party. If your SHA requires board approval, you need to plan for this in your secondary sale timeline.

**Lock-In Periods**: Some SHAs (particularly for founders) include lock-in periods after a fundraising round — commonly 12–24 months after each round — during which founders cannot sell shares. Always check your specific SHA.

**Key question for founders**: Read your SHA before assuming you can execute a secondary. The typical process:
1. Review SHA for transfer restrictions and ROFR provisions
2. If board/investor approval required, initiate that process early
3. Serve ROFR notices to all relevant shareholders per the SHA
4. If ROFR not exercised, proceed with third-party buyer

## How Secondaries Are Typically Structured in Indian Funding Rounds

The most common way Indian founders access secondary liquidity is by negotiating a secondary component into a primary fundraising round. This is the cleanest, most investor-accepted route.

### The Series B / C Secondary Component Model

The typical structure:

| Round | Primary Component | Founder Secondary Component | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seed | ₹2–5 Crore | Rare / not standard | Too early; investors want all capital going to company |
| Series A | ₹10–50 Crore | ₹0–3 Crore | Possible if strong metrics; often just 10–15% of round |
| Series B | ₹50–200 Crore | ₹5–20 Crore | Standard practice; 10–20% of round typical |
| Series C+ | ₹200Cr+ | ₹20–75 Crore | Larger secondaries acceptable; precedent well-established |

**How to negotiate the secondary component:**

The framing matters enormously. Do not say "I need to take some money out." Say "As part of structuring this round, I would like to include a secondary component of X%. This will allow me to focus entirely on building the company without personal financial distraction, which aligns with your interest in a focused, committed founder."

Investors have evolved significantly on this. In 2015–2018, founder secondaries were viewed with suspicion (why is the founder selling?). In 2023–2026, sophisticated investors recognize that founder liquidity events improve long-term commitment and reduce the risk of founders making desperate decisions (accepting bad M&A offers because they need liquidity) later.

**The pricing convention:**
Founder secondary shares are typically sold at the same price as the primary round, or occasionally at a 5–10% discount to primary. The buyer is usually the incoming lead investor (who wants a larger stake), sometimes an existing investor who wants to maintain their percentage.

### Late-Stage Secondary: Dedicated Secondary Funds

At Series C and beyond, dedicated secondary buyers enter the picture:
- **Temasek / GIC** (Singapore sovereign funds with secondary programs)
- **Steadview Capital** (active in Indian startup secondaries)
- **Kedaara Capital** (PE with secondary capabilities)
- **International secondary funds**: Coller Capital, Lexington Partners — less active in India but present at large deal sizes

Dedicated secondary buyers are willing to purchase founder/early employee shares outside of a primary fundraising round — a "pure secondary" transaction. These typically require the company to facilitate the process and all SHA approvals to be obtained, but the company itself is not raising capital.

## FEMA Compliance: The Regulatory Layer That Most Founders Miss

Secondary sales involving foreign investors — which is most Indian startup secondary sales, given that most Series A+ investors are foreign-origin funds — have mandatory FEMA compliance requirements.

### The Critical Distinction: Who is Buying, Who is Selling

| Transaction Type | FEMA Filing Required | Timeline | Filing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indian founder sells to Indian buyer | No FEMA filing | N/A | N/A |
| Indian founder sells to Foreign investor | FC-TRS with RBI | Within 60 days | Form FC-TRS |
| Foreign investor sells to Indian buyer | FC-TRS with RBI | Within 60 days | Form FC-TRS |
| Foreign investor sells to another Foreign investor | CS-01 (ODI) potentially | Case-specific | Advice of specialist |

**Form FC-TRS** (Transfer of Shares) is filed through RBI's FIRMS portal. The AD bank (Authorised Dealer bank — typically the company's banker) handles this filing on behalf of the transacting parties.

**Pricing requirements**: FEMA has pricing guidelines for share transfers involving foreign investment. The transfer price must be at or above Fair Market Value (FMV) as determined by a SEBI-registered merchant banker or chartered accountant using a recognized valuation methodology (DCF or Comparable Company method).

This means: if you want to sell founder shares to a foreign investor at a price below the primary round price (common in distressed secondaries), you need a formal FMV valuation report confirming that the discounted price is at or above FMV.

### The Practical FC-TRS Process

```
FC-TRS Process for Founder Secondary:

Step 1: Agree on price and terms with buyer
Step 2: Get board/investor approval per SHA
Step 3: Serve and clear ROFR notices per SHA
Step 4: Execute Share Transfer Agreement (STA)
Step 5: Update share register
Step 6: Physical/dematerialized share certificate transfer
Step 7: Update MCA (SH-4 form for share transfer recording)
Step 8: AD bank files FC-TRS with RBI FIRMS portal within 60 days
        of receipt of consideration

Documents required for FC-TRS:
- Board resolution approving transfer
- Copy of Share Transfer Agreement
- FMV valuation report (for India-foreign transfers)
- KYC documents of buyer and seller
- Proof of consideration receipt (bank statement)
```

Failure to file FC-TRS within 60 days attracts a compounding penalty of ₹5,000 per day from RBI. For a secondary that closes in December and is discovered in the following year's compliance audit, this can exceed ₹5 Lakh easily.

## Tax Treatment of Secondary Sales for Indian Founders

This is often the part that surprises founders most. Secondary sales can have significant personal tax implications.

### Capital Gains Classification

For shares in an unlisted Indian private limited company:

| Holding Period | Classification | Tax Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Less than 24 months | Short-Term Capital Gain (STCG) | Added to income and taxed at slab rate (up to 30% + surcharge) |
| 24 months or more | Long-Term Capital Gain (LTCG) | 20% with indexation benefit |

**The 24-month threshold** is measured from the date of original share allotment to the date of transfer. For founders who have held shares since inception, most secondary sales will qualify as LTCG.

**Indexation benefit**: For LTCG on unlisted company shares, founders can apply the Cost Inflation Index (CII) to their cost of acquisition, reducing the effective taxable gain.

```
Indexation Example:

Founder originally purchased/allotted shares at ₹1/share in April 2020
Selling shares in March 2026 at ₹800/share
Shares sold: 5,00,000 shares
Sale consideration: ₹40 Crore

CII for FY 2020-21: 301
CII for FY 2025-26: 363 (approximate)

Indexed cost = Original cost × (CII at sale / CII at purchase)
             = ₹1 × (363 / 301)
             = ₹1.21 per share (approximately)

Taxable LTCG = Sale price - Indexed cost
             = ₹800 - ₹1.21 = ₹798.79 per share

Total taxable LTCG = ₹798.79 × 5,00,000 = ₹39.94 Crore

Tax at 20% (LTCG) = ₹7.99 Crore
Plus surcharge (if applicable at this income level): ~₹1.2 Crore
Total tax: ~₹9.2 Crore
Net founder proceeds: ~₹30.8 Crore
```

Note: If the same shares had been held for less than 24 months, the gain would be taxed at the founder's marginal rate (30% + surcharge = approximately 39%), resulting in approximately ₹3.5 Crore additional tax on the same transaction.

### Angel Tax and Secondary Sales

The Angel Tax provisions (Section 56(2)(viib) of the Income Tax Act) apply to *primary* share issuances — where the company issues shares at a premium above FMV, the excess is taxed as income in the company's hands. Secondary sales are not subject to Angel Tax because no new shares are being issued. The company is not a party to the secondary transaction.

### TDS on Secondary Sales Involving Foreign Buyers

When a foreign investor (or any buyer purchasing from an Indian resident) purchases shares in an unlisted Indian company, TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) provisions may apply. Under Section 194-IA (as amended), TDS at 1% applies on property transfers above ₹50 Lakh — but this provision covers immovable property, not shares.

For share transfers, if the seller is an Indian resident, the buyer does not need to deduct TDS on the purchase price. However, the seller must include the capital gains in their income tax return for the applicable year.

If the seller is a Non-Resident Indian (NRI), Section 195 TDS provisions apply, and the buyer must deduct TDS at the applicable rate on the capital gains component.

## How to Negotiate a Secondary in Your Fundraising Round

Execution matters as much as strategy. Here is the negotiation approach that works.

### Timing: Raise It Late in the Negotiation

Do not lead with the secondary. Build the primary round negotiation around the company's growth story, metrics, and the investment thesis. Once you have agreement on valuation and primary deal terms, introduce the secondary.

"As we finalize the round structure, I wanted to discuss including a secondary component. I'm looking to sell X% of my stake. Happy to walk you through the rationale."

### Sizing: Stay Below 15% of the Round

A secondary component above 15–20% of the total round capital starts to raise questions about founder conviction. The implicit message of a large secondary is "I'm not as sure about this as I'm saying I am."

Sizing secondary at 10–15% of the round (e.g., ₹5 Crore secondary in a ₹50 Crore round) is the sweet spot where investors don't feel the secondary dominates the conversation.

### Framing: Diversification and Stability, Not Exit

The framing that resonates with investors: "I have 85% of my net worth in this company. Selling a small portion converts that into financial stability for my family, which means I'm making decisions for the company's long-term health rather than personal financial pressure. This aligns with your interest in a focused, committed founder."

The framing to avoid: "I've worked hard and deserve some liquidity." This is true and valid, but it frames the secondary as personal reward rather than strategic alignment.

### Who Buys the Secondary?

Common buyers in Indian fundraising secondaries:

1. **The incoming lead investor**: Wants a larger stake; buying secondary achieves this without additional dilution on the primary
2. **Existing investors**: May exercise ROFR or choose to increase their stake
3. **Secondary-specific funds**: Steadview, A91, or international secondary funds who participate alongside primary rounds

## The ESOP Secondary Market: Liquidity for Early Employees

Founder secondaries are well understood. What is less discussed but increasingly important: secondary sales for early employees whose ESOPs have vested.

### The Employee ESOP Liquidity Problem

An engineer who joined an Indian startup in 2018 at employee #15, with 0.5% ESOP grant on 4-year vesting, has fully vested shares. If the company is valued at ₹500 Crore in 2026, those shares are worth ₹2.5 Crore on paper. But to realize that value, the employee must either wait for an IPO/acquisition (another 5–7 years potentially), or find a way to sell.

Most Indian ESOPs are structured as stock options (the right to buy shares at exercise price) rather than restricted stock. An employee must first exercise (pay the exercise price, plus tax) before holding actual shares. The exercise-plus-hold situation creates a cash outlay plus tax liability for an asset that remains illiquid.

### Platforms Facilitating Employee ESOP Liquidity in India

| Platform | Model | Stage |
|---|---|---|
| Trica (formerly tyke) | Secondary marketplace for startup shares + ESOPs | Active; facilitating employee secondary transactions |
| ESOPGuru | ESOP structuring + secondary facilitation | Advisory + platform |
| iSPIRT ESOP Policy Framework | Industry initiative for ESOP standardization | Policy/advocacy |
| Liquidstack | Secondary transactions platform | India-focused |
| Quid (backed by IndigoEdge) | Employee liquidity programs | Emerging |

### How Employee ESOP Secondaries Work

The process for an employee to sell vested ESOPs:

```
Step 1: Employee exercises options (pays exercise price, triggers tax event)
Step 2: Employee now holds actual shares
Step 3: Company or secondary platform finds buyer willing to purchase shares
Step 4: SHA ROFR process: offer to existing shareholders first
Step 5: If ROFR not exercised, sell to external buyer
Step 6: Employee pays capital gains tax on gain from exercise price to sale price
```

Alternatively, some companies are building "tender offer" programs where the company buys back employee shares at set prices during a defined window — eliminating the employee's need to find their own buyer.

### Tax for Employee ESOP Secondaries

Two tax events for employees selling ESOPs:

**Event 1 (Exercise)**: The difference between FMV at exercise and exercise price is treated as perquisite income — taxed as salary at the employee's marginal rate. This is the tax event that most employees find painful: they "make" ₹20 Lakh in income on paper the moment they exercise, before they've received any cash.

**Event 2 (Sale)**: The difference between sale price and FMV at exercise is capital gain. If held for 24+ months after exercise, LTCG at 20% applies. If held for less than 24 months, STCG at slab rate.

DPIIT-recognized startups can defer the Exercise Tax event (Event 1) to the earlier of: 5 years from the date of exercise, the date of sale, or the date the employee leaves the company. This deferral is a significant benefit for employees of DPIIT-recognized startups — they don't face a tax liability they cannot pay in cash at the time of exercise.

## The Secondary Sale Red Lines: When It Doesn't Work

Not all secondary sale attempts succeed. Here are the situations where secondaries fail:

**1. Company is in distress or underperforming**: Investors will not agree to a secondary component if the company is missing targets. A secondary in a down round (lower valuation than previous round) is extremely difficult to negotiate.

**2. SHA prohibits or heavily restricts transfers**: Some SHA provisions include near-absolute restrictions on founder share transfers until a specific milestone (IPO filing, 5+ years, etc.). Review carefully before investing time in secondary negotiations.

**3. Secondary would trigger material ownership changes**: If selling X% of founder shares would drop a founder below a threshold that triggers vesting acceleration provisions, put-option triggers, or other SHA provisions, legal complexity can kill the transaction.

**4. Foreign investment valuation discrepancy**: If the secondary price does not meet FEMA's FMV threshold (must be at or above FMV for inbound foreign investment buying from Indian residents), the transaction cannot close without a downward FMV revaluation, which creates its own complications.

**5. Due diligence reveals compliance gaps**: As with primary rounds, secondary buyers do their own diligence. A founder with unresolved ROC compliance issues, a deactivated DIN, or FEMA non-compliance on prior share allotments may find the secondary held up until compliance is resolved.

## Summary: The Secondary Sale Decision Framework for Indian Founders

```
Should you pursue a secondary in your next round?

  Is your company at Series B or later?
    YES → Secondaries are standard; pursue with appropriate sizing
    NO (Series A) → Possible but less common; sizing must be small

  Do you have >= 70% of net worth in startup equity?
    YES → Strong case for liquidity; the concentration risk is real
    NO → Evaluate based on opportunity cost, not concentration

  Is the company on a strong growth trajectory?
    YES → Investor receptiveness to secondary is high
    NO → Don't push for secondary in a challenging fundraise; focus on primary

  What size secondary makes sense?
    Formula: min(10-15% of round, 15-20% of your total equity)
    Keep personal holding above 25% post-secondary to maintain credibility

  Have you reviewed your SHA for transfer restrictions?
    YES, cleared → Proceed
    NO → Review before engaging investors on secondary
```
