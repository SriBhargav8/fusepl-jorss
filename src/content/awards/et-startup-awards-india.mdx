# ET Startup Awards — How to Win India's Most Prestigious Startup Recognition

There are hundreds of startup awards in India. Most produce a trophy and a LinkedIn post.

The ET Startup Awards produce something different: a front-page feature in the most-read business daily in India, access to the Economic Times editorial network, and a stage appearance that puts you in front of every CXO and investor who matters in the Indian ecosystem.

Since the award's inception in 2015, winning the ET Startup Awards has been a reliable signal of startup credibility — not because the award creates great companies, but because the award committee has consistently identified great companies before the broader market did.

This guide covers how the ET Startup Awards actually work, what the committee evaluates, what past winners did right, and how to prepare an application that gives you a real shot at winning.

---

## A Brief History of the ET Startup Awards

The Economic Times launched the Startup Awards in 2015, during the first major wave of Indian startup funding that produced companies like Zomato, Ola, Paytm, and Snapdeal. The timing mattered — ET recognised the startup ecosystem as a major newsroom beat and created an award programme that served both editorial and ecosystem purposes.

Since 2015, the awards have consistently identified companies at inflection points. Past categories of winners have included companies that went on to become unicorns, complete successful IPOs, or receive significant international recognition.

The award is annual. It is managed by the ET Bureau's startup vertical — the same journalists and editors who run ETtech, ET's dedicated technology and startup publication.

## The 7 Categories of the ET Startup Awards

Understanding which category you belong to is the first step in building a compelling application. Each category has distinct evaluation criteria and different benchmark expectations.

### 1. Startup of the Year

The flagship award. This goes to a company that demonstrates the strongest combination of growth, innovation, team, and impact over the past 12 months. The winner is typically at Series B or later — there is no explicit stage requirement, but the committee's bar for this award assumes meaningful scale.

**What evaluators look for:** ₹50 crore+ ARR or ₹200 crore+ GMV (unofficial threshold based on past winners), strong YoY growth (minimum 80–100% for serious consideration), clear market leadership or a credible path to it, and a founder who can articulate vision at scale.

**Past winner profile:** Companies typically shortlisted here are Series B–D, have raised ₹100 crore+, and are operating in large markets (edtech, fintech, healthtech, SaaS, D2C).

### 2. Bootstrap Champ

For companies that have reached significant scale without institutional venture funding. This is not just for companies that raised zero money — companies with angel funding or small seed rounds (under ₹2–3 crore) that are now profitable and growing are eligible.

**What evaluators look for:** Profitability or clear path to it, revenue growth from customer-funded operations, unit economics discipline, evidence that the business works without continued external capital. The narrative here is efficiency and resilience.

**Past winner profile:** B2B SaaS companies with ₹15–50 crore ARR, profitable since Year 2 or 3, bootstrapped by founders from their own savings and early customer revenue. Also direct-to-consumer brands that grew through organic channels.

**Why this category matters for more founders:** Bootstrapped founders are systematically under-recognised in Indian startup media, which gravitates toward funding announcements. The Bootstrap Champ category is one of the few India-wide recognitions designed specifically for them.

### 3. Best on Campus

For startups founded and led by current college students or recent graduates (within 2 years of graduation). This is not just about being young — it's about building a real company while navigating the constraints of campus life.

**What evaluators look for:** Demonstrable traction (revenue, users, pilot customers) despite the founder being in college, clarity of vision and market understanding unusual for the founder's age, early team quality.

**Why this category matters:** India produces over 1.5 million engineering graduates and 400,000 MBA graduates annually. The Best on Campus award is one of the few that specifically celebrates founders who started while still at college rather than waiting until after graduation.

### 4. Comeback Kid

For startups or founders who faced a major setback — near-death experience, pivot, market collapse, regulatory challenge — and recovered to build a stronger company. This is a human story award as much as a business award.

**What evaluators look for:** The authenticity and severity of the setback, what specifically changed (strategy, team, product, market focus), evidence that the recovery is real and sustainable (not just a narrative of resilience without improved metrics).

**Why this category matters:** Indian startup culture has historically been poor at celebrating failure and recovery. The Comeback Kid award is one of the few that explicitly values the journey through adversity rather than only the endpoint.

### 5. Most Innovative Startup

For companies whose primary differentiation is technological or business model innovation — not just growth. A company can have mediocre revenue but genuinely novel technology and qualify here.

**What evaluators look for:** IP position (patents filed or granted), proprietary technology stack, novelty of approach (has anyone done this before in India or globally?), expert testimony from domain specialists, early signals of defensibility.

**Why this category matters:** It's one of the few ET award categories that rewards innovation over scale, which matters for deep tech, biotech, climate tech, and hardware startups that are pre-revenue or very early revenue.

### 6. Social Enterprise of the Year

For startups that are building profitable or sustainable businesses with an explicit and measurable social mission. This is not for NGOs or CSR projects — it's for commercially viable companies whose business model delivers social impact as a core output, not a side effect.

**What evaluators look for:** Clarity and measurability of impact (number of beneficiaries, change in measurable outcome — income, health, education level, access), business model viability (not dependent on grants or subsidies unless that is the business model), evidence that social impact and commercial success are aligned rather than in tension.

**Past winner profile:** Rural fintech companies serving farmers and unbanked populations, affordable healthcare startups, skilling and livelihood platforms, clean energy access companies.

### 7. Women Ahead

For women founders who are building companies with strong traction and growth. The company does not have to serve women as customers — the award recognises the founder.

**What evaluators look for:** Same metrics as Startup of the Year but with the added dimension of how the founder has navigated gender-specific challenges in the Indian startup ecosystem (fundraising bias, hiring biases, access to networks). The committee values both the business metrics and the founder story.

**Why this category matters:** Women founders receive approximately 2–3% of total VC funding in India. The Women Ahead award is one of India's most visible recognitions designed to address this visibility gap.

## The ET Startup Awards Application Process

### Step 1: Nomination

The ET Startup Awards accepts:
- **Self-nominations:** Founders can submit their own companies directly through the ET award portal
- **Peer nominations:** Investors, co-founders, or industry leaders can nominate a company
- **ET editorial team identification:** The ETtech editorial team proactively reaches out to companies they have covered or identified as strong candidates

The portal is typically open for 6–8 weeks in the March–April window.

### Step 2: Initial Screening

The ET editorial team conducts a first-level screen to confirm:
- Company is India-founded and primarily India-operating
- Company meets basic scale thresholds for the relevant category
- The nomination is complete with required documentation

Companies that pass initial screening are moved to the committee review phase.

### Step 3: Committee Review

This is where the real evaluation happens. The ET Startup Awards committee typically includes:
- 2–3 senior ET journalists and editors
- 2–3 VC/PE fund partners or managing directors
- 1–2 domain experts (varies by year — may include a startup founder, corporate executive, or policy expert)

The committee reviews written applications, financial documentation, and in some years conducts brief calls or interviews with shortlisted companies.

### Step 4: Shortlist Announcement

Typically 4–6 weeks before the award event, ET announces the shortlist across categories. This is itself a significant PR event for shortlisted companies.

### Step 5: Winners and Event

Winners are announced at the ET Startup Awards event — historically held in Bengaluru or Mumbai, typically in August–September. The event is attended by 300–500 ecosystem participants including investors, founders, and corporate leaders.

## What ET's Editorial Committee Actually Evaluates

Based on past winners, shortlists, and publicly available information about the evaluation process, the committee focuses on six areas:

### Revenue Growth Trajectory (Not Absolute Revenue)

The committee is more interested in your growth rate than your absolute revenue. A ₹10 crore ARR company growing 200% YoY is a more interesting story than a ₹50 crore ARR company growing 15% YoY.

Prepare to show:
- Revenue for FY2023, FY2024, FY2025 (3-year trend)
- Growth rate calculation (CAGR and YoY)
- Forward projection with assumptions

For GMV businesses (e-commerce, marketplace, aggregators): show both gross and net revenue, and explain the GMV-to-net-revenue ratio.

### Team Quality and Composition

The committee evaluates founder credibility (relevant domain experience, prior track record), senior team depth (is there a leadership team or just the founder?), and advisor quality.

Be specific about why your team is the right team to solve this particular problem. Generic claims like "experienced team with 20 years combined experience" are weak. Specific claims like "our CTO led payments infrastructure at Razorpay for 4 years before this" are strong.

### Innovation Claim

What is genuinely new about what you're doing? The committee pushes on this. In an ecosystem where many companies are incremental improvements on existing models, true innovation stands out.

Be honest if your differentiation is execution rather than technology — execution-based differentiation is valid and should be framed as such rather than overclaiming technical novelty.

### Social Impact and India Relevance

Even for commercial categories like Startup of the Year, the committee gravitates toward companies whose success creates broader social value — employment, financial inclusion, healthcare access, education access. Indian awards consistently favour companies that tie commercial success to India's development story.

### Media Coverage to Date

The ET committee knows the ecosystem well. Companies that have received thoughtful coverage (not just funding announcements) in ET, Inc42, Entrackr, Mint, and similar publications are more legible to the committee than companies that have deliberately stayed low-profile. This is not a requirement, but it helps.

### Financial Documentation

Audited financials or CA-certified management accounts are expected for any serious application. The committee cannot evaluate a company's financial health based on an Excel sheet provided by the founder.

## How to Prepare a Winning ET Application

### The 90-Day Preparation Timeline

**90 days before applications open (December–January):**
- Commission or update your CA-certified management accounts
- Compile your key metrics dashboard (ARR, MAU/DAU, LTV, CAC, churn, NPS, team size, geographic reach)
- Build your impact case — quantify the social or economic value your company has created
- Identify 3–5 customer references willing to speak on your behalf

**60 days before applications open (January–February):**
- Draft your narrative (see framework below)
- Identify which category you're applying to and study the last 3 years of winners in that category
- Gather media coverage archive (screenshots, PDFs, links)
- Get advisor letters if applicable

**30 days before applications open (February–March):**
- Complete a dry-run of the application form with internal team feedback
- Have someone outside your company read your narrative and identify weak claims
- Prepare supporting documents (product screenshots, customer testimonials, team org chart)

**When applications open (March–April):**
- Submit early — do not wait until the last day
- Follow up on any committee queries within 24 hours

### The Winning Application Narrative Structure

The ET Startup Awards application typically has 8–12 question fields. Structure your answers as a coherent narrative rather than standalone responses.

**Open with your differentiated claim (first answer):**
State your one-line insight that explains why your company could not have existed 3 years ago and why it matters in India today. This is your innovation thesis.

**Follow with your evidence (middle answers):**
Layer in your metrics, customer stories, and team credentials. Let the evidence prove the claim you made at the start.

**Close with your impact and trajectory (final answers):**
Show where you're going. The committee is not just evaluating what you've built — they're evaluating whether this company is going somewhere. A clear 3-year vision with specific milestones is more compelling than a generic "we plan to expand" statement.

## Common Application Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Applying to the wrong category**
Many strong companies apply for "Startup of the Year" when they would be more competitive in "Bootstrap Champ" or "Most Innovative Startup." Study which category your company's strengths align with, not which category has the most prestige.

**Mistake 2: Revenue without context**
Stating "₹25 crore ARR" without context is weak. "₹25 crore ARR, up from ₹8 crore two years ago, with 85% gross margins and 120% net revenue retention from our top 50 enterprise clients" is strong.

**Mistake 3: Generic innovation claims**
"We use AI and machine learning to solve the problem" is not an innovation claim. What specifically does your AI do that could not be done before? What data advantage do you have? What proprietary training data or model architecture makes your product defensible?

**Mistake 4: Not addressing the India angle**
The ET Startup Awards is explicitly for Indian companies solving Indian problems. If your company's success story is disconnected from India's specific context, you're leaving points on the table. Tie your story to India's demographic realities, infrastructure trajectory, regulatory environment, or cultural context.

**Mistake 5: Weak team section**
Founders underestimate how much weight the committee places on team. A paragraph of generic team bios is not enough. Show why this specific team — with their specific backgrounds, domain experience, and prior track record — is uniquely positioned to win this specific market.

**Mistake 6: No supporting documentation**
Applications without audited financials or CA-certified accounts, without media coverage links, without customer testimonials, are at a structural disadvantage. The documentation is the evidence. Without it, your narrative has no proof.

## Why Being Shortlisted is Valuable Even Without Winning

Most founders who don't win the ET Startup Awards treat the experience as a near-miss. This is a mistake.

**Being shortlisted means:**

1. **The ET editorial team has read your company's story.** ETtech reporters cover startup beats for years. Having your company in their minds — even without a win — increases the probability of future coverage, interview requests, and mentions in ecosystem roundups.

2. **You have a PR story to tell.** "Shortlisted for ET Startup Awards 2026" is a legitimate announcement. A significant percentage of shortlisted startups don't publicise this — which means the ones that do stand out. Write a LinkedIn post, send a press release to 3–4 journalists, and mention it in your investor updates.

3. **You have committee feedback.** In some years, committee members provide informal feedback to shortlisted companies. If you can get this feedback, it is some of the most valuable outside perspective available to an early-stage founder — senior investors and experienced editors telling you what's missing from your story.

4. **You are now part of the ET Awards alumni community.** Shortlisted and winning companies appear in ET's awards archive, which serves as a reference point for journalists writing about the ecosystem, investors doing background research, and corporate partners evaluating startup collaborators.

## Past Winners and What Happened Next

A consistent pattern across ET Startup Awards winners: the award accelerates a trajectory rather than creating one. Companies that were already on strong growth curves used the ET recognition to compress timelines — closing fundraising rounds faster, onboarding enterprise customers who cited the award as a trust signal, and attracting senior talent who wanted to join an award-winning company.

In several documented cases, ET Startup Awards shortlisting led directly to:
- Inbound term sheets from VCs who had not previously been in conversation with the company
- Media requests from international publications covering the Indian startup ecosystem
- Enterprise RFP invitations from corporate procurement teams

The companies that saw the most downstream value were those that proactively leveraged the recognition — not those who won and moved on.

## Is the ET Startup Awards Process Right for Your Stage?

| Stage | ET Award Value | Recommended Category |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| Pre-revenue / Pre-product | Low — wait | Not recommended |
| Early revenue (₹1–10 crore ARR) | Moderate — Best on Campus, Most Innovative | Best on Campus (if eligible), Most Innovative |
| Growth (₹10–50 crore ARR) | High — strong candidate | Bootstrap Champ (if bootstrapped), Social Enterprise |
| Scale (₹50 crore+ ARR) | Very high — prime candidate | Startup of the Year, Bootstrap Champ |
| Turnaround story | High regardless of revenue | Comeback Kid |

If you're pre-revenue, the ET Startup Awards is not the right use of your limited time. Focus on getting to initial traction, then apply.

If you're past ₹10 crore ARR with strong growth and a compelling story, the ET Startup Awards should be on your calendar every year.
