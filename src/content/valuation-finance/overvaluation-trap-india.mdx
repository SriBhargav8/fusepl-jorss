## The Day ₹80 Crore Became the Worst Thing That Happened to Them

In early 2022, a Delhi-based D2C personal care brand raised ₹10 crore at an ₹80 crore post-money valuation. The founders celebrated. Their investors celebrated. The press ran a piece on the "booming Indian D2C ecosystem."

By late 2024, that company was trying to raise a bridge round — and failing. The problem was not product-market fit. The problem was not the team. The problem was that their last round valued them at ₹80 crore. Any new investor willing to write a cheque was offering ₹40–50 crore — a down round. And once you accept a down round, the legal and economic consequences cascade in ways most founders never anticipate.

The founders were trapped. Not by the market. By their own valuation.

This is the overvaluation trap — and it claimed dozens of Indian startups in the 2022–2025 correction. Understanding the mechanics of why high valuations are dangerous is one of the most important pieces of financial literacy a founder can develop. This article gives you the complete picture: the math, the mechanics, the real examples, and the framework for calculating a "safe" valuation ceiling before you sign your next term sheet.

---

## Why Founders Accept Overvalued Rounds

The incentive to accept a high valuation is obvious and powerful. A high valuation means:

1. Less dilution for the same capital raised
2. A higher headline number for press coverage and talent recruitment
3. A better-looking cap table for future investor conversations
4. Founder ego satisfaction — valuation as scoreboard

All of these are real. But they are short-term benefits that create long-term structural problems. Let's look at what actually happens.

## The Mechanics of the Overvaluation Trap

The overvaluation trap works as a self-reinforcing loop:

```
High valuation at Round N
→ Must achieve higher valuation at Round N+1 to avoid down round
→ Requires exceptional growth to justify higher valuation
→ Smaller investor pool willing to invest at that level
→ Higher probability of bridge round, down round, or failure
→ Down round triggers anti-dilution clauses
→ Founder equity gets crushed
→ Founder motivation drops
→ Company performance drops further
```

Each step in this chain is a real mechanism, not a theoretical concern. Let's work through each one.

### Step 1: The Valuation Ratchet

Every time you raise at a higher valuation, you set a floor for the next conversation. Investors in future rounds know your previous post-money. They will not easily accept being in at a lower valuation than the previous round — it signals to the market that something is wrong.

The 2021 venture bubble in India created thousands of companies that raised at 30–50x ARR multiples. By 2023, the market had repriced to 5–10x ARR for most sectors. Companies that raised at ₹200 crore on ₹5 crore ARR (40x) were now in a world where comparable companies raised at ₹30–50 crore on ₹5 crore ARR (6–10x).

To avoid a down round, these founders needed to grow ARR to ₹25–40 crore — a 5–8x increase — before going back to market. Most could not.

### Step 2: The Smaller Investor Pool Problem

At every valuation level, the number of investors willing and able to invest at that valuation decreases.

| Valuation Range | Investor Category | Approximate Pool Size (India) |
|---|---|---|
| ₹5–20 Cr | Angels, micro VCs, family offices | 500–1,000+ |
| ₹20–100 Cr | Seed/pre-Series A funds, early-stage VCs | 100–200 |
| ₹100–500 Cr | Series A VCs | 30–50 active |
| ₹500 Cr–2,000 Cr | Series B/growth funds | 10–20 |
| ₹2,000 Cr+ | Late-stage, crossover funds | 5–10 |

When you raise at ₹150 crore post-money at seed stage — which happened multiple times in 2021 — you have eliminated most of the investor pool who can participate in your next round. The next investor writing a cheque must value you at ₹200 crore+, and there are only 30–50 funds in India that operate at that level. If those funds don't like you, you have nowhere to go.

### Step 3: The Anti-Dilution Trigger

When a down round does occur, anti-dilution provisions kick in. Most Indian venture deals include anti-dilution protection. There are two common types:

**Broad-Based Weighted Average (BBWA):** The most common, most founder-friendly form. The conversion price of preferred shares is adjusted based on a formula that accounts for the new shares issued. The adjustment is weighted — a small down round doesn't crush founders as badly.

**Full Ratchet:** The most investor-friendly and founder-hostile form. If you have any down round, the conversion price of previous investors resets to the new, lower price. This can be catastrophic.

**The Math of Full Ratchet Anti-Dilution:**

Suppose an investor put in ₹5 crore at ₹50 crore post-money (10% stake, 10 lakh shares at ₹50/share). Now you raise a down round at ₹30 crore post-money.

With full ratchet: the investor's conversion price resets to ₹30/share. To maintain the same economic value (₹5 crore invested), they now get 16.67 lakh shares instead of 10 lakh. Those extra 6.67 lakh shares come from somewhere — they come from the founders and ESOP pool.

```
Original cap table (simplified):
- Founders: 80 lakh shares (80%)
- Investor A (Series Seed): 10 lakh shares (10%) — ₹5Cr at ₹50/share
- ESOP: 10 lakh shares (10%)
Total: 100 lakh shares

After down round with full ratchet:
- Investor A gets 6.67 lakh additional shares (free)
- New investor B: ₹5Cr at ₹30Cr post-money = 16.67 lakh new shares
- Founders: 80 lakh shares but total pool is now 123.34 lakh shares

New ownership:
- Founders: 80/123.34 = 64.9%
- Investor A: 16.67/123.34 = 13.5% (same money, more equity)
- Investor B: 16.67/123.34 = 13.5%
- ESOP: 10/123.34 = 8.1%
```

The founders lost ~15% of the company to the anti-dilution mechanism alone — without any new capital coming in.

**The BBWA Math (Less Severe, Still Painful):**

BBWA formula: New Conversion Price = Old Price × (Old Shares + New Shares at Old Price) / (Old Shares + New Shares Issued)

Using the same example:
- Old price: ₹50/share
- New price: ₹30/share
- Old shares: 100 lakh
- New shares issued at ₹30: 16.67 lakh
- Hypothetical new shares at ₹50: 10 lakh

New conversion price = ₹50 × (100 + 10) / (100 + 16.67) = ₹50 × 110/116.67 = ₹47.14

So Investor A's 10 lakh shares convert at ₹47.14 instead of ₹50. This is much gentler — but the dilution still exists, and at a large down round (say 50% down), even BBWA bites hard.

## The Liquidation Preference Stack

Every time you raise a new round, investors typically get liquidation preference — the right to get their money back before founders see anything in an exit. In a down round scenario, this creates what's called the "liquidation preference overhang."

Let's model this through a realistic Indian startup scenario:

**Company: EdTech startup, raised in 2021 bubble**

| Round | Amount Raised | Post-Money Valuation | Liquidation Pref Multiple |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seed 2020 | ₹3 Cr | ₹15 Cr | 1x non-participating |
| Series A 2021 | ₹25 Cr | ₹125 Cr | 1x non-participating |
| Bridge 2023 | ₹8 Cr | ₹60 Cr (down round) | 1.5x participating |
| Total raised | ₹36 Cr | — | — |

The bridge round investor, sensing leverage, negotiated 1.5x participating preferred. This means in any exit:

1. Bridge investor gets ₹12 Cr (1.5x ₹8 Cr) off the top
2. Series A investor gets ₹25 Cr off the top (1x)
3. Seed investor gets ₹3 Cr off the top (1x)
4. Then remaining proceeds split by ownership

If this company exits for ₹80 crore:

```
Exit proceeds: ₹80 Cr

Step 1 - Bridge investor (1.5x participating):
  Preference: ₹12 Cr
  Remaining: ₹68 Cr

Step 2 - Series A investor (1x non-participating):
  Preference: ₹25 Cr
  Remaining: ₹43 Cr

Step 3 - Seed investor (1x non-participating):
  Preference: ₹3 Cr
  Remaining: ₹40 Cr

Step 4 - Pro-rata distribution of remaining ₹40 Cr:
  Assume founders own 45%, bridge 10%, Series A 30%, Seed 5%, ESOP 10%
  Bridge investor also participates: 10% × ₹40 Cr = ₹4 Cr additional
  Founders: 45% × ₹40 Cr = ₹18 Cr
  ESOP: 10% × ₹40 Cr = ₹4 Cr (mostly worthless since strike price > payout per share)

Founder total: ₹18 Cr out of ₹80 Cr exit = 22.5%
Investor total: ₹25 + ₹3 + ₹12 + ₹4 (bridge participation) = ₹44 Cr + ESOP remainder
```

The founders raised ₹36 crore, own 45% of the company, but in an ₹80 crore exit — which is still nominally 2.2x the amount raised — they walk away with ₹18 crore. The liquidation preference stack consumed most of the upside.

If this same company had never raised the bridge round (raised the series A at a more reasonable ₹75 crore valuation), they might have been able to raise a proper Series B instead, and the founders would not be underwater on a decade of work.

## India's 2021 Bubble Victims: Real Patterns

India's venture market hit peak valuation insanity in 2021. ZIRP (Zero Interest Rate Policy) in the US pushed global capital into emerging markets, and Indian startups saw valuation multiples expand by 3–5x compared to historical norms. The sectors most affected:

### EdTech

BYJU'S is the most visible example but not the most instructive — it was unique in scale. More instructive are the mid-tier EdTech startups that raised at 20–30x ARR in 2021 and are now operating with flat or declining revenue.

A pattern that played out across at least a dozen EdTech companies:
- Raised ₹20–100 Cr at ₹200–500 Cr valuations in 2021
- Rode the COVID tailwind on online learning adoption
- Post-COVID, retention dropped sharply (NRR fell below 80% for most)
- Revenue plateaued at 2021 levels or declined
- Cannot raise at previous valuation; bridge rounds from existing investors at 50–70% discount
- Several now operating as zombie companies (see our companion article on zombie startups)

The structural issue: EdTech in India is fundamentally constrained by parents' willingness to pay for supplemental education. The TAM is real but the price ceiling is low (₹2,000–5,000/month per student for most products). Companies that got valued on "COVID growth" were valued on an anomaly, not a trend.

### D2C Consumer

The D2C space saw even more aggressive overvaluation. Brands with ₹2–5 crore monthly GMV were raising at ₹80–150 crore valuations in 2021. The logic: "India's consumption story," "brand building takes time," "Mamaearth showed the path."

What the investors didn't model adequately: customer acquisition costs in India's digital advertising market tripled between 2019 and 2023. A D2C brand making money at ₹200 CAC in 2020 was losing money at ₹600 CAC in 2023 on the same product.

Brands that raised at high valuations were unable to demonstrate the unit economics improvement needed to justify the next round. Several are still operating — slowly spending down capital, not growing, not dying.

### Consumer Fintech

Buy Now Pay Later startups, neo-banking apps, and credit-focused consumer fintechs raised aggressively in 2021. RBI's tightening of digital lending regulations in 2022 then fundamentally changed the business model for many.

Companies that raised at ₹300–1,000 crore valuations based on aggressive loan book growth projections found that the growth required to justify those valuations was now illegal (high FLDG structures, co-lending arrangements, etc. got restricted). Down rounds of 60–80% became common.

## How to Calculate Your "Safe" Valuation Ceiling

Before accepting a term sheet, every founder should run this calculation. The goal is to determine the maximum valuation you can raise at today without creating a structural problem for your next round.

### Step 1: Identify Your Realistic Next-Round Metrics

Based on your current metrics and reasonable growth assumptions, project what you'll look like in 18–24 months (typical time to next round).

| Metric | Today | 18 months (Base Case) | 18 months (Good Case) |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARR / Revenue Run Rate | ₹X | ₹X × 2 | ₹X × 3 |
| Gross Margin | Y% | Y% | Y+5% |
| MoM Growth Rate | Z% | Z-2% (deceleration) | Z% |
| Burn Rate | ₹A | ₹A × 1.5 | ₹A × 2 |

Use your base case for the safety calculation, not the good case.

### Step 2: Apply Current Market Multiples for Your Sector

These are 2025–2026 Indian market multiples for pricing rounds. These will change — recalibrate before you raise.

| Sector | Seed Multiple (ARR) | Series A Multiple (ARR) | Series B Multiple (ARR) |
|---|---|---|---|
| B2B SaaS | 8–12x | 10–18x | 15–25x |
| Fintech (regulated) | 5–8x | 8–12x | 10–18x |
| D2C Consumer | 2–4x | 3–6x | 5–10x |
| EdTech | 3–5x | 5–8x | 8–12x |
| Healthtech | 5–10x | 8–15x | 12–20x |
| AgriTech | 4–8x | 6–12x | 10–15x |

### Step 3: Calculate the Implied Next-Round Valuation

Next round post-money = Projected ARR at next raise × Sector multiple (base case)

For example:
- Current ARR: ₹3 Cr
- Projected ARR in 18 months (2x growth): ₹6 Cr
- Sector (B2B SaaS), base case multiple: 10x
- Implied next round valuation: ₹60 Cr

### Step 4: Apply the 2x Buffer Rule

Your current round post-money should be no more than half the implied next round valuation. This ensures you have room to grow into the valuation before going back to market.

```
Safe current round ceiling = Next round implied valuation ÷ 2

In the example:
Safe current round ceiling = ₹60 Cr ÷ 2 = ₹30 Cr

If an investor is offering you ₹10 Cr at ₹50 Cr post-money,
that exceeds your safe ceiling of ₹30 Cr.
You should push back or accept the math of the risk.
```

### Step 5: The Downside Scenario Check

Also run the calculation assuming your growth is 50% of base case (things go slower than expected). If the next round implied valuation in that scenario is still above your current round post-money, you're in a defensible position.

```
Downside ARR (50% of base): ₹4.5 Cr
Downside multiple (sector compression): 7x
Downside next round valuation: ₹31.5 Cr

Current round post-money: ₹30 Cr
Buffer: ₹31.5 Cr vs ₹30 Cr → very thin, borderline

Conclusion: ₹30 Cr current round post-money is at the edge of safety.
Consider negotiating to ₹25 Cr for real margin of safety.
```

## The ₹40Cr vs ₹80Cr Decision: A Real Worked Example

This is the exact scenario many Indian founders face. Two term sheets: same money, different valuations.

**Setup:**
- Company raising ₹10 crore
- Forecast: next round in 18 months, likely at ₹60 crore post-money
- Two offers: ₹10 Cr at ₹40 Cr post-money (20% dilution) vs ₹10 Cr at ₹80 Cr post-money (11% dilution)

The instinct: take ₹80 Cr. Less dilution. Better signal.

But run the numbers on what happens when the next round is at ₹60 Cr:

**Scenario A: Took ₹10 Cr at ₹40 Cr (20% dilution)**

```
Cap table after current round:
- Founders: 75% (after ESOP of 5% already allocated)
- Current investor: 20%
- ESOP: 5%

Next round at ₹60 Cr (UP round, no anti-dilution trigger):
- Raise ₹15 Cr → 25% dilution to new investor
- Post-money: ₹60 Cr

New cap table:
- Founders: 75% × 75% = 56.25%
- Round 1 investor: 20% × 75% = 15%
- Round 2 investor: 25%
- ESOP: 5% × 75% = 3.75%

Exit at ₹200 Cr:
- No preference overhang (both 1x non-participating, company worth 3x+)
- Founders get: 56.25% × ₹200 Cr = ₹112.5 Cr
```

**Scenario B: Took ₹10 Cr at ₹80 Cr (11% dilution)**

```
Cap table after current round:
- Founders: 84% (after ESOP of 5%)
- Current investor: 11%
- ESOP: 5%

Next round at ₹60 Cr (DOWN round, anti-dilution triggers):
- Current investor has BBWA anti-dilution
- Investor's effective stake increases from 11% to ~13% post-adjustment
- Raise ₹15 Cr at ₹60 Cr post-money → 25% dilution to new investor
- But new investor demands 1.5x participating preferred (leverage from down round signal)

Post-money cap table:
- Founders: 84% × 75% × (adjustment factor ~0.95) = 59.85%
- Round 1 investor (post-anti-dilution): 13% × 75% = 9.75%
- Round 2 investor (participating pref): 25%
- ESOP: 5% × 75% × 0.95 = 3.5625%
- Note: Founders also face reputational damage from down round, harder next raise

Exit at ₹200 Cr with participating preferred:
- Round 2 investor: ₹15 Cr (1.5x pref) + 25% × (₹200 - ₹15 - ₹10) = ₹15 + 43.75 = ₹58.75 Cr
- Round 1 investor: ₹10 Cr (1x pref) + 9.75% × ₹175 Cr remaining after pref = ₹10 + 17.06 = ₹27.06 Cr
  [Wait: simplified calculation below]

Simplified:
- Total preference claims: ₹10 Cr (R1) + ₹22.5 Cr (R2 1.5x) = ₹32.5 Cr
- Remaining for distribution: ₹200 - 32.5 = ₹167.5 Cr
- R2 participates in distribution: 25% × ₹167.5 = ₹41.875 Cr + ₹22.5 pref = ₹64.375 Cr
- Founders' share of remaining (₹167.5 × 59.85%): ₹100.25 Cr
- But R2's participation comes out of founders' share effectively
- Founders net: ~₹100.25 Cr (before R2 participation adjustment)
- Adjusted founders: ₹200 Cr × 59.85% - participation drag = approx ₹92–95 Cr
```

**The Comparison:**

| Scenario | Founder Proceeds at ₹200 Cr Exit |
|---|---|
| A: ₹40 Cr valuation (down round avoided) | ₹112.5 Cr |
| B: ₹80 Cr valuation (down round happens) | ~₹92–95 Cr |
| Difference | ₹17–20 Cr |

The founder who took the lower valuation at current round ends up with ₹17–20 crore more at a ₹200 crore exit. The "extra dilution" from the lower valuation round cost 9% upfront but the down round mechanics cost 15–20% at exit.

The higher valuation was a trap.

## When High Valuations Are Actually Fine

To be balanced: there are situations where accepting a high valuation is rational.

**1. You are extremely confident in your growth trajectory.** If you have signed contracts, locked distribution, or a regulatory moat that makes 3x growth in 18 months nearly certain, the down round risk is low.

**2. The capital is strategically critical beyond its financial value.** A ₹5 crore investment from a strategic corporate investor who gives you distribution to 1,000 stores might be worth taking at any valuation — the value is the strategic relationship, not the money.

**3. You have a clear path to profitability that eliminates the next fundraise.** If you raise at a high valuation but use the capital to become profitable, you never need to face the next round market. The valuation trap only springs if you need to raise again.

**4. The investor waives anti-dilution in the term sheet.** Some founders negotiate away anti-dilution provisions in exchange for a higher valuation. If you can get the investor to agree to no anti-dilution protection, the down round consequences are much more manageable.

## Negotiating Valuation When You Have Competing Term Sheets

The best leverage in valuation negotiation is competition. Here is how to use it properly.

### Creating Honest Competition

"We have multiple term sheets" is a common bluff. Indian VCs have networks and they know each other. If you're bluffing, it often backfires. Instead, create real competition:

1. **Run a tight process.** Tell all investors you're making a decision by a specific date. This creates urgency and parallel processing.
2. **Be specific without revealing the other party.** "We have a term sheet in the ₹40–50 crore range from a fund focused on B2B SaaS" is more credible than "we have multiple term sheets."
3. **Use the term sheet to open doors.** The first term sheet is the hardest to get. Once you have one, use it to accelerate conversations with other investors.

### The Valuation Negotiation Framework

When negotiating valuation, the most effective approach is not "I want more" — it's "here is the data."

```
Step 1: Anchor on comparables
"Three comparable companies in our space raised at 12–15x ARR in Q4 2025.
Our ARR is ₹4 crore, which implies ₹48–60 crore post-money.
Your offer of ₹35 crore is below market for our metrics."

Step 2: Offer to bridge the gap with structure
"I understand your concern about paying 15x ARR.
What if we did ₹50 crore post-money with a 20% discount on the NEXT round
for you if we miss our 18-month projections?
That aligns our incentives without punishing either side upfront."

Step 3: Identify the actual bottleneck
Investors rarely say "your valuation is too high" if everything else is good.
Usually the objection is about a specific risk:
- Team completeness (common at seed)
- Market size belief (common at Series A)
- Unit economics clarity (common at any stage)
Address the underlying concern, and the valuation discussion often resolves itself.
```

### What to Demand When Accepting a Lower Valuation

If you decide to accept a lower valuation, make sure you negotiate the structural terms that matter:

| Term | What to Negotiate for | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Anti-dilution | BBWA only, not full ratchet | Limits damage of future down round |
| Liquidation preference | 1x non-participating | Preserves exit upside |
| Pay-to-play clause | Include it | Forces existing investors to participate in future rounds or lose preferences |
| Board composition | Founder majority or 50/50 | Preserves control through down market |
| Drag-along threshold | High (>80% shareholder approval) | Prevents forced sale at bad time |
| Information rights | Standard; don't give excessive reporting | Reduces friction |

## The Psychological Trap: Why Founders Accept Bad Deals

Understanding the psychology matters because the rational calculation above rarely happens in the heat of a fundraising process.

**Loss aversion in reverse.** Normally, loss aversion makes people too risk-averse. In fundraising, it works the other way: founders fear the loss of the deal more than they fear the future consequences of the deal terms. The term sheet is certain; the down round is probabilistic. Founders take the certain win.

**Anchoring.** Once you hear "₹80 crore," it's very hard to go back to "₹40 crore." Every piece of information you encounter after that gets filtered through the ₹80 crore anchor. Your employees, your family, your co-founders have all been told ₹80 crore.

**Social proof from other founders.** In 2021, every WhatsApp group of founders had someone raising at 30x ARR. Social norms shifted. Accepting a "low" valuation felt like leaving money on the table or signaling weakness.

**Investor validation hunger.** Being valued at ₹80 crore by a reputable fund feels like validation that you are building something real. It is — but the valuation number is not the validation. The investment itself is.

The antidote to all of these biases is the same: run the math. Write down the scenario in which the high valuation creates a down round. Calculate exactly what happens to your equity in that scenario. Make the future consequence concrete.

## The "Take a Lower Valuation and Win More" Framework

Here is the complete decision framework. Use this before you sign any term sheet.

```
VALUATION SAFETY CHECK

Current metrics:
- ARR: ₹_____ Cr
- MoM growth: _____%
- Gross margin: _____%
- Monthly burn: ₹_____ Cr
- Runway post-raise: _____ months

Step 1: Project 18-month metrics (base case, 50% confidence)
- ARR at next raise: ₹_____ Cr
- Sector ARR multiple (base case): _____x
- Implied next round valuation: ₹_____ Cr

Step 2: Calculate safe ceiling
- Safe current round ceiling: ₹_____ Cr (= Step 1 ÷ 2)

Step 3: Evaluate the offer
- Offer post-money: ₹_____ Cr
- Is offer > safe ceiling? YES / NO
- If YES: What growth rate do you need to avoid a down round?
  Required ARR growth = (Offer post-money × 2) ÷ sector multiple ÷ current ARR
  Is that growth rate achievable? YES / NO

Step 4: If offer exceeds safe ceiling and growth is uncertain:
- Negotiate down valuation + better terms
- OR document that you accept the risk consciously
- OR check if this round is your last (path to profitability)

Decision: Accept / Negotiate / Walk Away
```

## What to Do if You're Already Trapped

If you're reading this and recognize your own company in these scenarios, you have a few options. None are painless, but some are better than others.

**Option 1: Grow your way out.** The cleanest solution. Focus entirely on reaching the revenue milestone that justifies your current valuation before you need to raise again. Extend runway (cut burn), increase revenue, and delay the next fundraise as long as possible.

**Option 2: Bridge round from existing investors.** If you need capital but can't raise at your current valuation, go to your existing investors first. An internal bridge at the same valuation (no down round) is far better than an external down round. Most investors will prefer a note or small follow-on at current valuation over watching their investment go to zero.

**Option 3: Negotiate a structured down round.** If a down round is unavoidable, negotiate the structure hard. Get the new investor to accept: (a) BBWA anti-dilution triggered (not full ratchet), (b) existing investor preferences reset (not stacked), (c) a founder retention package (new shares or re-vesting) to keep you economically motivated.

**Option 4: Strategic acquisition search.** Start quietly. Many acqui-hires and strategic acquisitions happen below the last round valuation. These conversations take 6–18 months. Start them before you're desperate.

**Option 5: Graceful wind-down.** If none of the above is available, shutting down cleanly and returning remaining capital to investors is better than years of zombie operation. See our article on zombie startups for the full framework.

## Key Takeaways

1. A high valuation is a liability, not just an asset. It creates a structural obligation to perform at a level that justifies the next, higher valuation.

2. The overvaluation trap is mechanical, not accidental. High valuation → smaller investor pool → higher performance requirement → more likely down round → anti-dilution triggers → founder equity crushed.

3. Calculate your safe valuation ceiling before signing. Use: (projected ARR at next raise × sector multiple) ÷ 2.

4. The ₹40 Cr vs ₹80 Cr example is real. Taking a lower valuation today and avoiding a down round often leaves founders with more money at exit than taking the higher valuation.

5. If you're already trapped: grow, bridge, negotiate structure, or find an acquirer. Don't wait until you're out of runway to have these conversations.

6. The best time to think about this is before you raise, not after. Read term sheets with a financial lens, not an ego lens.
