# Pre-Revenue Startup Valuation India: 5 Methods That Actually Work

The hardest valuation question in startups is not "what are we worth at Series B?" That one has data: ARR multiples, comparable rounds, Damodaran tables.

The hardest question is: **"What are we worth before we have any revenue?"**

Most founders answer it one of three ways:
1. Copy a friend's valuation from a similar-sounding round
2. Work backwards from "I want to raise X and give up Y%"
3. Ask their CA, who has no idea

None of these are right. And in India, they're not just wrong — they can create a tax problem. Because a pre-revenue valuation that's "too high" relative to a defensible calculation can trigger angel tax under Section 56(2)(viib) / Rule 11UA.

This guide explains 5 methods that genuinely apply to pre-revenue Indian startups, when to use each, what numbers to use, and how the whole thing interacts with angel tax compliance.

---

## Why Pre-Revenue Valuation Is Different

Standard valuation approaches — DCF, revenue multiples, EBITDA multiples — all require something you don't have yet: revenue, or at least the clear prospect of it arriving soon.

Pre-revenue valuation is fundamentally different because:

1. **There's no financial history to model from** — you're projecting into the unknown
2. **The primary value drivers are qualitative** — team, IP, market, progress
3. **The investor is pricing a bet, not an asset** — uncertainty is baked in
4. **Angel tax compliance requires a defensible number** — you can't just assert a valuation

The five methods below are specifically suited to pre-revenue or very-early-revenue startups. They're used in India by angel investors, incubators, and Merchant Bankers — and each has specific use cases.

## Method 1: The Berkus Method

Developed by US angel investor Dave Berkus, this method assigns a maximum value to five key risk-reducing elements of a startup.

### The Framework

| Element | What It Represents | Max Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Idea/Concept** | Is the basic value proposition sound? | ₹1 crore |
| **Prototype** | Does a working product exist? | ₹1 crore |
| **Quality Management Team** | Can this team execute? | ₹1 crore |
| **Strategic Relationships** | Do you have anchor customers/partners? | ₹1 crore |
| **Product Rollout/Sales** | Any early commercial traction? | ₹1 crore |
| **Maximum Pre-Revenue Value** | | **₹5 crore** |

### How to Apply It

For each element, assess: fully present (₹1 crore), partially present (₹25L–75L), or absent (₹0).

**Example: EdTech startup, pre-revenue**

| Element | Assessment | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Idea | Proven demand — tutoring is large, well-validated market | ₹80L |
| Prototype | Working app with 50 beta users | ₹75L |
| Team | 2 founders: ex-teacher + IIT engineer | ₹90L |
| Strategic Relationships | LOI from 2 schools, 1 coaching institute | ₹60L |
| Traction | 50 beta users, no revenue | ₹20L |
| **Pre-Money Valuation** | | **₹3.25 crore** |

### When to Use It

- Very early-stage (idea to prototype)
- Indian angel network rounds (IAN, LetsVenture early-stage)
- When you need a simple, explainable framework for first-time investors

### Limitations

- The ₹1 crore per element cap is a US-origin benchmark. In India, some angels apply ₹50L per element for very early stage; ₹2 crore per element for more developed startups. The framework is directional, not precise.
- Doesn't reflect sector-specific premiums (a defensible fintech with RBI connections is worth more than a generic app)

### Angel Tax Implications

The Berkus method is **not a prescribed method under Rule 11UA**. You cannot use it as a standalone defence against angel tax scrutiny. Use it to determine a reasonable valuation range, then support that number with a Merchant Banker DCF report.

## Method 2: The Scorecard Method (Bill Payne Method)

More systematic than Berkus, the Scorecard Method compares your startup to an average seed-stage startup in your region and sector, then adjusts based on specific factors.

### Step 1: Determine the Average Pre-Money Valuation for Comparable Startups

**Indian seed-stage benchmarks (2026):**

| Sector | Average Pre-Money Valuation Range |
|---|---|
| B2B SaaS (pre-revenue) | ₹2–5 crore |
| Consumer App (pre-revenue) | ₹1–3 crore |
| Fintech (pre-revenue, licensed) | ₹3–8 crore |
| DeepTech / AI (pre-revenue) | ₹3–10 crore |
| D2C (prototype stage) | ₹1–3 crore |
| HealthTech (with clinical validation) | ₹3–7 crore |

For our example: B2B SaaS, average pre-money = ₹3.5 crore.

### Step 2: Scorecard Weighting

| Factor | Weight | Your Rating | Adjusted Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strength of Management Team | 30% | 1.2x (strong team) | 36% |
| Size of Opportunity | 25% | 1.1x (large TAM) | 27.5% |
| Product/Technology | 15% | 0.9x (competitive space) | 13.5% |
| Competitive Environment | 10% | 0.8x (crowded) | 8% |
| Marketing/Sales Channels | 10% | 1.0x (average) | 10% |
| Need for Additional Investment | 5% | 1.1x (capital efficient) | 5.5% |
| Other Factors | 5% | 1.0x | 5% |
| **Total** | 100% | | **105.5%** |

### Step 3: Apply the Score

Pre-money valuation = ₹3.5 crore × 1.055 = **₹3.69 crore**

### What the Rating Scale Means

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1.5x | Exceptional — top 5% of startups in category |
| 1.25x | Very strong |
| 1.0x | Average for the category |
| 0.75x | Below average |
| 0.5x | Significant weakness |

### Management Team Rating Guide (the most important factor at 30%)

| Team Profile | Rating |
|---|---|
| Serial entrepreneur with prior exit + relevant domain | 1.5x |
| Ex-founder, raised before, domain expert | 1.25x |
| Strong operator with 10+ years domain experience | 1.1x |
| IIT/IIM or comparable, first startup, relevant domain | 1.0x |
| Technical founder, no management experience | 0.75x |
| First-time non-technical founder | 0.5x |

### When to Use It

- Seed to pre-Series A
- When pitching to Indian angel networks (IAN, Mumbai Angels use variants of this)
- When you want a more rigorous framework than Berkus
- When comparable deals are available for calibration

## Method 3: The VC Method (Pre-Revenue Version)

The VC Method is the most investor-logic-aligned approach. It works backwards from the exit — asking: "Given the risk and return expectations, what should I pay today?"

### The Formula

```
Post-Money Valuation = Expected Exit Value / (Required Return)^n

Where:
Expected Exit Value = projected revenue at exit × exit multiple
Required Return    = VC's target return (typically 10x–25x for seed)
n                  = years to exit (typically 5–7)
```

### Pre-Revenue VC Method: Step by Step

**Inputs for an Indian B2B SaaS startup:**

| Input | Value | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Projected ARR at exit (Year 6) | ₹60 crore | Achievable for solid SaaS company |
| Revenue multiple at exit | 6x ARR | Conservative India SaaS exit multiple |
| Projected Exit Value | ₹360 crore | |
| Investor's required return | 15x | Seed stage risk premium |
| Time to exit | 6 years | |

**Calculation:**

Post-Money Valuation = ₹360 crore / 15 = **₹24 crore**

If the investor is putting in ₹1.5 crore:
Ownership required = ₹1.5 crore / ₹24 crore = **6.25%**
Pre-Money Valuation = ₹24 crore − ₹1.5 crore = **₹22.5 crore**

### Dilution Adjustment

The VC Method often includes a dilution adjustment because the investor knows you'll raise more rounds before exit, diluting their stake.

If the investor expects 60% dilution through future rounds (i.e., their 6.25% becomes ~2.5% at exit):

Adjusted required ownership = 6.25% / (1 − 0.60) = **15.6%**
Post-Money Valuation = ₹1.5 crore / 15.6% = **₹9.6 crore**
Pre-Money Valuation = ₹9.6 crore − ₹1.5 crore = **₹8.1 crore**

**This is why investors ask for higher ownership than the simple math suggests.** They're accounting for future dilution.

### Key Variables and How to Set Them

**Exit multiple (what to use for Indian startups):**

| Sector | Conservative | Base Case | Optimistic |
|---|---|---|---|
| B2B SaaS | 5–7x ARR | 8–12x ARR | 15–25x ARR |
| Consumer/D2C | 1–2x revenue | 3–5x revenue | 6–10x revenue |
| Fintech | 3–5x revenue | 6–10x revenue | 12–20x revenue |
| HealthTech | 4–6x revenue | 8–15x revenue | 20x+ revenue |
| Marketplace | 2–4x GMV take-rate | 5–8x | 10x+ |

**Required return (what seed investors actually use):**

| Stage | Typical Required Return |
|---|---|
| Pre-seed | 25–50x |
| Seed | 10–25x |
| Pre-Series A | 8–15x |
| Series A | 5–10x |

**Why the variance?** Higher required returns compensate for higher failure probability. At pre-seed, 80%+ of investments fail. At Series A, the failure rate is lower because the company has demonstrated something.

### When to Use the VC Method

- When pitching institutional angels or micro-VCs who think in terms of portfolio returns
- When you want to reverse-engineer what ownership a rational investor should take
- Best combined with DCF or Scorecard to triangulate

## Method 4: Cost-to-Duplicate Method

This approach asks: **what would it cost someone to build what you've built?**

It's particularly relevant for:
- IP-heavy startups (proprietary algorithms, patents, clinical data)
- Software products with significant development investment
- Startups where the primary asset is the built product, not the team

### How to Calculate It

| Asset | How to Value |
|---|---|
| Software development | Market rate × developer-months invested |
| Proprietary data sets | Cost of collection/curation + annotation cost |
| Patents / IP | Filing cost + development cost to create the IP |
| Clinical/regulatory work | Actual cost of trials, approvals obtained |
| Brand/domain | Opportunity cost of building recognition |
| Key team assembled | Recruitment cost + salary premium over market |

**Example: AI-based diagnostics startup**

| Asset | Cost to Duplicate |
|---|---|
| ML model development (18 months, 4 engineers at ₹12L/yr) | ₹72L |
| Medical dataset collection and annotation | ₹45L |
| Clinical validation study (Phase 1) | ₹30L |
| CDSCO regulatory dossier preparation | ₹15L |
| Team assembly (2 senior hires with domain expertise) | ₹25L |
| **Total Cost to Duplicate** | **₹1.87 crore** |

The company raises at ₹5 crore pre-money. The ₹3.13 crore premium over cost-to-duplicate reflects the team, strategic positioning, and first-mover advantage.

### Limitations

- Ignores future value entirely — purely backward-looking
- Undervalues companies where the product is not the only asset (network effects, brand, relationships)
- Usually produces the **floor** valuation, not the target

### When to Use It

- As a floor/sanity check: "our valuation should be at least this"
- For hardware or deep-tech startups where capital expenditure is significant
- When defending valuation to a conservative investor who wants to see asset backing
- Useful combined with Berkus/Scorecard to bookend the range

## Method 5: Market Comparables (Adjusted for Indian Context)

This method finds recent fundraising rounds by comparable companies and uses those data points to anchor your valuation.

### The Process

1. Find 5–10 comparable Indian startups that raised at a similar stage in the last 18 months
2. Note their pre-money valuation, sector, city, team profile, and what they had at the time of raise
3. Adjust your valuation relative to those comps based on differentiating factors

### Where to Find Indian Comparable Rounds

| Source | What It Shows | Lag |
|---|---|---|
| **Entrackr** | Seed/angel rounds often broken down | 1–4 weeks |
| **Tracxn** | Database of Indian startup rounds, searchable by sector/stage | 2–6 weeks |
| **Inc42** | Deal announcements with some valuation data | 1–3 weeks |
| **LetsVenture** | Platform-specific deal history | N/A (need access) |
| **MCA/ROC filings** | Equity allotment returns (Form PAS-3) — ground truth but delayed | 3–6 months |
| **Angel investor LinkedIn posts** | Sometimes announce deal terms openly | Real-time |

### A Realistic Comps Table (B2B SaaS, India, Seed Stage, 2025–2026)

| Company | Sector | Stage | Raise Amount | Pre-Money | What They Had |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Company A | HR-Tech SaaS | Pre-revenue | ₹1.5Cr | ₹5Cr | MVP, 3 LOIs |
| Company B | Logistics SaaS | Pre-revenue | ₹75L | ₹2.5Cr | Beta product, 2 pilots |
| Company C | Fintech SaaS | Pre-revenue + RBI license | ₹3Cr | ₹10Cr | License + team |
| Company D | Healthcare SaaS | Pre-revenue | ₹2Cr | ₹6Cr | Clinical validation |
| Company E | EdTech SaaS | Pre-revenue | ₹1Cr | ₹3Cr | Beta, 100 users |

**Median pre-money:** ₹5 crore for a typical B2B SaaS pre-revenue round in India

**Adjusting from comps:**
- Your team is stronger than 3 of 5 comps: +20%
- Your market is smaller than average: −15%
- You have a regulatory license no comp has: +30%
- **Adjusted valuation: ₹5Cr × 1.35 = ₹6.75 crore**

### When to Use It

- When comparable rounds are available and recent (within 12 months)
- When raising from investors who know the market and will cross-check your number
- As the primary method if comps are strong; as a sanity check otherwise

## Putting It Together: The Triangulation Approach

No single method gives you the "right" answer. The professional approach is to use 2–3 methods and triangulate.

**Example: AI-powered legal-tech startup, pre-revenue**

| Method | Valuation |
|---|---|
| Berkus | ₹3.5 crore |
| Scorecard | ₹4.2 crore |
| VC Method (base case) | ₹6.8 crore |
| Cost-to-Duplicate | ₹2.1 crore (floor) |
| Market Comparables | ₹4.5 crore |

**Range: ₹3.5–6.8 crore, central estimate ~₹4–5 crore**

**The founder's ask:** ₹5 crore pre-money is defensible — it's at the top of the Scorecard/Comps range and well within the VC method upper bound. The floor (cost-to-duplicate) is ₹2.1 crore — showing the investor there is real asset backing.

## The Angel Tax Overlay: Matching Method to Compliance

Here's where Indian founders face a problem US founders don't: the commercial valuation (what you negotiate with investors) must be reconcilable with Rule 11UA (what the IT department will scrutinise).

| Commercial Method | Rule 11UA Status | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| Berkus | Not prescribed | Use as input to Merchant Banker's DCF |
| Scorecard | Not prescribed | Use as input to Merchant Banker's DCF |
| VC Method | Not prescribed for residents | Use for non-residents (PWERM variant); use as input to DCF for residents |
| Cost-to-Duplicate | Not prescribed | Use as floor/sanity check only |
| Market Comparables | Partially recognised | For non-residents: Comparable Company Multiple method is prescribed |

**The practical workflow:**
1. Use Berkus + Scorecard + Comparables to determine your commercial valuation range
2. Negotiate with investor on pre-money valuation
3. Engage Merchant Banker to prepare Rule 11UA DCF report that supports the agreed valuation
4. Ensure your DPIIT recognition is in place
5. Close the round with documented compliance

**Can the Merchant Banker DCF support any valuation you want?** Not quite. A competent Merchant Banker will build assumptions that are defensible under audit. If you want to raise at ₹20 crore pre-money for a product that's 3 months old with no traction, a legitimate MB will not be able to produce a defensible DCF at that number. This is actually good — it protects you from overvaluing yourself.

## What Investors Actually Use to Price Pre-Revenue Rounds in India

Having talked to angel investors across IAN, Mumbai Angels, Antler, and Surge about how they actually decide on pre-revenue valuations, here's the honest answer:

**They're not primarily using any of the 5 methods above.**

What they're actually doing:

1. **Gut check on team quality** — "Would this team figure it out even if the initial idea is wrong?"
2. **Market size sanity check** — "Is there a path to a ₹500Cr+ revenue business here?"
3. **Comparable deal anchoring** — "What did similar deals price at recently?"
4. **Ownership target** — "I want 10–15% for this check size"
5. **Negotiation** — "They asked for ₹8Cr, I'll offer ₹5Cr, we'll land at ₹6Cr"

The formal methods are used to:
- Sanity check a number that "feels right"
- Prepare the Rule 11UA compliance documentation
- Give both sides a framework for negotiation

**The implication for founders:** Your valuation is ultimately a negotiated number within a range that both sides find reasonable. The methods above help you establish and defend the top of that range. Investor gut check, ownership requirements, and market comparables determine where you actually land.

## Pre-Revenue Valuation by Stage: Indian Benchmarks

To give you a realistic anchor for 2026:

**Idea Stage (no product, just a team and deck):**
- ₹50L–₹2Cr typically
- Unless exceptional team (serial founder with prior exit: up to ₹5Cr)
- Most angel networks won't fund this stage unless they know the founders

**Prototype/MVP Stage (product exists, no revenue):**
- ₹2Cr–₹6Cr for most sectors
- ₹5Cr–₹15Cr for fintech with license, deeptech with IP, regulated sectors
- This is the most common Indian angel round stage

**Pilot/Beta Stage (product live, pilot customers, no commercial revenue):**
- ₹4Cr–₹10Cr for most sectors
- ₹8Cr–₹25Cr if pilots are with marquee enterprise customers or show clear conversion path

**First Revenue (₹5–50L MRR, 3–6 months):**
- ₹5Cr–₹20Cr depending on growth rate and sector
- At this point, you've crossed the threshold into revenue-based valuation methods

## Special Cases: Sector-Specific Pre-Revenue Adjustments

### Fintech (Pre-Revenue)
A fintech startup with an RBI license (PA, PPI, NBFC) commands a 40–80% premium over an equivalent non-fintech startup at the same stage. The license is an asset that takes time and money to obtain, and it creates a meaningful barrier.

### DeepTech / AI (Pre-Revenue)
If you have proprietary models, datasets, or patents, use the Cost-to-Duplicate method as a floor — it will be higher than for pure software. VCs in deeptech often accept lower near-term milestones because the technology itself is the moat.

### HealthTech (Pre-Revenue)
Clinical validation data is a significant asset. If you've completed a pilot study with a hospital and have validation data, that's worth ₹50L–₹2Cr in additional valuation in the eyes of healthcare-focused investors.

### SaaS (Pre-Revenue)
The most competitive space. Pre-revenue SaaS without clear enterprise pipeline or technical differentiation commands modest valuations (₹2–4Cr). Get to ₹5–10L MRR before raising if you can — the multiple improves dramatically.

## The One Number That Matters More Than Valuation

Here's the thing most pre-revenue founders optimise the wrong way.

You're spending hours arguing about whether you're worth ₹5Cr or ₹7Cr pre-money. That's a 2% dilution difference on a ₹1Cr raise.

The number that matters more: **how much are you raising, and will it get you to a clear next milestone?**

A well-sized seed round should fund you to a milestone that (a) de-risks the business, (b) allows the next raise at a higher valuation, and (c) is achievable in 12–18 months.

For most pre-revenue Indian startups, that milestone is:
- B2B SaaS: ₹10–20L MRR with 3–5 paying customers
- Consumer: 50,000–100,000 DAU with measurable retention
- Fintech: License obtained + 1,000 active users
- D2C: ₹50–100L monthly GMV, positive unit economics on paper

If your raise amount doesn't get you to that milestone, you'll be raising again in 9 months in a weak position. That's far more damaging than agreeing on a slightly lower valuation.

**Founder takeaway:** Optimise for raise size relative to milestone, not for highest possible valuation. A slightly lower valuation with a larger, right-sized raise is almost always better than a higher valuation with insufficient capital.
