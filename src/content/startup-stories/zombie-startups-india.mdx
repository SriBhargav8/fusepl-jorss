## A Different Kind of Failure

Most startup post-mortems are about companies that died quickly. They ran out of money, or the founder gave up, or the product never found customers. These are clean failures. They're painful, but they're over.

The zombie startup is a different beast. It doesn't die quickly. It doesn't die at all. It just exists — consuming founder time, a small trickle of investor capital, the careers of employees who could be somewhere better, and the mental bandwidth of everyone involved. Year after year.

India's 2021 funding boom created a generation of zombie startups that are still technically operating in 2026. They have ₹10–50 crore on the balance sheet from rounds raised in 2021–2022. They have 5–30 employees. They have paying customers. They have a founder who shows up every day. But they haven't grown in 18–24 months, they're not generating enough to survive without continued investor capital, and every attempt to raise the next round has failed.

These companies are the silent story of India's startup correction. They don't get written about because they haven't failed yet. But many of them are more painful than a clean shutdown would have been — for everyone involved.

This article is for founders and investors who need to honestly assess whether a company is a zombie, and what to do about it if it is.

---

## What Is a Zombie Startup?

A zombie startup is a company that meets all of the following criteria:

1. **Technically alive:** Has some revenue, some customers, some employees, some cash in the bank.
2. **Not growing:** Revenue has been flat or declining for 12+ months.
3. **Not profitable enough to survive independently:** The company requires continued external capital to pay expenses, OR it's burning through its remaining capital slowly.
4. **Unable to raise the next round:** Has tried (or is unable to try because the metrics don't support it) to raise a follow-on round without success.
5. **No clear path to changing any of the above:** The team has no concrete, near-term pivot or growth lever that would change the trajectory.

The critical qualifier is #5. Every company has bad quarters. Every startup has periods of flat growth. What makes a company a zombie is the absence of a clear, credible path out.

**The zombie is distinct from:**

- A company in a temporary trough (clear catalyst for return to growth within 6 months)
- A company that is deliberately slowing down to reach profitability
- A "lifestyle business" that the founder is deliberately building to be small and profitable
- A failing company that is actively shutting down

The zombie is the space between all of these — and it's the most damaging space to occupy.

## India's Zombie Startup Landscape: 2021–2026

The 2021 venture boom in India funded approximately 2,000+ startups. By any historical measure, a significant proportion of these — estimates range from 20–40% — did not achieve the growth trajectory needed to raise their next round.

The sectors with the highest zombie concentration:

### EdTech

Post-COVID, EdTech suffered one of the sharpest demand reversals in Indian startup history. Companies that raised at 20–30x ARR in 2021 on COVID-driven growth found that:
- Students returned to schools and coaching institutes
- Online learning retention dropped from 70–80% during lockdown to 30–40% in 2022–2023
- Competition for students' (parents') wallets intensified
- Unit economics deteriorated: CAC went up, LTV went down

The result: dozens of EdTech startups with ₹10–100 crore in the bank (raised in 2021) that have been slowly spending down capital for 3+ years with flat revenue. BYJU'S is the most visible name, but the same pattern plays out in dozens of smaller EdTech companies that aren't publicly discussed.

### D2C Consumer Brands

The D2C narrative of 2020–2022 was compelling: India's growing middle class, e-commerce adoption, direct brand relationships. Many brands raised at 15–25x revenue in 2021.

The reality by 2023–2024: customer acquisition costs on Meta and Google had tripled since 2019. Contribution margins for most D2C brands were thin or negative. Repeat purchase rates were lower than projected. The "brand building takes time" narrative became cover for an inability to demonstrate sustainable unit economics.

By 2024–2025, many D2C brands were operating in zombie mode: some revenue (₹2–5 crore monthly GMV), some repeat customers, but nowhere near the scale needed for their exit valuation expectations, and unable to raise a follow-on round at any reasonable valuation.

### Consumer Fintech

The RBI's tightening of digital lending regulations in 2022–2023 fundamentally changed the business model for many consumer fintech companies. Companies that had raised capital to build aggressive credit books found:
- New regulations restricted FLDG structures
- Co-lending terms tightened
- Credit bureau access became more competitive
- Default rates in target segments increased in 2023–2024 as consumer stress rose

Companies that were burning ₹50–100 lakh/month in 2021 "growing the loan book" were suddenly in a world where that growth strategy was partially illegal and partially unprofitable. Many are still operating — processing loans at much lower volumes, slowly spending down capital.

### B2B SaaS (lower tier)

India's B2B SaaS boom also created zombies — companies with 30–100 customers, ₹1–3 crore ARR, that grew quickly to a certain size and then stalled. The stalling usually happens for one of these reasons:
- The initial customers came from founder networks (finite)
- The product lacks the depth to move up to enterprise customers
- The market segment is too small to support the next stage of growth
- Competition from well-funded players (Indian or global) makes winning new customers increasingly expensive

## Why Founders Don't Shut Down

This is the most important psychological reality to understand, because it explains why zombification persists for years rather than months.

### 1. The Sunk Cost Fallacy

"I've spent 4 years on this. If I shut down now, those 4 years were wasted."

This is the classic sunk cost error. The 4 years are gone regardless of what you do next. The question is whether the next year will generate positive or negative expected value. If the answer is negative, the rational choice is to stop — but the psychological pull of the sunk cost is extremely powerful.

For Indian founders, this is compounded by family and social pressure. A founder who shuts down a startup and has to explain it to parents, relatives, and peers experiences social cost in a way that many Western founders don't.

### 2. Fear of Founder Reputation Damage

Indian founders worry, often excessively, that shutting down a startup will permanently damage their reputation and make it impossible to raise money for the next venture.

The data does not support this fear. Serial founders who demonstrate honest, well-managed shutdowns are often viewed positively by investors. The investors who will fund your next company are sophisticated enough to distinguish "ran out of ideas and quietly faded away" from "made a principled decision, handled it well, extracted learnings."

What actually damages reputation: leaving employees without severance, disappearing without communicating, not returning investor capital when it can be returned, lying about the company's status to extend the zombie period.

### 3. The Pivot Hope

"We're not shutting down, we're pivoting."

The pivot is a legitimate strategic move — but it is frequently used as a delay mechanism rather than a genuine strategic reorientation. A real pivot involves: a clear hypothesis about why the original approach didn't work, a specific new direction with testable assumptions, a timeline for testing the new direction, and a decision rule for evaluating whether the pivot is working.

A zombie pivot involves: changing the product description on the website, making some new introductions, writing a new deck, and continuing to spend ₹10–20 lakh per month while the new direction is "explored."

### 4. Employees with Underwater ESOPs

If you have 15 employees who have been with the company for 3 years and whose ESOPs are 2 years from full vesting, the humanistic argument against shutdown is real. Shutting down costs them significant ESOP value.

But the calculation needs to be honest: if the company is a zombie, the ESOPs are likely already worthless. Employees staying for unvested ESOPs in a zombie company are staying for an illusion. The more honest act — for employees — is to tell them clearly where the company stands so they can make their own decisions.

### 5. Investor Relations Management

The investor has 20 portfolio companies. Your company is one of them. If you're not their best performer, the minimal path of resistance is to continue presenting at the quarterly portfolio update, report "making progress on the pivot," and defer the difficult conversation.

Founders have incentive to manage investor perception (avoid being written off, maintain optionality). Investors have incentive not to force the conversation (marking a write-off reduces the fund's reported IRR). Both parties tacitly agree to extend the zombie existence.

## Why Investors Don't Force Shutdown

This is the structural incentive problem that enables zombie startups to persist.

**Fund accounting:** Venture funds report "unrealized value" for active portfolio companies. Until a company is formally written off, it sits on the books at its last valuation (or some mark). A live portfolio company at ₹80 crore stale valuation looks better in a fund update to LPs than a ₹0 write-down.

Fund managers have incentive to delay write-downs, especially when the fund is mid-life (years 4–7) and is being evaluated for raising a successor fund. A clean mark is better for fundraising optics than a string of write-downs.

**Optionality:** "We're giving management more time to find a buyer / complete the pivot / raise a bridge." This is legitimate in year 1 of difficulty. By year 3, it is usually wishful thinking.

**The legacy investment problem:** At a ₹50 crore investment, writing off the company means writing off ₹50 crore from the fund's performance metrics. The human impulse — even for sophisticated investors — is to believe something will change.

**Relationship preservation:** The investor knows the founder. Forcing a shutdown destroys that relationship. Investors who see themselves as "founder-friendly" are psychologically resistant to being the one who forces the call.

## The Real Cost of Zombification

This is the calculation that almost no one does, but it is the most important number in this article.

### The Founder's Opportunity Cost

A founder running a zombie startup for 3 years is spending 3 years of their most valuable career years on a company going nowhere. What is the alternative cost?

```
Scenario: 32-year-old founder running a zombie startup

Option A: Continue zombie for 3 more years, then shut down
- Year 4–6 salary equivalent foregone: ₹30–60L/year × 3 = ₹90–180L
- Founder equity value: ~₹0 (zombie company likely liquidates at low or no value)
- Learning: Some marginal learnings from continued operation
- Next startup delay: 3 years
- Mental health cost: high (stress of managing decline, employees, investor relationships)

Option B: Shut down in Year 4, take 6 months to recover/explore, start new company
- Shutdown month: ₹0 income, but 6 months of active exploration
- New company (Year 5–7): If previous startup taught real lessons, probability of success higher
- 3 years of focused execution on new idea with learnings from first
- Expected value of second startup: substantially higher than continued zombie operation
```

The founder who shuts down a zombie and starts fresh is almost universally better off after 5 years than the founder who ran the zombie for 5 more years.

### The Employee Opportunity Cost

An employee at a zombie startup:
- Is likely not getting market-rate salary increases (zombie companies cut costs)
- Is likely not learning at the same rate as at a growing company
- Has unvested or vested-but-worthless ESOPs
- Is delaying their career progression

The right thing for employees is to know the truth so they can make informed career decisions. Keeping a zombie alive "for the employees" usually means keeping them in a situation that is worse for their careers than the alternatives.

### The Capital Opportunity Cost

₹20 crore sitting in a zombie startup bank account, being spent at ₹50 lakh/month for 40 more months, is ₹20 crore that could be returned to investors and redeployed into a more productive company. India has real capital scarcity for truly promising early-stage companies. The capital locked in zombie startups is not getting to the places where it could create value.

## How to Diagnose if You Are a Zombie

Use these specific metric thresholds. If you cross two or more of these, you are in zombie territory.

| Metric | Zombie Threshold | Healthy Range | Source of Data |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARR growth (YoY) | < 10% for 12+ consecutive months | > 30% | Monthly MRR tracking |
| Net Revenue Retention (NRR) | < 90% for 6+ months | > 110% (B2B SaaS) | Cohort analysis |
| Monthly burn vs MRR | Burn > 2x MRR | Burn < 0.5x MRR | P&L |
| Runway | < 6 months with no fundraising traction | > 18 months | Cash balance / burn rate |
| Last fundraise conversation | > 10 VCs passed in last 6 months | Not applicable | CRM |
| Team NPS | Top performers leaving (>2 in 6 months) | Retention >85% | Exit interviews |
| Customer NPS | < 20 or declining | > 40 | Customer surveys |
| Last growth hack | Cannot name a single new channel working | Multiple channels | GTM analysis |

**The conversation test:** Ask yourself: "If I were an outsider seeing this company for the first time today, would I invest?" If the answer is no — and you need to be ruthlessly honest — you may be running a zombie.

**The pivot test:** "Do I have a clear, specific hypothesis for a pivot that I am testing with measurable results in the next 90 days?" If yes, you're not a zombie yet. If the answer is vague ("we're exploring some new directions"), you're in zombie territory.

## The Zombie Decision Framework

If you've diagnosed your company as a zombie, here are the four paths and how to evaluate each.

### Path 1: The Real Pivot

A real pivot requires answering five questions with specificity:

```
1. What specific insight about the market/customer do we have that we didn't have before?
2. What specifically are we changing (product, customer segment, business model, geography)?
3. What is the minimum viable test of the new direction, and what does success look like?
4. What is our timeline to test it? (Should be 60–90 days, not "we'll know in a year")
5. If the test fails, what do we do?

If you cannot answer all 5 with specific numbers and dates, it is not a real pivot plan.
It is a delay strategy.
```

The classic Indian startup pivot patterns that actually work:
- **SMB to enterprise:** If your SMB customers churn, but the 3 enterprise customers you picked up are sticky, consider moving upmarket. This requires product investment but is a real pivot.
- **B2C to B2B2C:** If you cannot acquire consumers profitably, but your content/product is valuable to businesses who serve consumers, the B2B channel may be the real business.
- **India to global:** Some B2B SaaS companies find that Indian customers are price-sensitive but US/global customers will pay 10x. If you have 5 global customers showing strong retention, the pivot may be to international focus.
- **Platform to services:** When a platform business model isn't working, sometimes the underlying domain knowledge can be packaged as consulting/managed services.

### Path 2: Acquisition Search

Starting an acquisition process before you run out of runway is one of the most strategic decisions a zombie startup founder can make. The window for a reasonable acquisition closes when:
- You have < 3 months runway (desperation pricing)
- Your best employees have left (only the asset remains)
- Your customer base has shrunk significantly

The best acquisition processes for zombie startups in India:

**Strategic acqui-hire:** A larger company acquires your team + product for a combination of cash and employment contracts. Common in tech startups where the engineering team is the primary asset. Typical acqui-hire for a 10-person engineering team in India: ₹5–15 crore total consideration.

**Asset sale:** Selling the customer base, product code, or IP to a competitor or adjacent company. A zombie EdTech company's subscriber base (even if shrinking) may have value to a larger EdTech player trying to expand.

**Distressed sale to strategic:** A corporate (large IT company, conglomerate) acquires the startup at below-last-round valuation but above zero. Founders get some exit, investors get partial recovery, employees get jobs.

**How to start:** Quietly contact 5–10 potential acquirers through your network. "We're exploring strategic partnerships and acquisitions" is the standard first message. You don't have to announce you're a zombie. You do have to be honest once conversations get serious.

### Path 3: Bridge Round from Existing Investors

This is viable only if: the investors believe a real pivot is possible and are willing to fund it, AND there is a specific plan with specific milestones tied to the capital.

Bridge round mechanics in India:
- Usually structured as a CCD (convertible note) at current valuation or with a discount to the next round
- Typically ₹1–3 crore for a 6–12 month extension
- Conditional: investors tie the bridge to specific milestones (e.g., "reach ₹1 Cr MRR within 6 months or we wind down")

The honest bridge round is one where all parties agree: this is 6 months to prove or disprove the pivot thesis. If we don't hit X by date Y, we initiate shutdown.

This kind of explicit conditional bridge is rare but healthy. It aligns incentives and removes the indefinite zombie existence.

### Path 4: Graceful Wind-Down

Sometimes the right answer is to stop. Here is what actually happens when an Indian startup shuts down.

## What Actually Happens When You Shut Down

Most founders don't know the mechanics of shutting down a company in India. The uncertainty about the process is itself a reason some founders avoid it. Here is the reality.

### Notifying Employees

The first step is informing employees. In India, employees are entitled to:
- **Notice period or pay in lieu:** Usually 1–3 months as per employment agreement
- **Gratuity:** For employees with 5+ years of continuous service, gratuity is mandatory (15 days salary per year of service)
- **Provident Fund settlement:** All PF contributions must be settled; employees can withdraw or transfer their PF
- **Full and final settlement:** Outstanding salary, leave encashment, any reimbursements

The common Indian startup shutdown pattern: keep 2–3 core employees for 2–3 months to handle wind-down tasks, lay off others with settlement. Budget: typically ₹50–200 lakh for a 10–30 person startup, depending on tenure and salary levels.

### ESOP Treatment

In a shutdown:
- Unvested ESOPs lapse (no obligation to accelerate vesting)
- Vested ESOPs can be exercised but are typically worthless if the company is shutting down below the exercise price
- The ESOP plan document may provide for accelerated vesting on change of control (acquisition) — not typically on shutdown
- Founders should communicate clearly to employees about ESOP status and not mislead them about potential value

### Cap Table Cleanup

The formal shutdown process in India:

**Step 1:** Board resolution approving voluntary winding up (if solvent) under Companies Act 2013 Section 484.

**Step 2:** Shareholder resolution (special resolution — 3/4 majority) approving winding up.

**Step 3:** Appointment of liquidator.

**Step 4:** Liquidator realizes assets (collects receivables, sells equipment, intellectual property if any value).

**Step 5:** Payment of debts in priority order:
1. Workmen's dues / Employee dues (wages, PF, gratuity)
2. Secured creditors (if any)
3. Tax dues (GST, Income Tax)
4. Unsecured creditors (vendors, etc.)
5. Preference shareholders (investors with liquidation preference)
6. Ordinary shareholders (founders, ESOP holders)

In most zombie startups, the sequence ends at step 5 or even step 4. After paying employees, vendors, and tax authorities, the remaining cash goes to investors (up to their liquidation preference), and founders typically receive nothing.

**Step 6:** File Form STK-2 with ROC for voluntary strike-off (if company has not traded in 2+ years) OR complete the formal winding up process with NCLT.

The timeline: voluntary winding up can take 6–18 months for a clean company. Strike-off (simpler, for companies with minimal activity) can take 3–6 months.

### Investor Write-Off Process

For the VC fund:
- Once the company is formally wound up or strike-off is complete, the investment is formally written off
- The fund adjusts its NAV downward (the mark-down that fund managers had been avoiding)
- Capital returned to investors (if any) from the liquidation proceeds
- Loss is reported in fund performance metrics

For tax purposes: investors can claim capital loss on write-off, which can be set off against capital gains from other investments (within 8 years for long-term losses).

The investor write-off process is clean, final, and — for reputable startups with honest founders — does not permanently damage the founder's relationship with the investor community.

## What Happens After Shutdown: The Founder's Next Chapter

The fear of post-shutdown stigma keeps founders in zombie mode. The data on what actually happens to founders who shut down cleanly is much more encouraging.

**Pattern 1: Raising again successfully.** Indian founders who have started, failed, and started again are increasingly respected in the ecosystem. Investors know startup building is hard. A founder who learned from a failure and demonstrates those lessons in a new company is often a better investment than a first-time founder.

**Pattern 2: Joining a growth-stage company in a leadership role.** The credibility of having built something — even if it didn't succeed — is valuable. Many zombie startup founders have been recruited into VP/Director roles at Series B/C companies where their operational experience is valuable.

**Pattern 3: Doubling down on what worked.** Often, a zombie startup failed at the business model but built deep expertise in a market segment. That expertise is valuable. The next venture or next role uses that expertise more effectively.

The common thread in all three patterns: the founder who shuts down at the right time has energy, clarity, and learnings. The founder who runs a zombie for 3 extra years has burnout, bitterness, and confusion.

## Zombie Startup Case Studies: Patterns From the 2021 Cohort

These are composite patterns drawn from the Indian startup correction of 2022–2025. Names are not used, but the patterns are real and recognizable to anyone who has been inside this ecosystem.

### Pattern 1: The EdTech that Couldn't Retain

A Bengaluru-based EdTech company raised ₹30 crore in early 2022 at a ₹150 crore post-money valuation. Product: live online tutoring for Class 8–12 students, primarily in Tier 2 cities. Revenue at raise: ₹2.5 crore ARR. Valuation multiple: 60x ARR — high, but not unusual for 2022 EdTech.

The 12 months post-raise showed the COVID tailwind unwinding. Schools reopened. Physical coaching returned. Their Month 6 cohort retention, which had been 65% during COVID, fell to 40% in the post-COVID cohorts. NRR dropped to 78%.

They launched three new product lines in 18 months — recorded content, an AI doubt-solving feature, and a parent coaching add-on. None achieved meaningful uptake. By mid-2024, ARR was ₹3.1 crore (barely growing in 2 years), burn was ₹80 lakh/month, runway was 18 months.

The founders tried to raise a Series B in H2 2024. Not a single term sheet. Series A investors declined to bridge at the current valuation. The company continued operating — releasing annual "new product" announcements, maintaining a team of 35, spending down the remaining capital.

By early 2026, this company has approximately 9 months of runway. The founders know the outcome. The employees are beginning to sense it. The investors have internally written off the investment but haven't had the formal conversation.

**What should have happened:** At Month 24 post-raise (late 2023), when two product pivots had failed and NRR was clearly below 90% for three consecutive quarters, the founders should have started an acquisition process. A larger EdTech player, a Tier 1 coaching chain seeking tech capabilities, or an international EdTech player entering India might have offered ₹15–25 crore for the team, product, and student base. The investors would have recovered 50–80 paise on the rupee. The founders would have had a job, some liquidity, and their learnings.

Instead, the company will likely wind down in late 2026 with ₹2–3 crore in remaining cash — returned to investors after employee settlements — representing a 90%+ capital loss.

### Pattern 2: The D2C Brand Trapped by CAC

A Mumbai-based D2C hair care brand raised ₹15 crore across two rounds (2020 and 2021) at a combined post-money of ₹80 crore. Revenue at last raise: ₹1.2 crore monthly GMV. D2C-first, DTC website plus Nykaa and Amazon.

The business grew to ₹2.5 crore monthly GMV by mid-2022, then flatlined. The problem: blended CAC had increased from ₹350 in 2020 to ₹900 in 2022 as Meta advertising costs rose, and their contribution margin was negative at ₹900 CAC on a ₹1,200 average order value.

They pivoted to offline retail — modern trade (supermarket chains) — in late 2022. The retail pivot required listing fees, trade margins of 30–35%, and a field sales team. The margin structure was different but the business became capital-intensive in a new way.

By 2024, revenue was ₹2.8 crore monthly GMV across D2C and offline. Contribution margin was still thin (8–10%). The brand had real customers who liked the product, but could not scale profitably via any channel.

**The zombie metrics:** Flat GMV for 18 months, negative or thin CM, burn of ₹50 lakh/month with 6 months runway. The founders believe a larger FMCG company will acquire them for the brand. That conversation has been "ongoing" for 18 months.

**The lesson:** Acquisition conversations take 12–18 months. Start them when you have 24+ months of runway, not 6. By the time the business is in distress, the acquirer has all the leverage.

### Pattern 3: The B2B SaaS That Hit a Ceiling

A Pune-based B2B SaaS company (HR tech for mid-size manufacturing companies) raised ₹5 crore at ₹25 crore post-money in 2021. They grew steadily from ₹40 lakh ARR to ₹1.8 crore ARR by end 2022. Growth rate: 15% per quarter.

Then they hit a ceiling. Their product was well-suited to manufacturing companies between 200–1,000 employees. The total number of such companies in India willing and able to pay ₹3–5 lakh/year for HR software was approximately 2,000–3,000. At 15% quarterly growth, they would exhaust their TAM in 2–3 years.

The founders recognized this in early 2023 but delayed action. The options were: (a) expand to other segments (retail, healthcare) with product investment, (b) expand geographically (Southeast Asia has similar manufacturing SME profiles), or (c) move upmarket to larger enterprises.

They attempted all three simultaneously — a classic mistake. None succeeded because the team was too small to execute three pivots at once.

By mid-2025, ARR was ₹2.2 crore (growth essentially stopped), team was 18 people, burn was ₹40 lakh/month, runway 14 months. NRR was 105% (their existing customers expanded slightly) but new customer acquisition had stalled.

**The non-obvious zombie:** This company had good metrics by many measures — 105% NRR, profitable unit economics, retention above 90%. But the growth had stopped. It wasn't a dying company. It was a company that had reached its natural ceiling in its initial market and hadn't successfully extended beyond it.

This is the most insidious type of zombie: everything looks fine until you model out what the next 3 years look like if nothing changes.

## The Zombie-to-Acquisition Playbook

Because acquisition is often the best path out of zombie territory, it deserves a detailed treatment. Here's the complete process.

### Step 1: Map Potential Acquirers (While You Still Have Leverage)

There are four types of acquirers for zombie startups in India:

**Type A: Larger players in the same space.** Your direct competitors or adjacent companies who want your customer base, team, or technology. Example: a larger EdTech platform acquiring a smaller one for its student base in a specific city.

**Type B: Corporate strategics.** Large companies (Tata, Reliance, HDFC group, large IT companies) seeking to add capabilities. They buy startups when the capability takes too long to build internally and the price is right.

**Type C: Acqui-hire specialists.** Companies that primarily want your engineering team. The product may or may not continue. Consideration is usually employment contracts + token equity payment. Common in tech-forward companies like Atlassian, Google, or growing Indian tech companies.

**Type D: PE / distressed asset buyers.** Private equity or family offices that buy distressed startups at low multiples, restructure the business, and look for a path to profitability or sale. Rare in India but growing.

### Step 2: The Opening Conversation

Do not open with "we're looking to be acquired." Open with "exploring strategic partnerships and synergies." This preserves optionality and price discovery.

```
Sample opener message (LinkedIn or warm introduction):
"[Name], our company [X] has built [Y specific capability] that we think could be
interesting to [their company] given your focus on [Z]. We've been approached by a
few parties about strategic collaboration, and wanted to explore if there's a fit.
Happy to share a brief overview if relevant."

Note: "approached by a few parties" is true if you've had any acquisition conversation.
It creates appropriate urgency without desperation.
```

### Step 3: Establishing Value (Even in Distress)

What assets are you selling? Be clear about this before you start conversations.

| Asset Type | How to Value It | Documentation Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Customer base (active, paying) | X months of revenue × retention rate | Cohort analysis, customer list |
| Technology / product | Replacement cost or comparable transaction | Code documentation, architecture |
| Team (key people) | Market salary for skills × 12–18 months | Team CVs, retention agreements |
| Data | Unique dataset that's hard to recreate | Data description, size, labeling |
| Regulatory position | License value (NBFC, insurance, etc.) | License documentation |
| Brand | Aided awareness in target segment | Brand survey data if available |

### Step 4: Navigating the Investor Conversation

Any acquisition at below-last-round valuation requires investor consent (drag-along rights, or simple majority shareholder consent). The conversation with investors must happen before the term sheet is signed — not after.

**The honest framing:**
"We've been in conversations with [acquirer]. The offer is below our last round valuation, but given where the business is today, we believe this is the best outcome for all stakeholders. Here's the waterfall analysis at the proposed acquisition price." Then show exactly what each investor receives.

Investors who see honest, early communication are far more likely to be cooperative than investors who feel surprised or manipulated.

## The Honest Conversation You Probably Need to Have

If you're reading this article and recognizing your own company, there's a conversation you probably need to have — with your co-founders, with your board, with yourself.

The conversation is not "are we failing?" The conversation is "are we a zombie, and if so, what is our explicit plan — and timeline — to stop being one?"

The most damaging thing you can do is continue the zombie existence while telling everyone (including yourself) that you're "making progress" and "close to a breakthrough." Every month you spend in zombie mode is a month of opportunity cost for everyone involved.

The honest founder has the conversation. Sets a specific timeline. Names specific milestones. And if those milestones aren't met, makes the hard decision.

That is the hardest thing about zombie startups — not the legal process of shutting down, not the investor conversations, not the employee settlements. It is the founder's willingness to be honest when the evidence is clear.
