# Hiring Your First 10 Employees — The Mistakes That Kill Indian Startups Early

When Kunal Shah was building CRED in 2018, he didn't start by hiring engineers. His first hires were product leaders and designers — people who would shape what the product should be before a line of code was written.

When Girish Mathrubootham built Freshworks, his first sales hire was critical: he needed someone who could sell enterprise software to US customers from Chennai. A wrong hire there would have killed the early traction that fueled every subsequent round.

The first 10 employees are not "early employees." They are co-founders without equity rights. They set the template — the culture, the work style, the decision-making patterns — that every subsequent hire will be evaluated against. They get the most equity, the most access, and the most influence per person that your company will ever give to any group.

Getting this right is not just an HR question. It is a company architecture question.

---

## Why the First 10 Are Different from Everyone Who Follows

The difference between employees 1–10 and employees 11–100 is structural, not just seniority.

**They operate without playbooks**: Employees 11–100 join with processes, team structures, and institutional knowledge. The first 10 arrive when almost none of that exists. They don't just execute a job description — they write the job description.

**They define "how we do things here"**: The first 10 employees establish patterns of behavior that become culture. How do you run meetings? How do you make decisions when you don't have data? How do you communicate bad news? How do you hire the next person? Employees 1–10 set these norms by doing them, even without intending to.

**They get the most equity per contribution**: An engineer who joins at employee #3 (before product-market fit, before Series A, when failure probability is highest) should receive more equity than an engineer who joins at employee #30 (after Series A, after PMF). The equity gradient is steep in the first 10.

**Their mistakes compound**: A bad hire at employee #50 is costly but manageable. A bad hire at employee #5 — who then hires three direct reports, sets cultural precedents, and creates team structures around their working style — is potentially catastrophic.

## The Hiring Order: What to Hire First by Startup Type

There is no universal "first hire." The right early hiring order depends fundamentally on what kind of startup you are building. The most common mistake: copying the hiring order of a startup type different from your own.

### B2B SaaS Startup Hiring Order

```
B2B SaaS: The metric you are driving is ARR.
Everything else is in service of that.

Hire 1-2: Technical co-founder (if not already) or founding engineer
  → Build the product. There is nothing to sell without this.

Hire 3: First Sales / Customer Success (before you think you need them)
  → Common mistake: hiring another engineer instead
  → Reality: you need someone who can demo, manage POCs,
    and onboard paying customers NOW
  → Sales Engineer or Account Executive who can own the full
    early customer lifecycle

Hire 4: Founding product manager (if founders are not doing this)
  → Only if neither founder has strong product instincts
  → If founders can PM, delay this hire until 15-20 employees

Hire 5-7: 2-3 more engineers (now that you have customer
  validation and know what to build)

Hire 8: Customer Success Manager
  → You have 5-10 customers; retention is now existential
  → This hire often comes too late

Hire 9: Marketing (content/demand gen) or second AE
  → Depends on whether inbound or outbound is working better

Hire 10: Finance/operations (if fundraise is imminent)
  or second Sales (if sales is the bottleneck)
```

**The key insight for B2B SaaS**: The sales hire should come before you feel ready for it. Most technical founders delay the first sales hire because they feel the product isn't ready. The sales hire is often how you discover what "ready" means.

### D2C / Consumer Product Startup Hiring Order

```
D2C: The metric you are driving is unit economics
(CAC, LTV, gross margin per order).

Hire 1-2: Operations / Supply Chain person
  → Without reliable supply, everything else is irrelevant
  → This should be day-1 priority, often overlooked by
    founder-heavy teams with marketing or tech backgrounds

Hire 3: Designer (or founding designer on team already)
  → D2C is won on product and brand; design is not a luxury

Hire 4: Performance Marketing specialist
  → Not a generalist marketer; someone who lives in Meta Ads,
    Google Ads, and can read ROAS tables in their sleep

Hire 5: Tech lead (for website, app, backend systems)
  → D2C tech is operations tech, not product tech
  → Inventory management, order management, logistics integration

Hire 6: Category / Merchandising person
  → Who is curating the product assortment?
  → Pricing decisions, vendor relationships, product expansion

Hire 7-8: Warehouse + logistics operations (if building own)

Hire 9: Content / Social media
  → Not as early as most D2C founders think

Hire 10: Finance + data analytics
  → Unit economics visibility becomes critical at scale
```

**The key insight for D2C**: Operations before marketing. The biggest mistake D2C founders make is spending ₹50 Lakh on digital advertising before they have reliable inventory, consistent product quality, and a functional returns/customer service process. Acquire 100 customers; serve them perfectly; then scale.

### Marketplace / Two-Sided Platform Hiring Order

```
Marketplace: The core problem is the chicken-and-egg problem.
Both sides must scale simultaneously.

Hire 1: Supply-side acquisitions person
  → Immediately. The supply side (service providers, sellers,
    hosts) is almost always the constraint and the harder side.

Hire 2: Tech lead / engineer
  → Lightweight platform first; manual matching is acceptable early

Hire 3: Demand-side growth person
  → Can be combined with supply initially; split when scale requires

Hire 4: Operations / Trust & Safety
  → Marketplace integrity is a product feature, not an afterthought
  → One bad actor experience can kill early word-of-mouth

Hire 5-6: Engineers #2-3

Hire 7: Customer support lead
  → Marketplaces generate 3-5x more support contacts than
    equivalent SaaS; invest early or you will drown

Hire 8: Data / Analytics
  → Marketplace metrics (liquidity, fill rate, take rate,
    repeat rate by cohort) require real data infrastructure early

Hire 9-10: Category expansion (supply) or growth (demand)
  → Depends on which side is the current constraint
```

## The Equity Question: ESOP Ranges for Early Employees

This is the most practically useful and most poorly executed aspect of early hiring. Indian founders make two recurring mistakes:

**Mistake 1**: Giving too much equity to early engineers (because engineers are easier for technical founders to evaluate, and founders feel generous toward "their tribe")

**Mistake 2**: Giving too little equity to early non-technical hires (operations, sales, design) because founders undervalue these functions relative to engineering

Here is a data-informed framework for ESOP grants for the first 10 employees:

### ESOP Benchmark Table (India, 2026)

All figures as percentage of fully diluted shares, on standard 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff.

| Role | Seed Stage (Pre-Series A) | Series A Stage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| CTO (not co-founder) | 2.0–4.0% | 0.75–1.5% | Should be treated as co-founder economically |
| VP Engineering (first) | 0.5–1.5% | 0.25–0.75% | — |
| Engineer #2 or #3 | 0.2–0.75% | 0.1–0.4% | — |
| Engineers #4–10 | 0.1–0.3% | 0.05–0.2% | — |
| First Product Manager | 0.5–1.5% | 0.2–0.75% | High range if PM is defining the product direction |
| First Designer | 0.25–0.75% | 0.1–0.3% | Often undercompensated; should be closer to PM range |
| First Sales (AE / BD) | 0.25–0.75% | 0.1–0.35% | Often gets too little; critical early hire |
| VP Sales (first revenue leader) | 0.75–2.0% | 0.3–0.8% | — |
| Operations Lead | 0.25–0.75% | 0.1–0.35% | — |
| Finance / CFO (early) | 0.5–1.5% | 0.2–0.6% | — |
| Head of Marketing | 0.3–0.8% | 0.15–0.4% | — |
| Head of Customer Success | 0.25–0.6% | 0.1–0.3% | — |

**The 1-year cliff explained**: The equity vests monthly over 4 years, but the first 12 months' worth of equity all vests at the 12-month mark. If someone leaves in month 11, they vest nothing. If they leave in month 13, they have vested 1/4 of their total grant. This protects against early departures while rewarding staying.

**The post-cliff acceleration question**: Some startups offer "double trigger acceleration" (all unvested equity accelerates if there's an acquisition AND the employee is let go). For senior early hires, this is increasingly standard and the right thing to offer.

### The ESOP Pool: How Much to Reserve

For a pre-Series A startup:
- **Standard ESOP pool at incorporation**: 10–15% of fully diluted shares
- **At Series A**: Investors will typically require a refresh to 15–20% of post-round fully diluted shares
- **Common mistake**: Not expanding the ESOP pool before a round. Investors will require expansion as a condition of closing — but if you negotiate this poorly, the expansion comes entirely from founder dilution

```
ESOP pool mechanics at Series A:

Pre-round cap table:
  Founders: 75%
  Angels: 15%
  ESOP pool: 10%
  Total: 100%

Series A: ₹20 Crore at ₹100 Crore pre-money (20% dilution)

If investors require 20% post-round ESOP pool:
  The pool expansion happens BEFORE the investment (standard practice)

Without understanding this: founders think they dilute 20% from investment
With understanding: founders actually dilute ~28–30% total (investment + pool refresh)

Post-round cap table:
  Founders: ~55–57%
  Angels: ~11–12%
  ESOP pool: 20%
  New investor: 16.7%
  Total: 100%

Founders: started at 75%, ended at ~56% — 19 points, not just 20%
from the investment itself
```

## Hiring for "Startup Fit": What It Actually Means and How to Test For It

"Startup fit" is discussed constantly and measured almost never. Here is a concrete definition and a testing methodology.

### What Startup Fit Is Not

- "Worked at a startup before" (many large-company people thrive at startups; many startup veterans fail)
- "Young and hungry" (age is irrelevant; experience mismatches matter)
- "Willing to work long hours" (if your selection criteria is "will work more hours," you have a cultural problem)

### What Startup Fit Actually Is

Three specific capabilities that distinguish people who thrive at early-stage startups from those who don't:

**1. Decision-making under radical uncertainty**
Early startup work requires making decisions with 30–40% of the information you would have in a large company. Some people are paralyzed by this. Some people are energized by it. The difference is measurable.

**Test question**: "Tell me about a time you had to make an important decision without enough information. What did you do, and what happened?"

Warning signs: "I escalated to my manager" / "I waited until I had more data" / "I brought in consultants to advise." These are reasonable large-company answers that reveal poor startup fit.

Strong signal: Specific, structured approach to making a decision with incomplete information, including what they used as proxies for missing data, how they defined the reversibility of the decision, and what they learned from the outcome (whether right or wrong).

**2. Ownership beyond job description**
Early employees at startups have job descriptions that are essentially placeholders. The actual job is "whatever needs to happen." The person who says "that's not my role" is a culture killer at 10 employees.

**Test question**: "Tell me about something important that needed to happen at your last job that wasn't technically your responsibility. What did you do?"

Warning signs: "I informed the relevant team" / "I escalated to leadership" (passing, not owning).

Strong signal: "I just did it" followed by a description of how they navigated the organizational territory — not ignoring other people's responsibilities, but filling a gap proactively.

**3. Comfort with role evolution**
The job someone is hired to do at employee #5 is not the same job 18 months later. People who need defined roles, established process, and clear career paths are not suited to early-stage startup work — not because they are bad, but because they are optimized for a different environment.

**Test question**: "Where do you see this role in two years? What would you like it to evolve into?"

Warning sign: A very specific answer about career progression, promotion timeline, team size they expect to lead. This indicates someone thinking about their career, not about building the company.

Strong signal: "I don't know — it depends on what the company needs and where I can have the most impact." This is not indifference; it is the right mindset.

## What "Culture Fit" Actually Means (And the Hiring Mistake It Causes)

"Culture fit" is used to mean two different things, and conflating them creates two different types of problems.

**Legitimate culture fit**: Alignment with the specific decision-making patterns, communication norms, and values that define how your company operates. This is real and worth evaluating.

**Illegitimate culture fit**: "Seems like someone we'd want to hang out with." This is a proxy for demographic similarity — same background, same vocabulary, same references. It creates homogeneous teams that are blind to the opportunities and risks that come from diverse perspectives.

### What Legitimate Culture Fit Looks Like in Practice

Define your actual culture in behavioral terms before hiring. Not "we value innovation" — that means nothing. Behavioral examples:

| Cultural Value | Behavioral Test |
|---|---|
| Direct communication | "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager. How did you handle it?" |
| Speed > perfection | "Tell me about a time you shipped something that wasn't fully ready. Was it the right call?" |
| Data-informed decisions | "Walk me through a decision you made recently. What data did you use?" |
| Customer obsession | "Tell me about a time you went significantly beyond your role to solve a customer problem" |

The behavioral interview question is not about finding the "right" answer. It is about finding a real example that demonstrates the behavior — and then probing the example to understand whether the pattern is authentic.

### The Reference Check Reality

Most Indian startup founders do reference checks poorly: they ask the candidate's handpicked references, get uniformly positive feedback, and consider the box checked.

Effective reference checks:
1. **Ask for references you choose, not just theirs**: "Can you give me the name of a peer who worked closely with you, and also your manager from your last role?" The peer reference is often more revealing than the manager reference.

2. **Ask specific questions, not open-ended ones**: "On a scale of 1–10, how would you rate their ability to make decisions under pressure?" A reference who gives a 7 is telling you something. Follow up: "What would a 10 look like?"

3. **Ask about failure**: "Can you tell me about a specific project or situation where [candidate] struggled? How did they handle it?" A reference who cannot produce a single struggle example is either not thinking hard enough or is being evasive.

4. **The "would you hire them again" question**: "If you were starting a new company tomorrow, would [candidate] be on your list?" The answer (and the speed of the answer) is often more informative than anything else.

## Salary Benchmarks for Early Startup Hires in India (2026)

Compensation at early-stage startups involves two components: cash salary and ESOP. Here are the benchmarks for both.

### Cash Salary Benchmarks

All figures in LPA (Lakh Per Annum), approximate 2026 benchmarks for Bangalore/Mumbai/NCR markets.

| Role | Pre-Seed / Seed | Series A | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Founding Engineer (Generalist) | ₹18–30 LPA | ₹25–45 LPA | Wide range based on experience |
| Senior Full-Stack Engineer | ₹20–35 LPA | ₹30–55 LPA | — |
| First Product Manager | ₹20–35 LPA | ₹30–55 LPA | PM with 3–5 years experience |
| UX Designer | ₹12–22 LPA | ₹20–35 LPA | — |
| First Sales (AE/BD) | ₹12–20 LPA + variable | ₹18–30 LPA + variable | Variable typically 30–50% OTE |
| Head of Marketing | ₹18–35 LPA | ₹25–50 LPA | Demand for growth marketers is high |
| Operations Lead | ₹15–25 LPA | ₹20–35 LPA | — |
| Customer Success Lead | ₹12–20 LPA | ₹18–30 LPA | — |
| Head of Finance / Controller | ₹20–40 LPA | ₹30–55 LPA | Post-Series A; CA preferred |
| Data Analyst | ₹12–20 LPA | ₹18–30 LPA | — |

**The salary vs. ESOP trade-off**: Early employees who believe in the company's upside should accept below-market salary in exchange for above-market ESOP. The implicit formula:

```
Fair compensation package:

Market salary = ₹X
Startup offer = ₹(X - salary_discount) + ESOP worth Y at exit

If company value reaches target:
  Total comp = salary × years + (ESOP% × exit valuation)

Example:
  Market salary for senior engineer: ₹45 LPA
  Startup offer: ₹30 LPA + 0.5% ESOP (4-year vest)
  Salary discount: ₹15 LPA × 4 years = ₹60 Lakh total discount

  For this to make sense, ESOP must be worth > ₹60 Lakh at exit
  0.5% of ₹60L = ₹30 Lakh (Series A valuation might be ₹100-200Cr)
  0.5% of ₹600Cr = ₹3 Crore (Series B valuation)
  0.5% of ₹3,000Cr = ₹15 Crore (unicorn scenario)

  The asymmetry is the point: capped downside (₹60L forgone salary),
  uncapped upside (₹3–15 Crore potential)
```

## The Offer Letter vs. Employment Agreement: What Must Be in Writing

Most Indian startup founders issue an offer letter and consider the hire complete. The offer letter is not a legally sufficient document for ESOP grants and other equity commitments. Here is what you actually need.

### The Offer Letter (Initial Document)

What the offer letter must contain:
- Designation and department
- Location / remote policy
- Compensation (base salary, variable structure if applicable)
- ESOP grant (number of options, vesting schedule, exercise price — even preliminary)
- Start date
- Conditions of employment (background check, reference checks, etc.)

**What the offer letter cannot do**: An offer letter is an expression of intent. It does not create a legally binding ESOP grant. For the ESOP grant to be valid, the employee must receive an ESOP grant letter under the company's ESOP Plan.

### The Employment Agreement (The Document That Actually Binds)

Every employee — but especially the first 10 — must sign a comprehensive employment agreement that includes:

**1. ESOP Grant Terms (Incorporated by Reference)**
The employment agreement should reference the ESOP plan document and the specific ESOP grant letter. The ESOP grant letter must include:
- Exact number of options granted
- Exercise price (strike price) — must be at FMV at grant date for compliance
- Vesting schedule (start date, cliff, monthly vesting post-cliff)
- Conditions for vesting acceleration (if any)
- Treatment of unvested options on termination (typically cancelled) and on acquisition (typically partially or fully accelerated)
- Exercise window post-termination (typically 90 days to exercise vested options)

**2. Intellectual Property Assignment**
All IP created during employment belongs to the company. This clause must be explicit: "All inventions, developments, software, writings, and other work product created in connection with the employee's duties are assigned to the company."

This is the clause that creates problems years later if not done properly. A founding engineer who leaves and claims they own code they wrote "in their personal time" — but on company equipment, during company hours, in service of company products — creates a nightmare.

**3. Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)**
Standard for all early employees. Should cover: company confidential information, customer information, investor information, product roadmap, financial information.

**4. Non-Solicitation Agreement**
Post-termination restriction on soliciting company employees or customers. In India, this is enforceable if limited in time (typically 12–24 months) and scope. A non-solicitation that is too broad may be held unenforceable by Indian courts.

**5. Notice Period**
Standard for Indian employment. Typically 1–3 months for senior employees. Match the notice period to the criticality and replacement difficulty of the role.

**Note on non-competes**: Non-compete clauses (restrictions on working for competitors post-employment) are generally not enforceable in India under Section 27 of the Contract Act, which restricts agreements in restraint of trade. Some courts have enforced narrow non-competes during active employment, but post-termination non-competes are largely unenforceable. Do not rely on them for competitive protection.

## The Hardest Early Hire Decision: Letting Go

Knowing when and how to let go of an early employee who isn't working out is the decision that costs Indian founders the most time and the most money — because they almost always delay it.

### The "Brilliant Friend" Problem

Early employees often become friends. The professional relationship deepens into a personal one. Founders feel loyalty and personal attachment. This is natural and often healthy — until the performance problem arises.

The performance problem is easier to handle with a new employee (who hasn't become a close colleague) than with someone you've worked alongside for 18 months, attended weddings with, and shared genuine startup struggles with. The emotional weight delays the decision by months.

**The cost of delay**: Every month an underperforming early employee continues in role:
- Direct: salary being paid for below-expectations output
- Indirect: team morale among the people who see the non-performance
- Strategic: decisions being made by someone who isn't right for the role
- Opportunity: the seat is occupied, preventing you from hiring the right person

### The Performance Conversation Framework

For an early employee showing declining performance or culture mismatch:

**Month 1**: Have the direct conversation. Be specific. Not "I'm worried about your performance" but "Here are the three specific areas where I need to see different behavior or different results. Here is what success looks like in each area. I want to revisit in 30 days."

**Month 2**: The 30-day check-in. If improvement is happening, acknowledge it explicitly. If not, be direct: "I don't think this is working the way we both hoped. I want to find a resolution that is fair to both of us."

**Month 3 (if no improvement)**: The parting conversation. Early employees deserve honest conversations, generous severance (even when not legally required), and privacy — not formal HR processes or public performance management systems.

### What "Generous Severance" Looks Like for Early Employees

India's Payment of Gratuity Act requires gratuity payment after 5 years of service. For early employees departing before 5 years, there is no legal obligation for severance beyond the notice period payout. But the right answer for your company reputation and your relationship with your early startup community is:

| Tenure | Minimum Legal | Right Practice |
|---|---|---|
| < 1 year | Notice period only | 1–2 months additional |
| 1–2 years | Notice period only | 2–3 months additional |
| 2–5 years | Notice period + consideration | 3–6 months |
| > 5 years | Gratuity + notice | Negotiate based on contribution |

Accelerating some unvested ESOP for departing early employees (even when there is no contractual obligation) is increasingly common at well-run Indian startups. It is the right thing to do, and it generates enormous goodwill in the startup community where word travels fast.

### When to Act Fast vs. When to Create a Plan

Not every early employee separation requires a 90-day PIP (Performance Improvement Plan). Two scenarios require immediate action:

**Scenario 1: Ethics or integrity violation**
Any confirmed lie, misrepresentation, theft, or serious dishonesty. Do not manage this over months. Act within days. The message to your team is more important than the cost of any single individual.

**Scenario 2: Toxic culture impact**
An early employee who is individually high-performing but creating a toxic environment for others — dismissive, credit-stealing, condescending — requires action faster than their personal performance might suggest. The cultural damage compounds with every week of delay.

## A Hiring Checklist for the First 10

Before making each of the first 10 hires, confirm:

```
Pre-Hire Checklist:

  Role Definition
  ✓ Clear job description (not just a title)
  ✓ Success metrics for 30/60/90 days defined
  ✓ Team structure around this hire clear
  ✓ ESOP grant size agreed among co-founders before making offer

  Process
  ✓ At least 3 structured interviews (not just casual coffee chats)
  ✓ At least one interview with a peer (someone at their level)
  ✓ Work sample test or case study for critical roles
  ✓ Reference checks completed (not just candidate's preferred references)

  Legal
  ✓ Offer letter sent and signed before start date
  ✓ Employment agreement signed (not just offer letter)
  ✓ ESOP grant letter issued under ESOP plan
  ✓ ESOP plan itself formally adopted by Board (many startups forget this)
  ✓ IP assignment clause in employment agreement
  ✓ NDA in place

  Onboarding
  ✓ First week schedule prepared
  ✓ Key people they need to meet in first 2 weeks identified
  ✓ Their primary success metric for month 1 communicated explicitly
```
