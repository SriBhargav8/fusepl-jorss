# Runway Calculator: When Does Your Startup Actually Die?

The most important number in a startup is not your valuation. It's not your ARR multiple or your NPS score. It's the date when your bank account hits zero.

Call it cash-zero date. Call it runway. Call it "the deadline" — but know it precisely.

Most founders don't. They know they have "about 12 months of runway" because their last bank statement divided by last month's expenses said so. They don't know they're actually 8 months from zero because of three upcoming expenses they haven't modelled.

This guide shows you how to calculate your actual runway, the most common errors founders make, and what to do when the number is too small.

---

## Why Average Burn Rate Lies to You

**The naive calculation:**

```
Runway = Cash in bank / Average monthly burn
= ₹1,20,00,000 / ₹10,00,000 = 12 months
```

This is wrong for most startups because:

1. **Burn isn't flat.** You're hiring, so burn is increasing.
2. **One-time payments aren't monthly.** Office deposit, annual software licenses, advance rent.
3. **Revenue isn't steady.** B2B payments come in batches; seasonal businesses have dry months.
4. **You haven't included the runway consumed by fundraising.** The next round takes 3–6 months to close, minimum.

The average burn calculation would fail to capture any of these — and each one can shorten your runway by 1–3 months.

## The Right Way to Calculate Runway

### Step 1: Build a Month-by-Month Cash Flow Model

Don't use averages. Build it month by month, 18 months out.

**For each month, model:**

**Cash Inflows:**
- Revenue collected (not invoiced — collected)
- Existing investment proceeds not yet deployed
- Any government grants or non-dilutive income

**Cash Outflows:**
- Salaries (current headcount × CTC/12, plus any planned hires)
- Rent and utilities
- Software subscriptions (identify annual ones — divide by 12 AND mark the lump sum month)
- Marketing spend
- Cloud/infrastructure
- Contractor/agency fees
- Legal and CA fees
- Travel
- One-time items: office refurbishment, equipment purchases, conference sponsorships

**Net Cash Flow = Inflows − Outflows**
**Cumulative Cash = Opening cash + Sum of net cash flows**

Your cash-zero date is the month when cumulative cash first goes negative.

### Step 2: Identify All Non-Monthly Expenses

This is what most founders miss. Make a list of every expense that does NOT hit your P&L evenly every month:

| Expense Type | Typical Indian Startup Examples |
|---|---|
| Annual software | AWS annual, Salesforce, Zoho, GitHub, Jira |
| Quarterly rent advance | Many Indian commercial leases require 3-month advance |
| Tax payments | Advance tax: June 15, Sep 15, Dec 15, Mar 15 |
| TDS deposits | Monthly, but sometimes missed and paid in bulk |
| ROC/MCA filing fees | Annual |
| Audit fees | Paid once a year |
| Conference/event costs | Lumpy — identify specific events |
| Equipment purchases | Laptops for new hires |
| Security deposits | If you're moving offices |

Add all of these to their specific month in your model. Not averaged.

### Step 3: Model Your Hiring Plan

Salaries are your largest expense and they're not flat — they grow as you hire.

For each planned hire:
- Month they join
- Monthly cost (CTC/12, including employer PF contribution which is ~12% of basic)
- Impact on runway from that month forward

**The compounding effect of hiring:**

| Month | Headcount | Monthly Salary Burn |
|---|---|---|
| Month 1 | 5 people | ₹4.5L |
| Month 3 | +2 hires | ₹6.3L |
| Month 6 | +3 hires | ₹9L |
| Month 9 | +2 hires | ₹10.8L |
| Month 12 | +2 hires | ₹12.6L |

If you built your runway on "₹4.5L monthly burn," you're actually at ₹12.6L by month 12. That 12-month runway is actually 6–7 months of real runway.

### Step 4: Model Revenue Conservatively

Most founders model revenue optimistically. The rule of thumb for runway modelling: **use 50–60% of your projected revenue.**

Why? Because:
- Enterprise deals slip by 2–3 months routinely
- Pilots don't convert to paid at the rate you project
- Churn happens and you didn't model it
- Payment terms in India often mean 45–90 day collection delays

For runway purposes, assume revenue arrives later and is smaller than your projection. If you're right, great — extra runway. If you're wrong, you're prepared.

## The Real Runway Formula

```
Real Runway = Cash-zero date (from month-by-month model)
              MINUS 3 months (fundraising lead time)
              MINUS 1 month (buffer)

Effective Fundraising Deadline = Today + Real Runway
```

**Example:**

Month-by-month model says cash-zero = Month 14 from today.
Subtract 3 months fundraising time = Month 11.
Subtract 1 month buffer = Month 10.

**You need to start your next fundraise in Month 10 — which is 10 months from today.**

That means you have 10 months to build the traction that justifies the next round. Not 14. **10.**

## Benchmarks: What's "Healthy" Runway

| Stage | Minimum Comfortable Runway | What You Should Aim For |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | 12 months | 18 months |
| Seed | 15 months | 18–24 months |
| Series A | 18 months | 24 months |
| Series B+ | 18 months | 24–30 months |

**Why 18 months minimum?** Because you need 3–6 months to raise the next round, and you want at least 12 months of runway left when you start that process. 12 + 6 = 18.

**Why founders undershoot:** They raise the minimum to hit the next milestone. But if the milestone slips (and it always does), they're raising from a position of weakness.

## The 3 Runway Danger Zones

### Danger Zone 1: The "Last Month's Burn" Trap

Using last month's burn to project forward ignores growth in your cost base. If you hired 2 people last month, your burn is about to step up. Model forward, not backward.

### Danger Zone 2: The Revenue Offset Fantasy

"We'll be cash-flow positive in 4 months so we don't need to raise." This works if revenue is 100% predictable. It never is. Build the runway model assuming revenue is 0 — it's the stress test that tells you if you have enough capital buffer.

### Danger Zone 3: The Fundraising Time Blindspot

Founders consistently underestimate how long fundraising takes. In India, a seed round from first meeting to money in bank takes 3–5 months (longer than US because of regulatory requirements). Series A takes 4–8 months. If you start fundraising with 4 months of runway, you're starting from a position of extreme weakness.

**Rule:** Start your fundraise when you have at least 9 months of runway remaining.

## What to Do When Runway Is Shorter Than You Thought

### Option 1: Cut Burn Immediately

Identify the top 3 monthly expenses. For each one, ask: can this be deferred, renegotiated, or eliminated without killing the business?

Common cuts Indian startups make:
- Defer hires by 2–3 months
- Renegotiate office lease (landlord often prefers reduced rent to vacant space)
- Cut marketing spend on channels not working
- Move from annual enterprise software to month-to-month (more expensive per month but no large upfront)
- Defer non-essential travel

**The target:** Extend runway by 2–3 months without material damage to growth.

### Option 2: Accelerate Revenue

If you have prospects in the pipeline, push for faster close:
- Offer annual prepay at 10–15% discount
- Agree to smaller initial contract to get the PO
- Activate existing customers for expansions

**The target:** Add ₹5–20L of cash in the next 30 days from existing pipeline.

### Option 3: Start Fundraising Earlier

If you're at 8 months runway, start the process now. Yes, your metrics might be weaker than ideal. But running out of money is worse than raising at a slightly lower valuation.

The "wait until metrics are perfect" strategy only works if you have 18+ months of runway. Below 12 months, start.

### Option 4: Explore Bridge Financing

Before your formal next round, a bridge can buy 3–6 months:
- Convertible note from existing investors (fast, relationship-based)
- Venture debt from NBFCs (Stride Ventures, Trifecta, InnoVen — available for Series A+ companies)
- Revenue-based financing if you have recurring revenue (GetVantage, Velocity)

**Bridge caution:** A bridge solves the immediate crisis but doesn't solve the underlying problem. Use it to buy time for a specific milestone that enables the next round — not just to delay the inevitable.

## The Weekly Cash Monitoring Habit

Once you know your runway, protect it. The best founders review cash weekly:

**Weekly cash review (15 minutes):**
1. What's in the bank account today?
2. What's going out this week (payroll runs, rent, vendor payments)?
3. What's coming in this week (customer payments expected)?
4. Has cash-zero date moved? Why?
5. Any unplanned expenses this week?

This sounds like overkill. It isn't. Founders who discover a problem in week 1 have options. Founders who discover it in week 10 often don't.

## Quick Reference: Runway Calculator

Use this quick formula for a directional number (then build the detailed model for precision):

```
Estimated Runway =
  Current bank balance
  ÷ (Monthly salary burn × 1.3 growth factor)
  × 0.8 revenue haircut
  − 3 months fundraising buffer
```

The 1.3 growth factor accounts for typical burn increase due to hiring. The 0.8 revenue haircut is your 20% conservative adjustment. Subtract 3 months for fundraising.

**Example:**
- Bank: ₹1.5 crore
- Monthly salaries: ₹8 lakh
- Monthly other burn: ₹3 lakh
- Total monthly burn: ₹11 lakh
- Adjusted burn: ₹11L × 1.3 = ₹14.3L
- Monthly revenue: ₹3L → haircut to ₹2.4L
- Net burn: ₹14.3L − ₹2.4L = ₹11.9L
- Raw runway: ₹150L / ₹11.9L = 12.6 months
- Subtract fundraising buffer: 12.6 − 3 = **9.6 months effective runway**

Start fundraising in 3–4 months. Not in 6 months. Now.
