# GST for Startups: What Indian Founders Need to Know (Without the CA Jargon)

Most founders encounter GST in one of two ways: either they get a panicked message from a customer demanding a GST invoice they're not set up to provide, or they get a notice from the GST department because they should have registered months ago but didn't know it.

Neither is a good situation. Both are avoidable.

GST (Goods and Services Tax) is India's primary indirect tax, and it applies to almost every commercial transaction above certain thresholds. For startups, the decisions around GST registration, filing, and optimization are not just compliance checkboxes — they have direct impact on your cash flow, your pricing, and your relationships with B2B customers.

This guide explains everything a startup founder needs to know, in plain language, without assuming you know what "reverse charge mechanism" or "zero-rated supply" means before you read it.

---

## When Does Your Startup Need to Register for GST?

The answer is more nuanced than most founders realize. There are two thresholds — a standard threshold and a mandatory registration list.

### The Standard Registration Thresholds

| Business Type | GST Registration Required When Annual Turnover Exceeds |
|--------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Services (including SaaS, tech, consulting) | ₹20 lakh (₹10L in special category states) |
| Goods (manufacturing, D2C products) | ₹40 lakh (₹20L in special category states) |

**Special category states** (lower thresholds): Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Arunachal Pradesh, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Sikkim, Tripura.

If your startup is a pure-play service business (consulting, SaaS, digital services) registered in a normal state (Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi, etc.), you need to register for GST once your annual turnover crosses ₹20 lakh.

### Mandatory Registration Regardless of Turnover

This is where most founders get surprised. Certain business types **must register for GST from the first rupee of turnover**, regardless of whether they've crossed ₹20L or ₹40L:

**1. Inter-state supply of goods or services**

If you are selling to customers in states other than your registered state, you must register for GST — even if your total turnover is ₹5 lakh.

*Practical impact:* Almost every D2C brand, SaaS product, or marketplace startup sells inter-state from Day 1. If you ship from Bengaluru to customers in Delhi, you are making an inter-state supply. You need GST registration immediately.

**2. E-commerce operators and sellers on platforms**

If you sell goods through an e-commerce platform (Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho), you must register for GST regardless of your turnover. The e-commerce platforms are required to collect tax at source (TCS) and remit it, which requires your GSTIN.

**3. B2B SaaS and technology services**

If you sell software or technology services to other businesses who will claim input tax credit on the purchase, they will require your GSTIN. Without it, they either can't claim ITC or will stop buying from you. Practically, any B2B startup should register for GST well before ₹20L.

**4. Import of services**

If your startup imports services from abroad (using AWS, Google Cloud, Stripe, Zoom, Slack, any non-Indian vendor), the reverse charge mechanism applies — you pay GST on these imports yourself. This triggers registration requirements. More on reverse charge below.

**5. Casual taxable persons / non-resident taxable persons**

One-off events, pop-ups, conference sales, or founders who are non-residents operating in India.

### The Bottom Line on Registration Timing

For most startups, the answer is: **register for GST when you first earn revenue**, or at the latest, before your first B2B customer asks for a GST invoice.

The ₹20L/₹40L threshold matters primarily for B2C-only startups selling only in one state. Once you have B2B customers or sell across states, you need to register.

## The 4 GST Rate Slabs: What Applies to What

GST has four primary rate slabs plus an exempted category. Knowing which rate applies to your product or service determines how much you charge customers and how much you need to remit to the government.

| GST Rate | What Falls Under It (Startup-Relevant Examples) |
|----------|------------------------------------------------|
| **0% (Exempt)** | Fresh agricultural produce, basic foods, educational services, healthcare services |
| **5%** | Some processed foods, textile fabrics below certain value, specific pharma products |
| **12%** | Processed food products, some medical equipment, business-class air travel |
| **18%** | Most technology and digital services, SaaS, cloud computing, IT services, consulting, advertising, financial services, most professional services |
| **28%** | Luxury goods, automobiles, tobacco, aerated drinks (not relevant to most startups) |

### GST Rates by Startup Type

**SaaS and Technology Products:**
- Software as a Service: **18%**
- Custom software development: **18%**
- IT consulting and services: **18%**
- Cloud infrastructure services: **18%**
- App development: **18%**

**D2C Consumer Products:**
- Skincare and cosmetics: **18%** (most products)
- Health supplements / nutraceuticals: **12% or 18%** (depends on specific product — verify)
- Food products: **0%, 5%, or 12%** (depends heavily on specific product — fresh vs. processed vs. packaged)
- Clothing: **5%** (on value ≤ ₹1,000 per piece) or **12%** (value > ₹1,000 per piece)
- Footwear: **5%** (≤ ₹1,000) or **12%** (> ₹1,000)
- Home furnishing textiles: **5% or 12%** (depends on product)
- Electronic consumer goods: **18% or 28%** (depends on product)

**Services Startups:**
- Professional consulting: **18%**
- Legal and CA services: **18%**
- Marketing and advertising: **18%**
- Edtech / online education: **18%** (most courses; some educational services may be exempt — complex area)
- Fintech / payment processing: **18%**
- Logistics and transportation: **5% or 12%** depending on mode

**Important:** GST classification disputes are real and can result in back taxes plus penalties. If there is any ambiguity about the rate applicable to your product — particularly for D2C food, health products, or hybrid product-service bundles — get a specific opinion from a CA before filing.

## The Startup-Specific GST Advantage Most Founders Miss: Input Tax Credit

Input Tax Credit (ITC) is the feature that makes GST a potentially cash-flow positive element for B2B startups — if you understand and use it.

### What ITC Is

When you are a GST-registered business, the GST you pay on your **purchases and expenses** (inputs) can be set off against the GST you **collect from customers** (output).

```
GST Payable to Government = Output GST Collected - Input Tax Credit Claimed

If ITC > Output GST → You get a refund (or carry it forward)
If Output GST > ITC → You pay the difference
```

### What You Can Claim ITC On (Startup Context)

| Expense Category | ITC Available? |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| SaaS tools (CRM, analytics, project management) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Office equipment (laptops, servers, peripherals) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Office furniture | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Professional services (CA, legal, consultants) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Digital advertising (Google Ads, Meta Ads) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Co-working space / office rent (if GST-registered vendor) | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Business travel and hotel | Yes — 12–18% ITC |
| Internet and telecom services | Yes — 18% ITC |
| Employee salaries | No — salaries are not subject to GST |
| Employee meals / canteen | Partially restricted |
| Motor vehicles | Restricted (unless business of transportation) |
| Personal expenses | No |

### The Math: How ITC Changes Your Real GST Burden

**Example: SaaS startup with ₹10L monthly revenue**

| Component | Amount | GST Rate | GST Amount |
|-----------|--------|----------|------------|
| Revenue from customers | ₹10,00,000 | 18% (output) | ₹1,80,000 collected |
| AWS bill | ₹60,000 | 18% (input) | ₹10,800 ITC |
| SaaS tools (Salesforce, Clevertap, etc.) | ₹30,000 | 18% (input) | ₹5,400 ITC |
| Digital advertising | ₹1,00,000 | 18% (input) | ₹18,000 ITC |
| CA and legal fees | ₹20,000 | 18% (input) | ₹3,600 ITC |
| Co-working space | ₹25,000 | 18% (input) | ₹4,500 ITC |
| **Total ITC Available** | | | **₹42,300** |
| **Net GST Payable** | | | **₹1,80,000 - ₹42,300 = ₹1,37,700** |

Instead of remitting ₹1.8L in GST, this startup remits ₹1.37L — saving ₹42,300 per month, or ₹5.07 lakh per year in cash. That's the ITC advantage.

**Important condition:** You can only claim ITC if your vendor is also GST-registered and has filed their returns correctly. If you pay a freelancer or vendor who is not GST-registered, you get no ITC on that payment.

### ITC Cannot Be Claimed On

- Goods/services used for personal/non-business purposes
- Goods destroyed, stolen, or written off before use
- Expenses blocked under Section 17(5): food and beverages for employees, membership of clubs, outdoor catering, beauty treatments, life insurance, health insurance (unless mandatory under any other law)

## GST Composition Scheme: Is It Right for Your Startup?

The composition scheme is a simplified GST filing option for small businesses. Instead of the standard CGST/SGST/IGST structure with monthly filings, composition taxpayers pay a flat rate on turnover and file quarterly.

### Composition Scheme Rates

| Business Type | Rate on Turnover |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Manufacturers | 1% |
| Restaurant/food services | 5% |
| Other service providers | 6% |

### Who Qualifies

- Annual turnover ≤ ₹1.5 crore (for goods) or ₹50 lakh (for services)
- Must be registered in only one state (no multi-state operations)
- Cannot be an e-commerce seller
- Cannot supply goods or services outside the state of registration

### The Critical Tradeoff

**Under the composition scheme, you CANNOT collect GST from customers, and you CANNOT claim ITC on purchases.**

This makes the composition scheme economically unviable for most B2B startups, where customers need a valid GST invoice to claim their own ITC. A B2B customer who buys from a composition dealer gets no ITC — which makes you a more expensive option than a regular-scheme competitor.

**When the composition scheme makes sense:**
- Pure B2C food/restaurant startups with turnover < ₹50L
- Small offline goods businesses with no B2B customers
- Businesses where suppliers are also unregistered (so ITC is unavailable regardless)

**For most startups — SaaS, D2C, B2B services, any business selling to other businesses — the composition scheme is not the right choice.** Register under the regular scheme.

## GST Invoicing Requirements by Startup Type

Issuing correct GST invoices is a legal requirement. Incorrect invoices can result in ITC reversal for your customers and penalties for you.

### B2B SaaS Invoicing

When you sell a SaaS product to another business, they need a proper tax invoice to claim ITC. Your invoice must include:

| Required Field | Example |
|----------------|---------|
| Your GSTIN | 29AABCT1332L1ZX |
| Invoice number (sequential) | INV-2026-0247 |
| Invoice date | 01-Apr-2026 |
| Customer's GSTIN | 27XXXXX1234X1Z5 |
| Customer's registered name and address | As per their GST registration |
| Description of service | "SaaS subscription — CRM platform, April 2026" |
| HSN/SAC code | 997331 (for IT software services) |
| Taxable value | ₹10,000 |
| CGST @ 9% | ₹900 |
| SGST @ 9% | ₹900 |
| Total invoice value | ₹11,800 |

For inter-state supplies, replace CGST + SGST with IGST @ 18%.

**Key rule:** For B2B invoices above ₹50,000, e-invoicing (generation through the GST portal's IRN system) is mandatory for businesses above certain turnover thresholds. Check current thresholds as they have been progressively lowered — as of 2026, businesses with ₹5Cr+ aggregate turnover must e-invoice.

### B2C Invoicing (D2C Brands)

When selling directly to consumers who do not have a GSTIN (and thus cannot claim ITC), you issue a **retail/consumer invoice** (not a tax invoice in the strict B2B sense). This is simpler — you don't need the customer's GSTIN — but you must still collect and remit GST.

For D2C brands, the product price is typically presented inclusive of GST. The GST component is included in the price you show on your website.

**Example:** A skincare product priced at ₹590 (inclusive of 18% GST)

```
Taxable value = ₹590 / 1.18 = ₹500
GST (18%) = ₹90
Total = ₹590
```

You remit ₹90 to the government for each unit sold.

### Export of Services (Zero-Rated Supply)

If you export services — meaning your customer is outside India and payment is received in foreign currency — your supply is **zero-rated** under GST. This means you charge 0% GST to the customer but can still claim ITC on your input expenses.

This is one of the most favorable GST positions available. An Indian SaaS startup selling to US/European customers effectively has 0% GST on revenue but 18% ITC credit on most of its expenses.

**Conditions for zero-rating export of services:**
1. Supplier of service is in India
2. Recipient of service is outside India
3. Place of supply is outside India
4. Payment received in convertible foreign exchange
5. Service is not supplied in India by a foreign establishment of the same entity

For most SaaS or technology service startups selling to international customers, these conditions are met. Maintain proper documentation: bank remittance certificates (FIRC/BRC), customer contracts, invoices in foreign currency.

**To claim ITC refund on zero-rated supplies:** File a refund application on the GST portal. The government refunds the accumulated ITC. This can significantly improve cash flow for startups with high foreign revenue ratios.

## The Reverse Charge Mechanism: When You Pay GST on Your Vendor's Behalf

The Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) is a concept that surprises many founders. Under RCM, instead of your vendor collecting and paying GST, **you (the buyer) pay GST directly to the government** on the vendor's behalf.

### When RCM Applies for Startups

**1. Import of Services (Most Common for Startups)**

When you pay a foreign vendor for services — AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Zoom, Slack, Stripe, any foreign SaaS or service provider — the transaction is treated as an import of service. You must pay IGST at the applicable rate (usually 18%) under RCM.

**Example:** Your monthly AWS bill is ₹2 lakh.
- GST under RCM: ₹2L × 18% = ₹36,000
- You pay ₹36,000 directly to the government (not to AWS)
- You can then claim this ₹36,000 as ITC (if you are a regular scheme taxpayer and the service is for business use)

**Important:** Even if you're below the ₹20L registration threshold, if you import services, you may need to register for GST specifically to account for RCM liability. Consult a CA if this applies to you.

**2. GTA (Goods Transport Agency) Services**

If you use a transport company (not a courier like Delhivery or Blue Dart which have their own GST registration, but an unregistered goods transporter) for commercial shipments, RCM may apply.

**3. Legal and Advocacy Services**

Payments to senior advocates (lawyers) and legal services in some cases attract RCM.

**4. Unregistered Dealers (Limited Scope Post-2019)**

After 2019 amendments, RCM on purchases from unregistered dealers applies only in limited notified sectors. Verify current applicability with your CA.

### The Practical Implication of RCM for Startups

If you use significant cloud infrastructure or imported SaaS tools (which virtually every technology startup does), you have an ongoing RCM liability. This must be:

1. Accounted for in your GST filings (GSTR-3B includes a specific section for RCM)
2. Paid by the 20th of the following month along with your regular GST
3. Claimed as ITC in the same return (the ITC offsets the payment, so the net cash outflow is typically zero for registered businesses that can claim ITC)

Many early-stage founders are completely unaware of their RCM liability until they get a notice. If you're spending ₹2L+ per month on foreign cloud/SaaS services, this is a material compliance item.

## Monthly vs. Quarterly Filing: GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B

GST requires regular filings. The two main returns are:

**GSTR-1:** Details of all outward supplies (your sales invoices). Due on the 11th of the following month (monthly filers) or 13th of the month following the quarter (quarterly filers).

**GSTR-3B:** Summary return of all supplies and input tax credits. This is where you calculate and pay your net GST. Due on the 20th of the following month.

### Monthly vs. Quarterly: Who Files What

| Annual Turnover | Filing Frequency |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| > ₹5 crore | Monthly (mandatory) |
| ≤ ₹5 crore | Can opt for quarterly filing (QRMP scheme) |
| Any turnover with e-invoicing obligation | Monthly effectively (real-time invoice reporting) |

**For most startups in early growth stage (₹5Cr+ ARR):** Monthly filing is required. Build this into your operational calendar. GST filing has strict due dates with automatic late fees.

### Filing Due Dates

| Return | Due Date |
|--------|----------|
| GSTR-1 (monthly) | 11th of following month |
| GSTR-3B (monthly) | 20th of following month |
| GSTR-1 (quarterly) | 13th of month after quarter |
| GSTR-3B (quarterly) | 22nd/24th of month after quarter |
| Annual Return (GSTR-9) | 31st December of following FY |

**Late filing penalties:** ₹50 per day (₹25 CGST + ₹25 SGST) for normal returns, ₹20 per day for nil returns. Maximum penalty: ₹5,000 per return. Plus, 18% interest per annum on outstanding tax amount.

## Common GST Mistakes Startups Make (and Their Consequences)

### Mistake 1: Not Registering When Required

**Consequence:** Back taxes for all periods you should have been registered, plus 18% interest, plus penalty up to 10% of tax due (minimum ₹10,000). In cases of fraud or deliberate evasion, penalties can be 100% of tax due.

**Fix:** If you realize you should have registered earlier, voluntarily register now and file back returns. Voluntary disclosure typically results in more lenient penalty treatment.

### Mistake 2: Wrong GST Rate on Your Product/Service

**Consequence:** If you charged customers a lower rate than applicable, you owe the difference plus interest (you can't retroactively charge customers). If you charged a higher rate, you over-collected and must refund customers.

**Fix:** Get a written opinion from a CA on your specific product/service classification before you start invoicing.

### Mistake 3: Claiming ITC on Blocked Items (Section 17(5))

**Consequence:** ITC reversal (you have to pay back the credit claimed), plus 18% interest, plus potential penalties.

**Common blocked items startups mistakenly claim:** Employee meal vouchers, gym membership for employees, personal cab/car costs, employee health insurance (restricted), club memberships.

### Mistake 4: Not Filing Nil Returns When Turnover is Zero

If you are registered for GST but had no transactions in a period, you must still file a nil return by the due date. Missing nil returns attracts the same ₹50/day penalty as missing regular returns.

### Mistake 5: Missing the Annual Return Deadline

GSTR-9 (annual return) is due by 31st December for the preceding financial year. Late filing: ₹200/day (₹100 CGST + ₹100 SGST). Many founders forget this annual obligation.

### Mistake 6: E-invoicing Non-Compliance

If you are above the e-invoicing turnover threshold and issue invoices without the government's IRN (Invoice Reference Number), those invoices are considered invalid. The customer cannot claim ITC on them, and you face penalties. Check whether you are above the e-invoicing threshold.

### Mistake 7: Not Reconciling GSTR-2A with Your Purchase Register

GSTR-2A is the auto-populated record of purchases made from you by your customers (shown in your portal). If your claimed ITC doesn't match your vendor's filed outward supplies, your ITC claim will be disallowed during audit.

Best practice: monthly reconciliation of your purchase invoices against GSTR-2A.

## When to Get a CA vs. Manage GST Yourself

| Situation | DIY Possible? | Recommendation |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Simple B2C business, single state, below ₹20L | Yes | Self-manage with GST portal + Zoho Books or Cleartax |
| B2B business with < 20 invoices/month, simple tax rate | Possible | Use Cleartax or KDK GST software; CA quarterly review |
| B2B with > 50 invoices/month | Risky | Get a CA for monthly filings |
| Inter-state + export of services + RCM | No | CA required |
| Any business > ₹5Cr turnover | No | Full-time CA or finance team + CA |
| E-commerce seller | No | CA required from Day 1 |
| Complex products with disputed GST rates | No | CA plus formal tax opinion |

**Cost of a startup-focused CA for GST compliance:**
- Monthly filing services: ₹3,000–₹10,000/month depending on transaction volume
- Annual return + audit: ₹15,000–₹40,000
- Dispute resolution / notices: ₹15,000–₹1,00,000+ depending on complexity

The cost of a CA is always less than the cost of penalties, back taxes, and interest from non-compliance. For startups in any complexity situation, a good CA is not optional.

## GST and DPIIT Startups: Is There an Exemption?

A common question: does DPIIT recognition give startups any GST benefits?

**The answer: No direct GST exemption from DPIIT recognition.**

DPIIT recognition provides:
- Angel tax exemption (Section 56(2)(viib))
- ESOP deferral benefit
- Self-certification for 80-IAC eligibility
- Various government tender benefits

It does not provide:
- GST registration exemption
- GST rate reduction
- Exemption from GST filing requirements

All GST obligations apply to DPIIT-recognized startups exactly as they would to any other business of similar type and size.

## The 5-Step GST Action Plan for Early-Stage Startups

1. **Determine if you need to register now.** If you have B2B customers, sell inter-state, use foreign cloud services, or sell on e-commerce platforms — register immediately regardless of revenue.

2. **Choose the right scheme.** Regular scheme for almost all startups. Composition scheme only for simple B2C businesses under ₹50L.

3. **Set up your invoicing system correctly.** Use accounting software that generates GST-compliant invoices with the correct GSTIN, SAC/HSN codes, and rate breakdowns.

4. **Account for reverse charge.** If you pay for foreign services (cloud, SaaS tools), calculate and pay RCM monthly.

5. **File on time, every time.** The penalty for late filing accumulates daily. The cost of a CA to ensure timely filing is trivial compared to penalties. Set calendar reminders: GSTR-1 by the 11th, GSTR-3B by the 20th.

## Conclusion

GST is not the most exciting part of building a startup. But it is one of the most consequential compliance areas — both because non-compliance is expensive and because the ITC mechanism can genuinely improve your cash flow if you claim it correctly.

The founders who get GST right from the beginning are not necessarily more financially sophisticated. They're simply the ones who registered at the right time, got competent advice on their product's rate classification, built invoicing into their systems from Day 1, and filed consistently.

The founders who struggle with GST are almost always those who treated it as something to "deal with later" — and later turned out to be after the notice arrived.

Start early. Register properly. Claim your ITC. File on time. Get a CA for anything complex.
