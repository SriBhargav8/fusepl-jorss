# Section 80-IAC Tax Holiday: 3 Years of Zero Tax for Eligible Startups

For most Indian startups, tax planning is an afterthought — something to think about once profitable. That is an expensive mistake.

Section 80-IAC of the Income Tax Act provides eligible startups with a **100% deduction on profits for any 3 consecutive years within the first 10 years of incorporation**. On a startup with ₹5 crore in annual profit, the 25% corporate tax rate means ₹1.25 crore saved per year — ₹3.75 crore over 3 years. That is capital that stays inside your business instead of going to the government.

The benefit is substantial. The application process is non-trivial. And a surprising number of eligible startups either don't know about it, apply incorrectly, or miss the window because they crossed a disqualifying threshold before claiming.

This is the complete guide.

---

## What Section 80-IAC Actually Provides

Section 80-IAC was introduced as part of the Finance Act 2016 and amended subsequently. The core benefit:

> An eligible startup can claim a **100% deduction of profits and gains** from a business involving innovation, development, deployment, or commercialization of new products, processes, or services driven by technology or intellectual property.

The deduction applies for **3 consecutive assessment years** out of the first **10 assessment years** from the year of incorporation.

**What this means in practice:**
- If your startup is incorporated in FY2022-23, you can claim the deduction in any 3 consecutive years from AY2023-24 to AY2032-33
- You pay zero tax on profits in those 3 years
- You choose when to start the clock (more on this strategic choice below)

### What It Is Not

- Not an exemption from minimum alternate tax (MAT) — though Section 115JB was amended to exclude startups with 80-IAC certification from MAT implications in the claim years (verify current status with your CA)
- Not automatic — requires IMB certification (explained below)
- Not applicable to all income — only income from the eligible business; passive income (interest, rental income, capital gains from investments) is still taxable

## Eligibility Criteria: The 5 Requirements

To claim 80-IAC, a startup must meet **all five** of the following criteria simultaneously:

### 1. Incorporated as a Private Limited Company or LLP

Only private limited companies (under Companies Act 2013) and limited liability partnerships qualify. Proprietorships, partnership firms, OPCs, and public limited companies are excluded.

**Implication:** If you started as a sole proprietorship or OPC and converted later, the conversion date — not the original date — is your incorporation date for 80-IAC purposes.

### 2. Incorporated After 1 April 2016

Startups incorporated before April 2016 are not eligible, regardless of how innovative their product is. The 10-year window runs from the date of incorporation.

### 3. DPIIT Recognition

The startup must have valid DPIIT (Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade) recognition at the time of applying for 80-IAC certification. DPIIT recognition is the easier, self-declaration-based step. If you don't have it yet, [apply for DPIIT recognition first](/startup-india/dpiit-recognition-guide-india) — it is a prerequisite.

### 4. Annual Turnover Has Not Exceeded ₹100 Crore in Any Financial Year

This is the most commonly misunderstood threshold. The ₹100 crore turnover limit applies to the financial year for which you are claiming the deduction — and to the year in which you apply for IMB certification.

**The trap:** If your startup crosses ₹100 crore turnover before you have applied for and received IMB certification, you are disqualified. Several well-known startups in the 2021–2023 cohort missed this window by growing too fast.

### 5. Working Toward Innovation, Development, or Commercialization of New Products/Processes/Services Driven by Technology or IP

This is the qualitative criterion. The Inter-Ministerial Board (IMB) evaluates whether your business genuinely meets this standard. Service-only businesses, trading companies, real estate ventures, and businesses involving financial securities or lending do not qualify.

The IMB looks at:
- Whether your product/service is genuinely novel or significantly improved
- Whether technology or intellectual property is central to your business model
- Whether the business has potential for employment generation or wealth creation in India
- Whether you are solving an India-specific or global problem with a scalable approach

## The Two-Step Application Process

### Step 1: DPIIT Recognition (Prerequisite)

If you don't have DPIIT recognition, apply at [startupindia.gov.in](https://startupindia.gov.in). The process is:

1. Register on Startup India portal
2. Fill the online application (company details, nature of business, how you are innovative/scalable)
3. Self-certify compliance
4. Receive recognition certificate (usually within 3–5 working days)

DPIIT recognition is largely self-declaratory. You are not heavily scrutinized at this stage. Most eligible startups get recognition quickly.

**Cost:** Free

**Documents needed:** Certificate of incorporation, PAN card of company, details of business activities

### Step 2: IMB Certification (The Hard Step)

The Inter-Ministerial Board of Certification is constituted by the Government of India and includes representatives from DPIIT, the Department of Biotechnology, the Department of Science and Technology, and the Ministry of Electronics and IT.

Getting IMB certification is meaningfully harder than getting DPIIT recognition. The board evaluates the quality of innovation, not just whether you meet baseline criteria.

**How to apply:**

1. Log in to Startup India portal with your DPIIT-recognized startup credentials
2. Navigate to Tax Exemption section
3. Submit the 80-IAC application with required documents
4. The IMB reviews, may ask for an in-person/video presentation
5. If approved, you receive a certificate that entitles you to claim the deduction in your IT returns

**Processing time:** 60–120 days (officially). In practice, many startups report 4–8 months including clarification requests.

**Success rate:** The IMB has historically approved 55–70% of applications. Rejections are typically due to insufficient evidence of innovation, sectors that don't qualify (trading, lending, real estate), or applications that don't demonstrate scalability clearly enough.

## What the IMB Looks at: The 4 Innovation Criteria

The IMB's evaluation framework, as communicated through published guidelines and industry feedback, focuses on four dimensions:

### 1. Innovation Quality

Is the product, process, or service genuinely new? The IMB does not require patented inventions — it evaluates whether there is meaningful differentiation from existing solutions. A reseller of existing software does not qualify. A company that has built proprietary algorithms, novel hardware integrations, or a genuinely new methodology does.

**What helps:** Patent applications (pending or granted), proprietary technology stack, unique data assets, novel process design, published research.

**What doesn't help:** Generic IT services, white-labelling, standard e-commerce operations without proprietary technology.

### 2. Scalability

Can the business scale to a large size without proportional cost increases? The IMB is looking for high gross margin potential, network effects, or platform dynamics. A one-to-one professional services business (one consultant serves one client) is less likely to impress than a SaaS product serving thousands.

**What helps:** Evidence that revenue can grow without proportional headcount or COGS growth, platform architecture, API-driven distribution, data network effects.

### 3. Employment and Economic Potential

Does the business have the potential to create significant direct or indirect employment? The IMB is not looking for a specific employment number — it is looking for a credible narrative about how growth of the business would generate jobs.

**What helps:** Technology businesses that enable SME productivity (more SME employees benefit), platforms that create gig work opportunities, deep-tech that requires skilled Indian employees.

### 4. India-Relevance

Is the startup solving a problem specific to India or global in scope but developed in India? The IMB positively views India-specific problem solving (agricultural technology, vernacular language tools, low-cost healthcare access) but does not exclude businesses targeting global markets.

### Preparing Your IMB Application

The application asks for:

| Document / Section | What to Include |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Business Description | 2-page narrative on your innovation — what you built, why it's new, how it differs from alternatives |
| Technology Architecture | Diagram or written explanation of proprietary technology components |
| Market Opportunity | TAM/SAM/SOM with India-specific data |
| Team Details | Founder and key team CVs with relevant technology/innovation credentials |
| Financials | Audited financials for all completed years |
| Traction Evidence | Revenue growth, customer counts, pilot results |
| Employment Data | Current headcount, projected hiring plan |
| IP Portfolio | Any patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets documentation |

**One practical recommendation:** Hire a CA or legal firm with prior 80-IAC application experience for the IMB stage. The difference between a well-structured application and a poorly-structured one is often the difference between approval in Round 1 vs. a 6-month back-and-forth with clarification requests.

## The Tax Calculation: What You Actually Save

Section 80-IAC is a deduction under the Income Tax Act. The math is straightforward.

### Corporate Tax Rates Applicable

| Company Type | Tax Rate | Surcharge | Effective Rate |
|--------------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| Domestic company (revenue < ₹400Cr) | 25% | 7% (if taxable income ₹1–10Cr) | ~26.5% |
| New manufacturing company (115BAB) | 15% | — | ~17% |
| Company opting Section 115BAA | 22% | 10% | ~25.2% |

Most startups file under the standard 25% rate. Use 25.17% as a reasonable effective rate for calculation.

### Worked Example: ₹5 Crore Profit Per Year

**Company profile:** SaaS startup, incorporated April 2022, DPIIT recognized, IMB certified in December 2024. Plans to claim 80-IAC for AY2025-26, AY2026-27, AY2027-28.

| Year | Taxable Profit | Tax Without 80-IAC (25.17%) | Tax With 80-IAC | Tax Saved |
|------|---------------|----------------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| AY2025-26 | ₹5,00,00,000 | ₹1,25,85,000 | ₹0 | ₹1,25,85,000 |
| AY2026-27 | ₹7,50,00,000 | ₹1,88,77,500 | ₹0 | ₹1,88,77,500 |
| AY2027-28 | ₹10,00,00,000 | ₹2,51,70,000 | ₹0 | ₹2,51,70,000 |
| **Total** | **₹22,50,00,000** | **₹5,66,32,500** | **₹0** | **₹5,66,32,500** |

Over 3 years of accelerating profitability, this startup saves **₹5.66 crore** in taxes. That capital stays inside the business to fund growth, reduce the need for equity dilution, or return to founders.

### For Earlier-Stage Startups with Smaller Profits

Even at ₹1 crore profit/year:

## Strategic Timing: How to Choose Your 3 Years

Section 80-IAC gives you flexibility — you choose which 3 consecutive assessment years to claim the benefit, within the first 10 years of incorporation. This flexibility is valuable and should be used strategically.

### The Guiding Principle

**Claim the deduction in the 3 years when your taxable profit is highest.**

This sounds obvious, but many founders claim it starting from the first profitable year, even if profits are small in Year 1. A better approach:

```
Strategy: Wait until your growth rate implies rapidly scaling profits,
then claim the deduction to capture the full benefit.
```

### Practical Scenarios

**Scenario A: Claim Early (Years 2-4)**

Best for: Startups that expect profits to plateau or face uncertainty beyond Year 4. Also relevant if you're approaching ₹100 crore turnover and need to claim before crossing.

Savings: Smaller in absolute terms but certain.

**Scenario B: Claim Later (Years 5-7)**

Best for: Startups with strong growth trajectories where profits at Year 5-7 will be 5-10x what they were at Year 2-3. Maximize the absolute rupee saving.

Risk: Reaching ₹100 crore turnover before claiming. If there's a 40%+ chance of crossing ₹100 crore within 3-4 years, don't wait.

**Scenario C: Claim During Expected Peak Margin Years**

Best for: Cyclical businesses, or businesses where a major contract/product launch creates a profit spike. Align the 80-IAC window with the spike.

**The ₹100 Crore Trigger Rule:**

```
If you are within 24 months of crossing ₹100Cr turnover:
→ Apply for IMB certification NOW
→ Claim 80-IAC starting from current year
→ Do not wait for "better" years
```

A startup that delays and then crosses ₹100 crore has permanently lost the benefit. No appeals mechanism exists for this disqualification.

### The Loss Year Question

What if your startup has loss years — can you use those loss years as your 80-IAC claim years?

**Answer:** Yes, but it is suboptimal. In a loss year, you have no taxable profit, so the deduction provides no benefit. The deduction does not carry forward unused — you simply don't get a tax benefit that year.

However, if you have a loss year that falls within your 3-year claim window (because you need consecutive years), that year is "used up" without generating benefit.

**Best practice:** Try to avoid including loss years in your claim window. If your startup went profitable in Year 3, skipped to a small loss in Year 4, and became solidly profitable from Year 5 onwards — claim years 5, 6, 7 rather than 3, 4, 5.

## How to Actually Claim in Your IT Return

Once you have IMB certification, claiming the deduction is an annual income tax process:

1. Ensure your IMB certification is current and valid for the claim year
2. In your corporate IT return (Form ITR-6), claim the deduction under Chapter VI-A, Section 80-IAC
3. Attach the IMB certificate as documentary support
4. Your CA will handle the mechanics of the claim

**Documentation to maintain:**
- IMB certificate (keep the original; certified copies for the CA)
- Annual financial statements showing the eligible business income
- DPIIT recognition certificate (maintain active status)
- Records demonstrating the company has not crossed ₹100 crore in any year

## Common Mistakes That Disqualify Startups

### Mistake 1: Crossing ₹100 Crore Before Claiming

As discussed, this permanently disqualifies you. Plan ahead — the IMB application process takes 4–8 months, so apply at least 12 months before you expect to cross ₹100 crore.

### Mistake 2: Not Maintaining Active DPIIT Recognition

DPIIT recognition is not permanent. It requires maintaining eligibility criteria and, in some cases, updating information on the portal. If your DPIIT recognition lapses, your IMB certification may be impacted, and your 80-IAC claim could be questioned during IT assessment.

### Mistake 3: Company Restructuring

If you reconstitute the company (merger, demerger, conversion from LLP to Pvt Ltd, etc.), you may be starting the clock over on a new entity — losing the 80-IAC benefit earned under the original entity. Consult a CA before any structural change.

### Mistake 4: Receiving the Benefit After the 10-Year Window

You can only claim 80-IAC within 10 years of incorporation. If you receive IMB certification in Year 8 and your startup was loss-making until Year 9, you may only get 1-2 years of the benefit instead of 3. Apply early enough that you have flexibility.

### Mistake 5: Claiming on Total Revenue Including Non-Eligible Business Income

The deduction applies only to income from the eligible business (the innovative/technology-driven business described in your IMB application). Income from subsidiaries, passive income, rental income, or other business lines is not covered. Maintain clear accounting separation.

## How 80-IAC Differs from Other Startup Tax Benefits

The Startup India scheme includes several overlapping tax benefits. Here is how they differ:

### Section 80-IAC vs. Section 56(2)(viib) DPIIT Exemption (Angel Tax Exemption)

| Feature | 80-IAC | 56(2)(viib) |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| What it covers | Tax on profits | Tax on share premium received from investors |
| Who benefits | Operating company | Company raising equity capital |
| Requirement | IMB certification | DPIIT recognition only |
| Trigger | Profitable years | Any equity investment above FMV |
| Max benefit | 3 years of zero profit tax | Unlimited (on qualifying investments) |

The angel tax exemption (Section 56(2)(viib)) prevents the government from treating investor money above FMV as "other income" and taxing it. This is separate from 80-IAC and requires only DPIIT recognition, not IMB certification.

**Key point:** Get DPIIT recognition immediately. This gives you the angel tax exemption, which is relevant from Day 1 of fundraising. Then pursue IMB certification when you are approaching profitability.

### Section 80-IAC vs. ESOP Deferral Benefit

DPIIT-recognized startups can defer the perquisite tax on ESOPs for employees. Instead of paying tax at exercise, employees pay at sale or 5 years post-exercise or upon leaving the company — whichever is earliest.

This benefit requires only DPIIT recognition, not IMB certification. It is important for employee retention at startups. It is separate from 80-IAC.

### Combined Tax Planning Framework for Startups

| Stage | Priority | Benefit |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Pre-fundraise | Get DPIIT recognition | Angel tax exemption protection |
| Hiring phase | Implement ESOP scheme | ESOP deferral benefit for employees |
| Approaching profitability | Apply for IMB certification | Prepare to claim 80-IAC |
| Profitable years | Claim 80-IAC | 3 years zero profit tax |

| Applications requiring re-submission | ~25% of total applications |

**The cohort that had the most applications (FY2021-23) also had the most rejections.** Many founders who registered DPIIT-recognized startups opportunistically — without genuinely innovative products — tried to claim 80-IAC. The IMB has been stricter since 2022.

If your product is genuinely technology-driven and innovative, your approval odds are high. If you're operating a services business that applied for DPIIT recognition primarily for the benefits rather than because you're innovating, the IMB application will likely be rejected.

## The ROI of Applying

Applying for 80-IAC costs money (CA/legal fees, management time). Is it worth it?

**Fees for IMB application support:** ₹50,000–₹2,00,000 (depending on CA firm and complexity)

**Management time:** 20–40 hours (collecting documents, writing innovation narrative, preparing for potential IMB presentation)

**Expected tax saving if approved:** ₹75 lakh to ₹5+ crore over 3 years (depending on profitability)

The ROI calculation is not close. Even at the low end, ₹2 lakh in fees and 40 hours of time to save ₹75 lakh is an obvious decision. The only reason not to apply is if you're genuinely ineligible — in which case, the cost is a ₹50,000 lesson in what the IMB looks for.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I apply for 80-IAC before my startup is profitable?**

Yes, and you should. Obtaining the IMB certification before you're profitable means you'll have it in hand when you need it. You don't have to claim the deduction until you want to — but you do need the certification to be in place.

**Q: What happens if I get acquired before using all 3 years?**

The acquirer's entity typically doesn't inherit the 80-IAC benefit — it belongs to the original certified company. If the acquisition is structured as an asset purchase, the benefit likely lapses. If it's a share purchase and the original company continues to operate, the remaining benefit may continue. Consult a CA for acquisition-specific structuring.

**Q: I have both a Pvt Ltd and an LLP for different parts of my business. Which one gets the benefit?**

Each entity applies separately. Only the entity (Pvt Ltd or LLP) that has DPIIT recognition and IMB certification can claim the deduction — and only for income from the eligible business within that entity. You cannot consolidate across entities.

**Q: Our startup has foreign shareholders (VCs). Does that affect eligibility?**

No — the 80-IAC benefit does not have restrictions on shareholder nationality. The eligibility criteria relate to the company structure and business activity, not the nationality of investors.

**Q: What if our startup was incorporated as a public limited company?**

You are not eligible for 80-IAC. Consider whether a conversion to private limited is feasible before you become profitable. Conversion must happen while you still meet all eligibility criteria.

## Conclusion

Section 80-IAC is one of the most underutilized benefits available to Indian startups. The combination of the eligibility window, the IMB application process complexity, and the common mistake of waiting too long means that a significant portion of eligible startups either miss the window or fail to claim the full benefit.

The strategic priorities are clear:
1. Get DPIIT recognition immediately (also protects you on angel tax)
2. Apply for IMB certification as soon as your product/service is demonstrably technology-driven and scalable — ideally 1–2 years before you expect to be consistently profitable
3. Plan your 3-year claim window around your profitability trajectory, not default to the earliest available years
4. Watch the ₹100 crore turnover threshold — it is the disqualifying trip wire most founders don't see until it's too late

The tax saving over 3 years, even for a moderately profitable startup, is significant enough to justify prioritizing this well in advance of profitability.
