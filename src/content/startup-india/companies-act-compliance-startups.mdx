# Companies Act Compliance for Startups — The 12 Filings That Can't Be Missed

Rahul, the founder of a Bangalore-based SaaS startup, was in the middle of closing a Series A round in mid-2024 when his investor's legal team flagged a problem in their due diligence: ₹3.7 Lakh in outstanding MCA penalties, a deactivated DIN (Director Identification Number) for one of his co-founders, and two years of unfiled annual returns.

The round took three additional months to close. The penalties had to be paid in full. One co-founder had to go through the DIN reactivation process, which requires a physical appearance before a Registrar of Companies.

This is not an unusual story. In every cohort of Indian startups raising Series A, a significant percentage discover compliance backlogs during due diligence that delay or complicate their fundraise.

This guide is the compliance education that every Indian startup founder needs before their CA has to deliver this news.

---

## Why Indian Startups Fall Behind on Compliance

The root cause is structural, not negligence. Startups optimize for product, growth, and fundraising — as they should. Compliance is delegated to a CA, and most early-stage startups use a CA who does 50+ company accounts simultaneously. The CA files what is filed; what falls through the cracks often goes unnoticed until due diligence.

**Three specific patterns that create compliance debt:**

**1. The "founding period" gap**: When a company is incorporated, the first 12–18 months have the most filings (auditor appointment, initial share allotments, director KYCs) and the least process. Founders are focused entirely on building. The CA sends a WhatsApp asking for documents; the founder responds "will send tomorrow" for three months.

**2. The event-based filing miss**: Annual filings have predictable deadlines. Event-based filings (when you allot shares, add a director, receive foreign investment) have short windows — 15 to 30 days — that are easy to miss if no one is tracking them actively.

**3. The foreign investment blind spot**: Startups raising from foreign investors have additional FEMA-related filings (FC-GPR, FC-TRS) that sit at the intersection of RBI and ROC regulation. Many CAs handle one side but not both, creating gaps.

**MCA21 Enforcement Since 2022**

The Ministry of Corporate Affairs migrated to MCA21 Version 3 in 2022, which significantly upgraded the automated enforcement and reminder systems. The old system where penalties quietly accumulated without anyone noticing has largely been replaced by automated penalty notices, better tracking, and increasing coordination with income tax databases. The enforcement environment is meaningfully stricter than it was in 2019–2020.

## The 12 Annual Mandatory Filings: A Complete Reference

Every Indian private limited company — including early-stage startups — must file these forms every year. There are no exemptions based on turnover or stage unless specifically noted.

### Filing 1: Form AOC-4 — Annual Financial Statements

**What it is**: Filing of the company's audited financial statements (Balance Sheet, P&L, Cash Flow Statement, Directors' Report, Auditor's Report) with the Registrar of Companies.

**Deadline**: Within 30 days of the Annual General Meeting (AGM). The AGM must be held within 6 months of the end of the financial year. Financial year ends March 31. Therefore:
- AGM deadline: September 30
- AOC-4 deadline: October 30

**Penalty for late filing**: ₹100 per day of delay (no cap for companies with paid-up capital below ₹10 Lakh; ₹200/day for larger companies). These compound daily.

**What founders often miss**: The financial statements must be approved by the Board before filing. A Board meeting resolution approving the accounts is required. The Director who signs the Directors' Report is personally attesting to its accuracy.

**DPIIT startup relaxation**: DPIIT-recognized startups can file a self-certified compliance form instead of some reporting requirements, but AOC-4 is not exempt — it must still be filed.

### Filing 2: Form MGT-7 / MGT-7A — Annual Return

**What it is**: Annual return detailing the company's shareholding pattern, directors, registered office, and key changes during the year. MGT-7A is the simplified form for small companies.

**Deadline**: Within 60 days of the AGM (by November 29 if AGM is on September 30).

**Penalty for late filing**: ₹100 per day of delay.

**What it contains (and why founders must review it)**:
- Complete shareholding structure including beneficial ownership
- All directors with their DIN, PAN, and address
- All charges (loans secured against company assets)
- Key changes during the year (share allotments, director changes)

**Founder responsibility**: The MGT-7 must be certified by a Company Secretary in Practice (if turnover < ₹2 Crore or paid-up capital < ₹5 Lakh, the director can self-certify). You are signing this document personally — errors in shareholding data or director information create legal liability.

### Filing 3: Form ADT-1 — Auditor Appointment

**What it is**: Notice to the ROC of the appointment or reappointment of the statutory auditor.

**Deadline**: Within 15 days of the AGM where the auditor is appointed/reappointed.

**Context**: An auditor must be appointed within 30 days of incorporation (for the first auditor) by the Board. Subsequent appointments are ratified at the AGM. An auditor can serve a maximum of 5 years (one term) and then must be rotated (for companies above certain thresholds).

**Penalty**: ₹100 per day of delay.

**What founders miss**: Many startups run multiple years with the same auditor without formally filing ADT-1 for reappointment each year. This creates a gap that comes up in due diligence.

### Filing 4: DIR-3 KYC — Director KYC

**What it is**: Annual KYC for every director holding a DIN (Director Identification Number). Each director must submit their KYC details every year to keep their DIN active.

**Deadline**: September 30 every year. This is a fixed annual deadline, not tied to the AGM.

**Consequence of non-filing**: DIN is deactivated. A director with a deactivated DIN cannot sign documents, execute board resolutions, or appear as a director in any MCA filing until the DIN is reactivated. Reactivation requires filing DIR-3 KYC with a ₹5,000 penalty.

**Critical implication**: If your co-founder's DIN is deactivated, you cannot file any other MCA form (including AOC-4, MGT-7) until it is reactivated. One deactivated DIN blocks your entire compliance calendar.

**Process**: DIR-3 KYC requires the director's PAN, Aadhar, mobile number, email address, and digital signature. It must be filed by the director personally (not by the CA on behalf).

### Filing 5: DPT-3 — Deposits/Loans Return

**What it is**: Return of deposits and certain loans received by the company from directors, shareholders, and others.

**Who it applies to**: Every company that has received any loan, deposit, or advance — including from founders, directors, and investors — that is classified as a "deposit" under Companies Act definitions.

**Deadline**: June 30 every year (for the financial year ending March 31).

**Why startups miss this**: Founder bridge loans (money founders put into the company as a loan before formal fundraising rounds), convertible notes from friends-and-family, and director loans are often treated as internal matters. DPT-3 requires reporting these formally.

**Penalty**: ₹100–₹500 per day per officer responsible, plus potential criminal liability for officers.

### Filing 6: MSME-1 — MSME Payment Reporting

**What it is**: Half-yearly return disclosing outstanding payments to Micro and Small Enterprises (MSMEs) that are overdue for more than 45 days.

**Deadline**: Two filings per year: by April 30 (for the half year October–March) and by October 31 (for the half year April–September).

**Applicability**: Companies with a turnover above ₹40 Lakh are required to file. For most seed-stage startups below this threshold, this filing may not apply.

**Why it matters for funded startups**: Once your company crosses the ₹40 Lakh turnover threshold, you need to track which of your vendors are MSME-registered. Failure to pay them within 45 days triggers an obligation to pay compound interest at 3x the bank rate — and the MSME-1 filing exposes this.

### Filing 7: BEN-2 — Beneficial Ownership Declaration

**What it is**: Declaration of Significant Beneficial Owners (SBOs) — individuals who ultimately own or control more than 10% of shares or 10% of voting rights.

**Deadline**: Within 30 days of receiving a BEN-1 declaration from beneficial owners, or within 90 days of the date the beneficial ownership threshold is crossed.

**Why startups miss this**: Foreign investment often creates a layer of structure between the investing fund and the company. The beneficial owner (the individual GP or LP who ultimately controls the investment decision) must be identified. Many startups and their CAs don't trace through the ownership layers to identify SBOs, creating BEN-2 gaps.

**Penalty**: ₹1,000 per day (one of the higher penalty rates in the Companies Act compliance stack).

### Filing 8: Form INC-20A — Business Commencement Declaration

**What it is**: Declaration by the Directors that subscribers to the Memorandum have paid their share subscription money and the company has a verified registered office.

**Deadline**: Within 180 days of incorporation.

**Note**: This is a one-time filing, not annual. But it is mandatory and is the most commonly missed filing by early-stage startups. Failure to file INC-20A means the company technically has not commenced business — which can invalidate subsequent filings and creates significant legal complications.

**Penalty**: ₹50,000 on the company + ₹1,000/day on each defaulting officer.

### Filing 9: PAS-3 — Share Allotment Return (Event-Based)

**What it is**: Return of allotment to be filed whenever new shares are issued — whether for cash (fundraising round), ESOP exercise, or conversion of convertible instruments.

**Deadline**: Within 15 days of allotment.

**Why this is critical for funded startups**: Every funding round involves a share allotment. If PAS-3 is not filed within 15 days of the Board/EGM resolution allotting shares, the company is in default. Startup founders routinely close rounds, sign documents, receive money, and then fail to file PAS-3 because they think the CA is handling it — and the CA is waiting for them to return a signature.

**Penalty**: ₹1,000 per day of delay. For a large round (Series A), where months can pass before anyone notices, this can reach ₹1–2 Lakh.

### Filing 10: DIR-12 — Director Change (Event-Based)

**What it is**: Intimation to the ROC of appointment or resignation of directors.

**Deadline**: Within 30 days of the board resolution appointing or accepting the resignation of a director.

**Why startups miss this**: When an investor director joins the board post-round, many startups treat the Board Consent Letter as sufficient. The ROC must also be formally notified via DIR-12. Similarly, when a co-founder leaves and resigns as director, DIR-12 must be filed even if the relationship ends amicably and informally.

**Special case — investor directors**: Investment agreements frequently require the investor's nominee to be added to the Board as a condition of investment. If DIR-12 is not filed within 30 days of the nominee's appointment, both the company and the directors are in default.

### Filing 11: CHG-1 / CHG-9 — Charge Creation (Event-Based)

**What it is**: Registration of charges (mortgages, hypothecations, pledges) created on company assets with the ROC.

**Deadline**: Within 30 days of the charge creation (extendable to 60 days with ROC fee).

**Applicability for startups**: Relevant when a startup takes a term loan or working capital facility from a bank or NBFC, secured against company assets. The bank's security interest must be registered as a charge with the ROC.

**Why this matters**: An unregistered charge is void against a liquidator and creditors if the company goes into insolvency. Banks know this — they typically require proof of charge registration before disbursal of secured loans.

### Filing 12: FC-GPR — Foreign Investment Reporting (Event-Based, FEMA)

**What it is**: This is technically an RBI/FEMA filing (not Companies Act), but it is so commonly missed by startups with foreign investors that it belongs in this guide.

Filing with RBI's FIRMS portal (Foreign Investment Reporting and Management System) of all foreign direct investment received into the company.

**Deadline**: Within 30 days of receipt of foreign investment.

**Penalty for late filing**: ₹5,000 per day of delay, compoundable by RBI's enforcement division.

**What triggers FC-GPR**: Any equity investment from a foreign entity — whether a foreign VC fund, a Mauritius-incorporated entity, or even an NRI investing as a foreign entity (as opposed to on non-repatriation basis).

**FC-TRS (Transfer of Shares)**: When existing shares are transferred from an Indian resident to a foreign person (or vice versa), Form FC-TRS must be filed within 60 days. This covers secondary transactions — foreign investors buying shares from Indian founders.

## What Founders Must Sign vs. What the CA Handles

This distinction is critical. Founders often believe they can hand the entire compliance stack to their CA and maintain plausible deniability for errors. This is legally incorrect.

### What You Must Personally Sign

**Directors' Report (AOC-4)**: The Directors' Report within the annual accounts is signed by the Director. By signing, you are certifying the accuracy of the financial statements, the directors' responsibility statement, and various disclosures. This is personal legal liability.

**DIR-3 KYC**: You must file this yourself. The CA cannot file your KYC on your behalf — it requires your personal digital signature and Aadhaar-linked mobile OTP verification.

**Board Resolutions**: All significant filings (PAS-3, DIR-12, CHG-1) require a Board resolution, which directors must sign. A CA can prepare the resolution but cannot sign it.

**INC-20A Declaration**: Directors must sign this personally, certifying that subscribers have paid their share subscription amounts.

**FC-GPR**: The company secretary or authorized signatory files this, but it requires Director authorization and the filing is under the Director's responsibility.

### What Your CA Handles (But You Must Verify)

- Preparation of financial statements and Directors' Report
- Filing of AOC-4, MGT-7, DPT-3, MSME-1 on the portal (after you've signed the underlying documents)
- Calculation of due dates and penalty calculations
- Preparation of board resolution drafts

**The critical gap**: Your CA prepares; you sign. If you sign without reading, the liability is still yours. If your CA prepares late and you don't follow up, the penalty is on the company and directors.

## The Investor Due Diligence Compliance Checklist

When a VC does legal due diligence before a funding round, here is what they check in the compliance area. Use this as a self-audit before your raise:

```
Compliance Due Diligence Checklist (Pre-Fundraise):

  Incorporation Documents
  ✓ Certificate of Incorporation
  ✓ MOA and AOA (current, including all amendments)
  ✓ INC-20A filed (with payment proof)

  Annual Filings (check all years since incorporation)
  ✓ AOC-4 filed for each financial year
  ✓ MGT-7 filed for each financial year
  ✓ ADT-1 filed for each year auditor was appointed/reappointed
  ✓ DPT-3 filed for each year

  Director Compliance
  ✓ DIR-3 KYC filed for all current directors (no deactivated DINs)
  ✓ DIR-12 filed for all director appointments and resignations

  Share Capital
  ✓ PAS-3 filed for each allotment (match with cap table)
  ✓ Share certificates issued to all shareholders
  ✓ BEN-2 filed for all significant beneficial owners

  Foreign Investment (if applicable)
  ✓ FC-GPR filed for each round within 30 days
  ✓ Annual return on foreign liabilities (FLA) filed by July 15 each year

  Outstanding Penalties
  ✓ No outstanding MCA notices or SCNs (Show Cause Notices)
  ✓ No DIN deactivation for any current director
```

## DPIIT Recognition: What Compliance Relaxations Actually Apply

DPIIT recognition (under the Startup India programme) provides several genuine benefits — but it is not a blanket compliance holiday. Here is what is and is not relaxed.

### What DPIIT Recognition Relaxes

| Benefit | Applicable Regulation | What Changes |
|---|---|---|
| Self-certification for labour laws | EPF, ESIC, Contract Labour, Maternity Benefit, Payment of Gratuity Acts | Self-certify instead of inspection-based compliance for 3 years |
| Fast-track IP filing | Patent, Trademark | Reduced fees, expedited processing |
| Tax holiday | Income Tax Act | 3-year tax holiday on profits (with angel tax exemption for recognized investors) |
| Wind-up timeline | Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code | Fast-track 90-day winding up |

### What DPIIT Recognition Does NOT Change

- ROC filing obligations (AOC-4, MGT-7, DIR-3 KYC, PAS-3 — all still mandatory)
- GST compliance (registration and filing obligations remain)
- TDS deduction and deposit obligations
- FEMA obligations (FC-GPR, FC-TRS)
- Auditor appointment requirements

The most common misconception: "We are DPIIT-recognized so we don't need to worry about ROC filings." Completely false. DPIIT recognition is a tax and labour law concession — it does not touch the Companies Act compliance calendar.

## Building a Compliance Process That Actually Works

The problem is not that founders don't know they should file. The problem is that compliance calendars are easy to build and hard to execute without an explicit tracking system.

### The Minimum Viable Compliance System for an Early-Stage Startup

**1. Designate a compliance owner**
This must be a specific named person, not "the CA." The owner can be a co-founder, a CFO (at Series A+), or a dedicated operations person. The owner's job is not to file the forms — the CA does that — but to track deadlines and chase signatures.

**2. Build a shared compliance calendar**
A simple Google Sheet or Notion database with:
- Form name
- Due date
- Responsible party (who signs, who files)
- Status
- Penalty if missed

Share this with the CA and review monthly.

**3. Create signature turnaround rules**
"Any compliance document sent by the CA for signature will be signed within 48 hours." Put this in writing. Compliance delays are often caused by a signature sitting in a founder's email for 3 weeks.

**4. Pre-round compliance audit**
6 months before you plan to raise, ask your CA to run a complete compliance audit: every filing due since incorporation, confirm filed, identify any gaps, calculate and pay any penalties. Fixing compliance problems before investor due diligence is always cheaper and less time-sensitive than fixing them during.

## What It Costs to Fix Compliance Backdating

If you are reading this and already have a compliance backlog, here is what you should expect:

**Penalty calculation for a 2-year-old startup with common gaps:**

```
Hypothetical: Startup incorporated April 2023, raising Series A in 2025
Compliance gaps identified:

1. AOC-4 FY 2023-24: filed 90 days late
   Penalty: ₹100 × 90 days = ₹9,000

2. MGT-7 FY 2023-24: filed 60 days late
   Penalty: ₹100 × 60 days = ₹6,000

3. DIR-3 KYC: 1 director missed 2023 filing; discovered 2025
   DIN deactivated; reactivation fee ₹5,000

4. PAS-3: Seed round allotment filed 45 days late
   Penalty: ₹1,000 × 30 days = ₹30,000

5. FC-GPR: Foreign investor portion of seed round filed 45 days late
   Penalty: ₹5,000 × 15 days = ₹75,000

Total outstanding: ~₹1,25,000

Additional cost: CA fees to identify and file all corrections: ₹25,000–50,000
Legal fees to respond to any show-cause notices: ₹25,000–75,000

Total resolution cost: ₹1.75L–3.5L
Time to resolve (if all documents available): 4–8 weeks
```

This is a manageable situation. Where it becomes unmanageable is when gaps compound over 3+ years, when directors' DINs have been deactivated and the directors are no longer reachable, or when share allotments were never filed and the cap table in the ROC records does not match the actual cap table being shown to investors.
