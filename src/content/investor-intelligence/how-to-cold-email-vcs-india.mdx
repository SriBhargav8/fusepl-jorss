# How to Cold Email Indian VCs: Templates That Actually Get Replies

Let's start with the uncomfortable truth about cold email and Indian VC.

Most VCs in India will tell you privately that they don't fund cold emails. Their portfolios skew heavily toward warm introductions — founders introduced by trusted portfolio companies, advisors, angels, or other investors. At Sequoia India, Matrix Partners, Blume Ventures, Elevation Capital, and most top-tier funds, warm intro conversion rates are **10–15x higher** than cold outreach.

None of that means cold email doesn't work. It means cold email works differently, and most founders approach it wrong.

The cold emails that get replies are not longer, more detailed, or more persuasive than the ones that don't. They are shorter, more precise, and more specific about what the investor cares about. They land in the right partner's inbox on the right day and give that partner exactly enough information to want to know more.

This guide is the playbook: what to write, who to send it to, how to find that person, how to follow up, and what to do when you get the intro path instead.

## The Reality of VC Inboxes in India

Before writing a single word of your email, understand what you're competing against.

**Volume by fund tier (estimated, 2025-26):**

| Fund Tier | Monthly Inbound (pitches, emails, LinkedIn) | Partners per Fund | Pitches per Partner per Month |
|-----------|-------------------------------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Tier 1 (Sequoia, Matrix, Accel) | 1,000–2,000+ | 4–8 | 150–300+ |
| Tier 2 (Blume, Elevation, Lightspeed) | 500–1,000 | 3–6 | 100–200 |
| Tier 3 (Early-stage, micro VCs) | 200–500 | 2–4 | 60–150 |
| Angel Networks (LetsVenture, ah! Ventures) | 300–600 | N/A | N/A |

A partner at a Tier 1 fund reviews 200+ cold inbounds a month. They will spend 3–5 seconds on each subject line. If the subject line is interesting, they'll read the first 2 sentences. If those are interesting, they'll read the full email (if it's short) or scan for key metrics (if it's longer).

**The math of cold email:**
- 500 cold emails per month to a Tier 1 partner inbox
- ~100 get opened (20% open rate for good subject lines)
- ~25 get read past the first paragraph
- ~5 lead to calendar meetings
- ~1 leads to a term sheet per quarter (from cold email — most deals come through warm paths)

Your cold email must be in the top 5% of everything that partner receives. Not top 50%. Top 5%.

## What Makes a Cold Email Get Read

### The Subject Line

The subject line does one job: get the email opened. It is not a place for your company name, your full value proposition, or a request for a meeting.

**What works:**

Subject lines that work cite a specific, impressive metric or a recognizable reference point that will mean something to this specific partner.

- `₹1.2Cr MRR, 180% NRR — [Your Name], [Company]`
- `Grew from ₹0 to ₹3.5Cr ARR in 9 months — intro?`
- `[Mutual Name] suggested I reach out — [Company], ₹2Cr MRR`
- `B2B SaaS for Indian logistics — ₹1Cr MRR, 40% MoM growth`

**What doesn't work:**

- `Startup pitch — [Company Name]` (generic, screams cold email)
- `Seeking investment for innovative AI platform` (vague buzzwords)
- `I have an idea that will disrupt [huge industry]` (everyone says this)
- `Urgent: Investment Opportunity in [Company]` (spam-level)
- `Following up on my pitch from last month` (if they didn't reply, this won't work)

**The rule:** If your subject line could have been written by anyone pitching any startup, rewrite it. It needs a specific number or a specific reference that proves you've done something.

### The First Two Sentences

If the subject line gets the open, the first two sentences determine whether the email gets read or deleted. They must answer: "What does this company do, and why should I care?"

**The formula:**

```
[Company] [what you do for whom] [proof it works].
[One sentence on market or traction trajectory.]
```

**Examples:**

> "Kodo helps Indian SMBs issue and manage corporate cards for employees — we've onboarded 340 businesses in 8 months with ₹1.8Cr MRR and 3% monthly churn. We're raising a ₹8Cr Seed round to scale distribution."

> "We built a vernacular AI sales assistant for D2C brands — currently used by 60 brands doing ₹180Cr combined GMV, reducing their customer support cost by 45%. Growing 22% MoM."

Both examples tell you exactly what the company does, who it serves, and why it's working — in two sentences.

### The Length Rule

**Maximum 150 words for a cold email.** If you cannot describe your company and its traction in 150 words, you haven't thought hard enough about what matters.

Investors receive 2,000-word pitch emails. They receive 50-word emails. The 50-word emails get read. The 2,000-word emails get skimmed and deleted.

Long emails signal that you haven't done the work of distilling your story. Short, precise emails signal clarity of thinking — which is itself a data point about the founder.

## The Anatomy of a Perfect VC Cold Email

Every effective cold email has 4 parts:

### Part 1: The Hook (1 sentence)

What you do + one proof point. 20–30 words.

### Part 2: The Traction (2–3 bullet points)

The 3 metrics that prove the business is working. No narrative, no explanation. Just numbers.

Pick from:
- MRR / ARR (with MoM or YoY growth)
- Net Revenue Retention (if SaaS)
- Customer count + notable customers
- CAC payback or LTV/CAC
- Gross margin
- 90/180-day repeat rate (D2C)
- GMV / transactional volume
- Burn multiple or runway

Pick the 3 that are most impressive. Do not include metrics where your numbers are not good.

### Part 3: Why This Fund, Why This Partner (1 sentence)

Show that you are not spray-and-praying. Reference one portfolio company investment they made or one public statement by this partner that is relevant to your space.

### Part 4: The CTA (1 sentence)

One specific ask. Not "I'd love to share more." Specifically: "20-minute call this week?" or "Could I send you our one-pager?"

## Cold Email Templates by Stage

### Template 1: Pre-Revenue / Very Early Stage

Use when: MVP built, first users, no or minimal revenue. This is the hardest cold email to write because your best proof points are qualitative.

---

**Subject:** `[Your Name], [Company] — [one specific proof point]`

*Example: Priya Sharma, Agristack — 450 farmers on waitlist, Bihar pilot*

---

Hi [First Name],

[Company] helps [specific target customer] [do specific thing] by [how]. We launched a pilot with [notable customer or metric] — [result]. I'm building in [specific India problem] that [fund/partner] has [invested in similar company] in.

We're pre-Seed, raising ₹[X]Cr to [specific use of funds]. Would you have 20 minutes this week?

[Your Name]
[Phone] | [LinkedIn URL]

---

**Notes:** At pre-revenue, a pilot with a recognizable institution, a waitlist that signals demand, or a prior successful exit carry more weight than they would later. Use what you have. Don't fake metrics you don't have.

---

### Template 2: Early Revenue (₹20L – ₹2Cr MRR)

This is the most common stage for fundraising cold email. Use your traction as the anchor.

---

**Subject:** `₹[X]L MRR, [Y]% MoM growth — [Your Name], [Company]`

---

Hi [First Name],

[Company] is [one-line description] — [target customer] pay us ₹[price] to [value delivered].

- **₹[X]L MRR**, growing [Y]% month-on-month for [Z] months
- **[N] customers**, including [2 recognizable names if any]
- **₹[CAC payback or gross margin or NRR]** — [brief context if needed]

We're raising a ₹[X]Cr Seed round. I saw [fund/partner] led [portfolio company]'s Seed — we're building the [description] equivalent for [our market/segment].

20 minutes this week?

[Name] | [Phone]

---

**Notes:** Three bullet points, each a specific number. No sentences except the opener and the ask. If you have one notable customer name, include it. If your NRR or retention is strong, that belongs here. If it isn't, don't mention it.

---

### Template 3: Series A-Ready (₹2Cr+ MRR)

At this stage, the email can be slightly longer because the investor wants to see ARR, NRR, and growth trajectory. But still keep it under 200 words.

---

**Subject:** `₹[X]Cr ARR, [Y]% NRR — [Company], Series A`

---

Hi [First Name],

[Company] is a [category] platform for [target market] — we help [customer type] [achieve outcome] by [mechanism]. Revenue is largely recurring.

- **₹[X]Cr ARR** (₹[Y]L MRR × 12), growing [Z]% YoY
- **[NRR]% NRR** — customers expand from ₹[initial ACV] to ₹[expanded ACV] within 12 months
- **[N] enterprise customers** including [1–2 names], **[M] SMB customers**
- Gross margin [%], CAC payback [months]

We're raising a ₹[X]Cr Series A. [Fund]'s portfolio includes [Company A] and [Company B] — I believe we're solving a similar infrastructure layer problem for [our market].

Would you have time for a 25-minute call this week or next?

[Name] | [Website] | [Phone]

---

**Notes:** At Series A, include NRR — it is the metric investors most want to see for SaaS. If you have it and it's > 120%, lead with it. If your ARR growth is extremely strong (200%+ YoY), that number should be in your subject line.

## What Kills Cold Emails: The 8 Deadly Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Attachments in the First Email

Never attach a pitch deck to a cold email. Attachments trigger spam filters, slow down the reader (now they have to decide whether to open a file from a stranger), and signal that you don't know email etiquette.

The deck should be linked, not attached. Even better: don't mention the deck in the first email. Get the meeting first, share the deck afterward.

### Mistake 2: Over-300-Word Emails

Already covered, but worth repeating. Every word past 150 reduces your reply rate. If you have more to say, save it for the call.

### Mistake 3: Vague Problem Statements

"We're solving the problem of inefficiency in Indian SMBs" is not a problem statement. "Indian SMB accountants spend 12+ hours/week manually reconciling bank statements" is. Be specific. Vague problem statements reveal that you haven't done customer discovery.

### Mistake 4: "We're like [X] but for India"

This framing — "we're like Stripe but for India" or "think of us as the Robinhood of India" — used to work. It no longer does. Indian investors have now seen dozens of "X for India" companies. Many failed because they tried to copy US models without adapting to Indian market realities.

Instead: tell them what you do and for whom. The analogy can come up in conversation if it's genuinely illuminating.

### Mistake 5: Emailing the Wrong Person

Emailing an associate at a fund when you should be emailing the partner. Emailing a partner who specializes in fintech when your company is in agritech. Emailing the fund's info@/contact@ address instead of a named individual.

Cold emails to the wrong person have near-zero conversion. The research to find the right partner to email is worth every minute spent.

### Mistake 6: No Specific Tie to This Fund

"I am reaching out to leading investors" tells the investor you are sending this to everyone. A specific reference to their portfolio, their stated thesis, or a statement they've made publicly tells them you've done research. One sentence of personalization doubles reply rates.

### Mistake 7: Asking for Too Much Too Fast

"I'd like a 60-minute meeting to walk you through our pitch deck, company overview, product demo, and financial projections."

Too much, too soon. The ask in a cold email is for a 20-minute introductory call. That's it. One call leads to the next. Asking for an hour from someone who doesn't know you yet shows you don't understand investor time.

### Mistake 8: Following Up More Than Once

You send the cold email. You follow up once after 5 business days with a 2-sentence "bump" message. You do not follow up again.

A third follow-up email signals desperation and poor judgment about social norms. If they haven't replied after 2 emails, they've passed. Move on.

**The follow-up template:**

> "Hi [Name] — following up on my note from last week. [Company] at ₹[X] MRR, [Y]% growth. Would still value 20 minutes if this is relevant."

That's it. Eight words of context, a re-statement of the ask. Nothing more.

## How to Find the Right Partner to Email

This is the research work most founders skip. Don't skip it.

### Step 1: Build Your Target Fund List

Start with the funds most active in your stage and sector. For India in 2026:

| Stage | Active Funds |
|-------|-------------|
| Pre-Seed / Seed | Blume Ventures, Speciale Invest, 100X.VC, Antler, Titan Capital, Angel investors via LetsVenture |
| Seed to Series A | Matrix Partners India, Elevation Capital, Accel India, Chiratae Ventures, Lightspeed India |
| Series A to B | Sequoia Capital India, Tiger Global, SoftBank Vision Fund, B Capital, General Atlantic |
| SaaS-focused | SaaSBoomi angels, Stellaris, Bessemer India |
| D2C / Consumer | DSG Consumer Partners, Fireside Ventures, Sauce VC |
| Deep Tech | Speciale Invest, Exfinity, pi Ventures |

### Step 2: Identify the Right Partner at Each Fund

Every fund has investment themes, and each partner typically owns specific sectors. Emailing the partner who led a deal in your sector is 5x more likely to result in a response than emailing a random partner.

**Research method:**

1. Go to the fund's portfolio page. Find their 3–5 most recent investments in your sector.
2. Search Crunchbase, Tracxn, or Inc42 for those deals. The lead investor at the fund is often named in press releases.
3. Go to LinkedIn. Find the partner profile. Read their recent posts/activity for stated investment thesis.
4. Google "[Partner Name] [sector] investment thesis" — most senior partners have given interviews, written posts, or spoken at conferences that reveal what they care about.

**Example:** If you're building a SaaS product for Indian SMBs and you want to pitch Matrix Partners India, look at which partner led their investments in Zetwerk, Razorpay, or Stanza Living. That partner is more likely to engage with your email than one whose portfolio is consumer or deeptech.

### Step 3: Find Their Email

Most Indian VC partners have emails in the format:
- `firstname@fundname.com`
- `firstname.lastname@fundname.com`
- `f.lastname@fundname.com`

Most fund websites list general contact emails but not partner emails. Strategies to find personal emails:

1. **LinkedIn message first**: If you can't find the email and they're active on LinkedIn, a short LinkedIn InMail (140 character limit — use it) can work.
2. **Hunter.io**: Enter the fund domain to find email format patterns.
3. **Conference speaker lists**: VC partners who speak at TechSparks, Nasscom Product Conclave, etc. often have their email on conference programs.
4. **Their published bylines**: Partners who write for YourStory, Inc42, or their fund blog often have contact info.

## The Warm Intro Path: Getting Introduced Through Portfolio Founders

This is the most important section of this guide if you want to actually raise capital.

Warm introductions convert at 10–15x the rate of cold email. The best warm introduction comes from a portfolio founder at the fund you want to reach — someone the partner trusts, whose judgment they have tested, who can vouch for your quality as a founder.

### Step 1: Map the Portfolio

For each fund on your target list, build a list of 5–10 portfolio companies at a similar stage to yours. Not the companies that have already raised ₹500 crore — find founders at companies that raised their most recent round 12–24 months ago. They remember what fundraising felt like and are more likely to help.

### Step 2: Build Genuine Connections First

Do not approach a portfolio founder with "can you introduce me to your investor?" as the first interaction. That is not how warm intros work.

Approach them with something valuable first:
- Genuine engagement on their content (detailed, thoughtful comments — not "great post!")
- A useful introduction you can make for them
- A specific question about their domain where their expertise is relevant
- Feedback on something they've built

Build a real connection over 2–4 weeks before the ask.

### Step 3: The Ask

Once you have a real connection:

> "I've been following [their company]'s progress and really admire [specific thing]. I'm building [Company] in [space] — we're at [traction]. I'm exploring raising from [Fund]. Would you be comfortable making a warm intro to [Partner Name] if you think it's relevant? No pressure at all — I understand if it's not a fit."

**Three keys:**
1. Ask specifically (not "anyone at the fund" — say the partner's name)
2. Give them an out ("no pressure")
3. Make it easy (offer to write the intro email they can forward)

### Step 4: Write the Forwardable Note

When the portfolio founder agrees to intro you, send them a 3-sentence "forwardable email" that they can send directly to the partner:

> "[Partner Name], wanted to introduce you to [Your Name] who's building [Company] — [one-line description]. They're at ₹[X]L MRR, growing [Y]% MoM. I've spent time with [Your Name] and think this is worth 20 minutes. [Your Name] — over to you."

This makes it easy for the portfolio founder, frames the intro with the right context, and creates a smooth handoff to you.

## Tracking Your VC Outreach Pipeline

Fundraising is a sales process. Treat it like one. Manage it with a pipeline.

### The Outreach Tracker Spreadsheet

Build this in Google Sheets. Update it after every contact.

| Column | What to Track |
|--------|--------------|
| Fund Name | Sequoia India |
| Partner Name | [Specific person] |
| Partner Focus | SaaS / consumer / fintech |
| Intro Method | Cold / Warm (source name) |
| Date of First Contact | 15-Mar-2026 |
| Contact Method | Email / LinkedIn / Conference |
| Response Received? | Yes / No / Bounced |
| Meeting Scheduled? | Yes / No |
| Meeting Date | |
| Meeting Outcome | Pass / Interest / DD |
| Next Step | Follow up 5 days / Send deck / Wait |
| Term Sheet | Yes / No / Pending |
| Notes | Any context — what they said, their concerns |

**Review this every Friday.** Update all statuses. Identify who needs follow-up. Identify patterns (which sectors of funds are most responsive, which cold email subject lines are working better).

### The 3-Stage Funnel View

At any point in your fundraise, you should be able to say:

| Stage | Target Count | Current Count |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Outreach sent | 60–80 | |
| Response received | 15–20 | |
| First meeting done | 8–12 | |
| Second meeting / DD | 4–6 | |
| Term sheet | 1–3 | |

If your funnel is narrowing faster than expected at any stage (e.g., you have 20 responses but only 2 first meetings), that's the stage to diagnose and fix.

## The Fundraising Calendar

Cold email fundraising requires process discipline, not just good writing.

**Months 1–2: Outreach Phase**

- Research and build your target list (40–60 funds/angels)
- Identify the right partner at each fund
- Send 15–20 emails per week (not all at once — stagger to manage incoming meetings)
- Track responses and meeting dates

**Months 2–4: Meeting Phase**

- Run first meetings, send follow-up decks within 24 hours
- Identify top 10 most engaged investors
- Run second meetings, share data room access
- Create competitive tension (mention other funds in process, not specific firms — just "we have 3 other first meetings this week")

**Months 4–6: Term Sheet Phase**

- Receive term sheets, evaluate, negotiate
- Close with target lead investor
- Use lead as anchor to close the rest of the round

**The competitive tension rule:** Fundraising moves faster when investors believe others are interested. You do not need to lie. You simply need to be running a process with multiple investors simultaneously and being honest about that: "We're in parallel conversations with a few other funds" is always true when you've sent 40+ emails.

## India-Specific Nuances

A few dynamics specific to Indian VC that affect your approach:

**The conference circuit matters.** TechSparks (Bangalore, October), Nasscom Product Conclave (February), YourStory's events, and IIM alumni investor days are high-density environments for meeting investors. One strong 10-minute conversation at a conference is worth 20 cold emails.

**LinkedIn is more effective than email for initial contact with Indian VCs.** Many Indian VC partners are highly active on LinkedIn and receive less noise there than in their email. A 2-paragraph LinkedIn message that hits the same format as the cold email templates above can outperform cold email for certain partners.

**WhatsApp is for follow-up, not first contact.** Once you have a meeting scheduled or ongoing conversation, WhatsApp is acceptable and often faster. Using WhatsApp cold (e.g., from finding their number somewhere) is considered presumptuous and will usually get you blocked.

**Accelerator alumni networks are the most efficient warm intro path in India.** If the founder of a Sequoia Surge portfolio company will intro you to Sequoia, or a YC India alum will intro you to a YC partner, these carry more weight than most other warm intros. Invest time in these networks before you start your fundraise.

## Conclusion

Cold email is a numbers game with a quality filter. You need enough outreach volume to have a working funnel, but quality determines how far each contact goes in that funnel.

The founders who succeed with cold email in Indian VC do three things:
1. Research the right partner for each fund (not spray-and-pray)
2. Write emails under 150 words with specific metrics and one sentence of personalization
3. Run the warm intro path in parallel — because warm intros win

Treat fundraising as a sales process. Track it. Diagnose bottlenecks. Improve the message. Follow up once and move on.

And remember: the cold email is not your pitch. It is your ticket to deliver your pitch in person. The goal is a meeting. Everything else — the full story, the deck, the vision, the team — happens there.
