## The Yes That Wasn't a Yes

A Hyderabad-based B2B SaaS startup got a verbal commitment from a mid-size Indian VC in Q3 2024. The partners liked the product, the traction, the founder. "Send us your data room and we'll move to term sheet quickly."

The founder sent a Google Drive folder with the pitch deck, a basic P&L, and a cap table in a PDF.

The VC's associate spent the next three weeks sending requests: "Can you share the SHA from your last round?" "Do you have the last 12 months of bank statements?" "What's the status of your DPIIT registration?" "Can you share the cohort analysis broken out by acquisition channel?" "We'll need the audited financials for FY2022–24." "Do you have the employment agreements for the founding team?"

Each request took the founder 2–4 days to respond to. The process took 7 weeks instead of 3. The VC's enthusiasm cooled. The deal closed, but at worse terms than were originally indicated — partly because the slow diligence signalled poor operational organisation.

A complete, well-organised data room would have cut this process in half. This article gives you the exact structure.

---

## What a Data Room Is and Why It Matters

A data room is a secure, organized repository of documents that investors examine during due diligence. In the VC context, it is typically a Docsend folder, a Google Drive with controlled access, or (for larger rounds) a purpose-built virtual data room (VDR) like Digify or Datasite.

The data room serves three purposes:

1. **Legal and factual verification:** The investor verifies that what you told them in the pitch is true. Cap table, financials, legal structure, key contracts.

2. **Risk identification:** The investor looks for anything that wasn't disclosed — undisclosed liabilities, cap table complications, legal disputes, compliance gaps.

3. **Conviction building:** Strong data room documents — particularly cohort analysis, retention data, and detailed unit economics — build investor conviction beyond what the pitch deck communicated.

A poorly organized data room does the opposite of all three: it creates delays, it reveals operational immaturity, and it signals that the company may have operational problems that parallel the disorganized documentation.

---

## When to Start Building Your Data Room

The answer is: before you start the fundraising process, not after you get a yes.

The data room preparation process reveals problems. Missing documents surface compliance gaps you need to address. Organizing the cap table documentation often reveals discrepancies. Preparing the financial model for external scrutiny reveals assumptions that haven't been stress-tested.

**Target timeline:**
- Start data room preparation: 6 weeks before you begin investor outreach
- Complete data room: 2 weeks before first investor meeting
- Data room should be available to send: the day you get a verbal yes

## The 8-Folder Structure: Complete Indian Startup Data Room

### Folder 1: Company

This folder establishes the legal identity and corporate history of the company.

**Documents:**

| Document | Description | Where to Get It |
|---|---|---|
| Certificate of Incorporation | Shows the company's legal birth date and CIN number | MCA21 portal or physical copy |
| Memorandum of Association (MoA) | Company's founding objectives and authorized capital | MCA21 portal |
| Articles of Association (AoA) | Internal governance rules | MCA21 portal |
| All MCA annual returns (Form MGT-7/7A) | Last 3 years of annual filings | MCA21 portal or your CS |
| All MCA financial filings (Form AOC-4) | Audited financials filed with ROC | MCA21 portal or your CS |
| DPIIT Startup India Certificate | Recognition certificate if registered | DPIIT portal |
| DPIIT Form 2 (for angel tax exemption) | If availed of angel tax exemption | DPIIT portal |
| GST Registration Certificate | GSTIN registration | GST portal |
| PAN Card (company) | Company PAN | Income tax portal |
| TAN Certificate | Tax deduction account number | Income tax portal |
| Shops and Establishment Registration | If applicable (depends on state) | State authority |
| Professional Tax Registration | State-specific | State authority |
| MSME / Udyam Registration | If applicable | Udyam portal |
| Any industry-specific registrations | NBFC license, insurance broking, etc. | Respective regulator |

**India-specific flag:** Many early-stage founders haven't filed their annual returns with the ROC consistently. Late filing has penalties, but more importantly, it signals operational gaps to investors. If you're behind on MCA filings, clean them up before starting the fundraising process. A company secretary (CS) can help.

### Folder 2: Financials

This is where sophisticated investors spend the most time. The completeness and honesty of your financial disclosures directly correlates with investor trust.

**Documents:**

| Document | Seed Deck | Series A |
|---|---|---|
| Audited financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow) | Last 1–2 years if available | Last 3 years mandatory |
| Management accounts (unaudited monthly P&L) | Last 12 months | Last 18–24 months |
| Bank statements | Last 6 months | Last 12 months |
| GST returns (GSTR-1, GSTR-3B) | Last 12 months | Last 24 months |
| TDS/TCS returns | Last 4 quarters | Last 8 quarters |
| Income tax returns (ITR) | Last 1 year | Last 2 years |
| Accounts receivable aging report | Current | Current |
| Revenue breakdown by customer | Top 10 customers, % of revenue | Full customer list with revenue |
| Monthly MRR/ARR tracker | 12 months | 18–24 months |
| Unit economics model | Working model | Detailed model with cohorts |
| Financial model / 3-year projection | Working model | Detailed with assumptions |
| Cap table (see Folder 4) | Current | Full waterfall analysis |

**What VCs actually look at in the financials:**

1. **Revenue concentration:** If your top 3 customers represent > 50% of revenue, this is a risk flag. Investors will ask about customer-level revenue in detail.

2. **Gross margin trend:** Is it improving, stable, or declining? Declining gross margin signals unit economics problems.

3. **Burn rate vs revenue:** The ratio of monthly burn to monthly revenue tells the investor how much capital efficiency is being exercised.

4. **Cash position vs stated position:** Investors verify the bank statements match the claimed cash position. Discrepancies are immediate red flags.

5. **GST compliance:** GST returns are a proxy for revenue accuracy. Investors familiar with Indian startups know that some companies show higher revenue in investor materials than in GST filings. If your investor deck revenue doesn't match your GST filings, you have a problem that goes beyond data room preparation.

**The GST-revenue reconciliation:** Your investor will notice if your GST-liable revenue (from GSTR-1) differs significantly from your reported revenue. Prepare a reconciliation that explains any differences (e.g., export services are zero-rated, pure equity capital infusions are not revenue). Don't hope they won't notice — they will.

### Folder 3: Legal

This folder covers all legal agreements that define the company's obligations and rights.

**Documents:**

| Document | Notes |
|---|---|
| SHA (Shareholders' Agreement) from all previous rounds | Every round generates a new or amended SHA |
| Term sheets from all previous rounds | Signed and unsigned versions |
| SSA (Share Subscription Agreement) from all previous rounds | Documents the actual investment |
| Voting agreements | Any separate voting agreements beyond SHA |
| Co-founder agreements | Including any buyout or cliff terms |
| Founder IP assignment agreements | Critical: IP must belong to the company, not the founders personally |
| Employment agreements (all team members) | Focus on IP assignment, non-compete, non-solicitation clauses |
| Advisor/contractor agreements | Including IP ownership provisions |
| Customer contracts (top 10) | Redacted versions acceptable initially; full versions needed later |
| Vendor contracts (material ones) | Any contract > ₹25L/year or critical to operations |
| Office lease / rent agreement | Or co-working agreement |
| Software licenses (material) | AWS, Salesforce, etc. — check auto-renewal and assignment provisions |
| Any ongoing litigation or disputes | Full disclosure, with legal opinions |
| Any regulatory notices | Tax notices, GST notices, any government communications |
| FEMA filings (if foreign investment received) | FC-GPR, FC-TRS filings |

**Why board meeting minutes matter:** VCs read board meeting minutes carefully. They want to understand: How does the founder communicate with the board? Are problems disclosed candidly? Are there any decisions that look questionable in retrospect? Are financial projections revised rationally? Founders who present honest, complete board minutes — including discussions of problems and how they were addressed — build significant trust.

**The IP assignment red flag:** This is one of the most common issues discovered in Indian startup due diligence. The founder(s) built the initial product before the company was incorporated, or built it while employed elsewhere. If the IP assignment agreement doesn't clearly vest all IP in the company, investors will require it to be remediated before they close — and depending on the circumstances, it can be difficult to fix cleanly.

Action item: Before starting the fundraising process, ensure every founder, early employee, and contractor has signed an IP assignment agreement that assigns all relevant IP to the company.

### Folder 4: Cap Table

The cap table is the document investors scrutinize most carefully for structural problems. A clean, complete cap table dramatically accelerates diligence. A messy one can kill a deal.

**Documents:**

| Document | What It Should Contain |
|---|---|
| Current cap table (fully diluted) | All shareholders, share classes, number of shares, % ownership, investment amount |
| Cap table with rights and preferences | Anti-dilution provisions, liquidation preferences, ROFR, drag-along, pro-rata rights |
| Historical cap table (pre-money at each round) | How the cap table has evolved |
| ESOP pool documentation | ESOP plan document, all grants made, vesting status, exercise prices |
| Convertible securities | Outstanding CCDs, convertible notes, iSAFEs — with conversion terms |
| Warrants | Any outstanding warrants |
| Capitalization certificate from CS | Company Secretary's confirmation of the authorized and issued capital |
| Share certificates | Or demat account statements showing share holdings |

**The waterfall analysis (Series A and later):** At Series A, investors will want a waterfall analysis — a model showing exactly what each shareholder receives at various exit valuations (₹50 Cr, ₹100 Cr, ₹200 Cr, ₹500 Cr). This reveals whether the liquidation preference structure creates any problematic misalignments.

```
Sample waterfall structure (for illustration):

Exit: ₹200 Cr
Series A investor (20%, 1x non-participating pref of ₹15 Cr):
  → max(₹15 Cr pref, 20% × ₹200 Cr = ₹40 Cr) = ₹40 Cr (participating would be higher)

Seed investor (10%, 1x non-participating pref of ₹3 Cr):
  → max(₹3 Cr, 10% × ₹200 Cr = ₹20 Cr) = ₹20 Cr

Founders (60%):
  → 60% × ₹200 Cr = ₹120 Cr

ESOP (10%):
  → 10% × ₹200 Cr = ₹20 Cr

Total: ₹40 + ₹20 + ₹120 + ₹20 = ₹200 Cr ✓
```

**Red flags VCs look for in the cap table:**

1. **Unusual provisions:** If an early angel has a full ratchet anti-dilution, or a blocking right on future fundraises, or other atypical terms — this will slow down or kill the next round.

2. **Missing investors:** If there are shareholders mentioned in previous SHAs who don't appear in the current cap table, or vice versa, there is a documentation problem.

3. **ESOP pool discrepancy:** ESOP pool size and individual grants don't match. Unvested grants not clearly documented.

4. **FEMA non-compliance:** Foreign investors on the cap table without corresponding FEMA filings (FC-GPR) is a serious issue.

5. **Cap table that doesn't match MCA records:** The official share register maintained with the ROC must match the cap table you present. Investors check both.

### Folder 5: Product

This folder demonstrates the reality of what you've built and how it works.

**Documents:**

| Document | Description |
|---|---|
| Product demo video or demo access | Short (5–10 min) walkthrough of the key workflows |
| Product roadmap (last 12 months + next 12 months) | Shows what was shipped and what's planned |
| Architecture overview | Non-technical diagram of system components (cloud provider, databases, APIs) |
| Tech stack documentation | Languages, frameworks, key SaaS dependencies |
| Data security overview | GDPR/PDPA compliance, data encryption, access controls |
| Any patents or patent applications | If IP is a moat |
| Third-party integrations | Key integrations (GSTN, banking APIs, ERP systems) |
| Key technical risks | Honest assessment of technical debt, scalability constraints |

**What VCs look for in the product folder:** They are verifying that what you showed in the demo is real, that the tech is defensible (or at minimum not trivially replicable), and that there are no hidden technical liabilities (massive technical debt that will prevent scaling, dependency on a single API that could be revoked, etc.).

Series A VCs increasingly do technical due diligence — a technical partner or advisor reviews the codebase quality, architecture, and team capability. Having clean, well-documented code is an advantage.

### Folder 6: GTM (Go-To-Market)

This folder demonstrates that you understand how you acquire customers and that the process is repeatable.

**Documents:**

| Document | Description |
|---|---|
| Customer cohort analysis | Revenue retention by cohort (monthly, quarterly) |
| Customer acquisition channel breakdown | % of new customers from each channel, CAC by channel |
| Sales funnel metrics | Leads → demos → trials → close, with conversion rates and timelines |
| NPS or customer satisfaction data | Survey results, testimonials |
| Case studies (top 3 customers) | Detailed story of how the customer used the product and what value they got |
| Top customer reference list | 5–10 customers willing to take reference calls |
| Churn analysis | Why customers have churned, when they churn, what the recovery rate is |
| Competitive win/loss analysis | When you win, why; when you lose, why |
| Geographic expansion plan | If relevant |

**The cohort analysis: the most valued and most underutilized document**

A cohort analysis shows how much revenue from a group of customers acquired in a given period is still active in subsequent periods. This is the single most powerful document for demonstrating product-market fit and LTV credibility.

```
Example cohort analysis structure (monthly revenue cohorts):

Cohort (Month 0) | M0    | M1   | M2   | M3   | M6   | M12
-----------------|-------|------|------|------|------|------
Jan 2024 (₹5L)  | 100%  | 95%  | 92%  | 90%  | 88%  | 87%
Feb 2024 (₹7L)  | 100%  | 96%  | 93%  | 91%  | 89%  | —
Mar 2024 (₹9L)  | 100%  | 94%  | 92%  | 90%  | —    | —
Apr 2024 (₹8L)  | 100%  | 95%  | 93%  | —    | —    | —

What VCs look for:
- Are later cohorts performing better than earlier ones? (Product improvement signal)
- Is Month 12 retention > 80%? (Healthy B2B SaaS)
- Does revenue expand in cohorts (numbers > 100%)? (Upsell/expansion signal)
- Is there a specific month where churn spikes? (Signals contract renewal issues)
```

An investor who sees cohort analysis showing 87% revenue retention at Month 12 will trust the LTV projection much more than one based on theoretical assumptions.

### Folder 7: Team

This folder provides information about the people building the company.

**Documents:**

| Document | Description |
|---|---|
| Founder CVs / LinkedIn profiles | Full background, education, previous companies |
| Key employee CVs (top 10) | Focus on VP-level and above |
| Org chart | Current structure with reporting lines |
| Headcount plan (next 12–18 months) | Planned hires, roles, timing, cost |
| Compensation structure | Salary bands by level, ESOP grants |
| Employment contracts (founders + key employees) | Verify IP assignment, non-compete terms |
| Background check policy | Do you do background checks? Have you done them for existing team? |
| ESOP grants table | All grants, vesting schedules, strike prices |
| Any pending HR disputes | Honest disclosure |

**What VCs look for in team diligence:**

1. **Background checks:** Sophisticated VCs and their legal teams may run background checks on founders. Anything in your background that will come up — previous company failures, legal disputes, gaps in employment history — should be disclosed proactively. Discovering it in diligence is far worse than disclosing it upfront.

2. **Key person risk:** If one person (usually the founder/CTO) is critical to nearly all product decisions, the investor will want to understand the succession plan.

3. **Employment agreement gap:** Some early startup hires were done informally — no written employment agreement. This creates legal risk. Get formal agreements in place before diligence.

4. **Non-compete clauses from previous employers:** If a founder or key technical employee left a previous employer with a non-compete agreement, this needs to be evaluated by a lawyer. Non-compete enforceability in India is limited for employees, but it can still create complications.

### Folder 8: References

This folder is often overlooked by founders but is important to the process.

**Documents and resources:**

| Resource | Description |
|---|---|
| Customer reference list | 5–10 customers willing to take calls, with name, title, company, and contact |
| Employee reference list | 3–5 senior employees or former employees who can speak to founder quality |
| Investor reference list | Any existing investors who can speak to the founder's character and execution |
| Key advisor contacts | Active advisors who can verify the market or technical thesis |

**How reference calls work in Indian VC diligence:** Unlike US firms that do extensive reference checking, Indian VCs often rely on their networks more than formal references. They will call a founder in their portfolio who knows you, or call a mutual connection at a customer company.

This means your informal reputation — how you've treated previous employers, how you've handled past relationships, whether the startup community respects you — is often more important than your formal reference list.

The implication: throughout your company building, behave as if you're always being referenced. Your relationship with your team, your honesty with existing investors, and your reputation with industry contacts will determine what investors hear when they make informal calls about you.

## Red Flags VCs Find in Data Rooms

These are the issues that either kill deals or require significant remediation:

| Red Flag | What It Signals | Remediation Difficulty |
|---|---|---|
| GST revenue < Deck revenue (unexplained) | Revenue inflation or undisclosed off-book revenue | High — requires full reconciliation and explanation |
| Foreign investors without FEMA filings | FEMA non-compliance, potential liability | Medium — can be compounded/remediated but takes time and money |
| IP not assigned to company | Critical IP may not be owned by the company | Medium to High — depends on circumstances |
| Missing or inconsistent SHAs | Cap table may have undocumented rights | High — legal work to reconcile |
| Full ratchet anti-dilution in old SHA | Existing investors have excessive rights | High — need waiver or amendment from existing investor |
| Board minutes not maintained | Corporate governance not practiced | Low — can be remediated going forward, but signals pattern |
| No ESOP plan document | ESOPs granted without legal basis | Medium — can be retroactively documented with CS help |
| Litigation not disclosed in diligence | Founder candor question | High — trust is hard to rebuild |
| Co-founder departure without formal exit | Legal and equity complications | Medium — document it properly |
| Cap table doesn't match MCA records | Documentation error or intentional discrepancy | Medium — CS can reconcile |

## Seed Data Room vs Series A Data Room

The depth of the data room scales with the size of the round.

| Category | Seed Data Room | Series A Data Room |
|---|---|---|
| Financials | Management accounts, bank statements | Audited financials (3 years), detailed model |
| Legal | Core incorporation docs, SHA | Full legal diligence (all agreements, litigation search) |
| Cap table | Current table with key terms | Full waterfall model, legal review of all terms |
| Technical | Demo video | Code review (may be requested), architecture doc |
| Customers | Reference list, top 10 revenue breakdown | Cohort analysis, NPS data, churn analysis by segment |
| Compliance | Basic tax + GST | Full compliance review — FEMA, tax, GST, sector regulations |
| Team | CV + brief bios | Background check, reference calls, employment agreements |
| Time to complete | 2–3 weeks | 4–8 weeks |
| Typical documents | 30–50 documents | 100–200 documents |

For seed rounds, many Indian investors are lighter on formal diligence — relying more on founder conviction and early traction than on comprehensive documentation review. But even for seed, having the key documents ready signals operational maturity.

## The 2-Week Data Room Build Plan

**Week 1: Corporate and Legal**

```
Day 1 (Monday):
□ Download all MCA filings from MCA21 portal (Certificate of Incorporation, MoA, AoA,
  all annual returns)
□ Gather all previous term sheets and SHAs
□ Pull GSTIN certificate and recent GST returns (GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B, last 12 months)
□ Gather all employment contracts (founders + key employees)

Day 2 (Tuesday):
□ Create cap table in Excel — all shareholders, share classes, amount invested, dates
□ Verify cap table against MCA share capital records
□ Document all outstanding convertible instruments (CCDs, notes)
□ Pull ESOP plan document and all grant letters

Day 3 (Wednesday):
□ Identify any missing documents (FA from previous rounds? IP assignments?)
□ Contact CS (Company Secretary) to confirm MCA filings are current
□ Check DPIIT registration status; apply if not done (takes 2–4 weeks)
□ Pull income tax returns for company (last 2 years)

Day 4 (Thursday):
□ Review all material contracts (top 3 customer contracts, office lease, key vendor contracts)
□ Identify any litigation, disputes, or pending legal matters
□ Document FEMA filings if any foreign investment received
□ Gather bank statements (last 6 months for seed, 12 months for Series A)

Day 5 (Friday):
□ Review everything collected in Week 1
□ Create list of missing documents with names responsible for procuring each
□ Begin IP assignment audit — who built what, when, and are assignment agreements in place?
```

**Week 2: Financials, Product, and GTM**

```
Day 6 (Monday):
□ Compile monthly P&L for last 12–18 months (management accounts)
□ Reconcile bank statements to P&L
□ Build or clean up revenue breakdown by customer (top 10 customers, % of revenue)

Day 7 (Tuesday):
□ Build cohort analysis (revenue retention by monthly or quarterly cohort)
□ Calculate and document CAC by channel
□ Compile NPS data or customer satisfaction surveys

Day 8 (Wednesday):
□ Record product demo video (15 minutes, covers key workflows)
□ Write product roadmap document (last 12 months shipped + next 12 months planned)
□ Document architecture and tech stack

Day 9 (Thursday):
□ Build financial model (3-year projection with documented assumptions)
□ Verify financial model outputs are consistent with historical data
□ Prepare use-of-funds detail for the round being raised

Day 10 (Friday):
□ Organize all documents into the 8-folder structure in Google Drive or Docsend
□ Set permissions (view only, no download for sensitive docs; download allowed for others)
□ Create data room index (one-page document listing all contents and folder locations)
□ Send to co-founder and advisor for review before first investor meeting
```

## Data Room Tools and Access Management

**Google Drive:** Free, familiar, easy to share. Limitations: no detailed access analytics, limited control over downloading.

**Docsend:** Best for pitch deck sharing and basic data rooms. Shows who viewed what and for how long. Pricing: ~$50–100/month. Good for seed rounds.

**Digify / Box:** More control, detailed analytics, NDA gating. Better for Series A and later where you're sharing with many investors simultaneously.

**Folder naming convention:**

```
[Company Name] Data Room - [Date]
  ├── 01_Company
  ├── 02_Financials
  ├── 03_Legal
  ├── 04_CapTable
  ├── 05_Product
  ├── 06_GTM
  ├── 07_Team
  ├── 08_References
  └── 00_Index [Data Room Index document]
```

Date-stamping the data room name is important — when investors request an updated version 3 months later, you can easily distinguish the current version.

**Access control best practice:**
- Create a master data room folder with all documents
- When sharing with a specific investor, create a shared link with view-only access
- Do not give multiple investors access to the same link (you lose visibility into who saw what)
- For sensitive documents (board minutes, customer contracts), require NDA before sharing

## Key Takeaways

1. The data room is not a formality — it is a critical signal of your operational capability. A well-prepared data room accelerates diligence by weeks and builds investor confidence.

2. India-specific documents that most founders miss: GST returns (reconciled with revenue), FEMA filings (FC-GPR for foreign investment), ROC annual filings, DPIIT registration, and IP assignment agreements.

3. The cohort analysis is the single most valuable document you can include. It proves retention claims and builds LTV credibility more than any other analysis.

4. Red flags that kill deals: revenue in the deck that doesn't match GST filings, foreign investors without FEMA filings, IP not assigned to the company, and full ratchet anti-dilution in old SHAs.

5. Seed data rooms are lighter than Series A data rooms — but even at seed, having the core documents organized and ready signals the kind of operational discipline that investors want to back.

6. Build the data room before you start investor outreach, not after you get a yes. The preparation process itself reveals issues you need to resolve.
